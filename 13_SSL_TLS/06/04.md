Domain Name System (DNS) records act like a phonebook for the internet. They translate human-readable domain names (like
`google.com`) into computer-readable IP addresses (like `142.250.190.46`), or route specific types of traffic like email.

Here are the most common types of DNS records you will encounter when managing a website:

---

### 1. A Record (Address Record)

The most fundamental DNS record. It maps a domain name directly to an **IPv4 address**.

- **What it does:** Points your website name to the physical server hosting it.
- **Example:** `example.com` ➔ `192.0.2.1`

### 2. AAAA Record (IPv6 Address Record)

This works exactly like an A record, but it maps a domain name to an **IPv6 address** (the newer, much longer internet
protocol standard).

- **What it does:** Points your website name to an IPv6-enabled server.
- **Example:** `example.com` ➔ `2001:db8:85a3::8a2e:370:7334`

### 3. CNAME Record (Canonical Name)

Instead of pointing a domain to an IP address, a CNAME record points a domain to **another domain name**. It acts as an
alias.

- **What it does:** Useful for mapping subdomains to a main domain, or pointing to external services like Shopify or a CDN.
- **Example:** `www.example.com` ➔ `example.com` (If the IP of `example.com` changes, `www` updates automatically).

### 4. MX Record (Mail Exchanger)

MX records specify the **mail servers** responsible for receiving emails on behalf of your domain name.

- **What it does:** Tells the rest of the internet where to send emails addressed to `@yourdomain.com`.
- **Note:** MX records always point to a _domain name_ (like `mail.google.com`), never directly to an IP address. They also
  include a "Priority" number so backup servers can be used if the main one goes down.

### 5. TXT Record (Text Record)

TXT records allow a domain administrator to insert arbitrary text into the DNS settings. They don't direct traffic anywhere
on their own, but external services read them.

- **What it does:** Primarily used for **domain ownership verification** (e.g., proving to Google or cloud services that you
  own the domain) and **email security** (like SPF, DKIM, and DMARC records, which prevent spammers from faking emails from
  your domain).

### 6. NS Record (Name Server)

NS records state which DNS servers are the **authoritative managers** for your domain.

- **What it does:** Tells the internet, "If you want to know the IP address for this website, go ask these specific servers."
  (For example, when you switch to Cloudflare, you update your NS records to point to Cloudflare's servers).
