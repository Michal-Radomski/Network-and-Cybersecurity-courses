In asymmetric cryptography (like RSA or Elliptic Curve Cryptography used in SSL certificates, SSH, and Bitcoin), a public key
is mathematically derived from a private key.

However, because of the math involved, it is a **one-way street**: it is easy to calculate the public key from the private
key, but mathematically impossible to reverse the process to find the private key if you only have the public key.

The exact calculation depends on the algorithm being used. Here is how the two most popular methods work:

---

### 1. Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC)

ECC is the modern standard for keys (like ECDSA or Ed25519) because it uses smaller key sizes for the same amount of
security. The calculation relies on **Elliptic Curve Multiplication**, which is a trapdoor function (easy to do forward,
impossibly hard to do backward).

1. **The Private Key:** Your private key is simply a massive, completely random integer (a secret number).
2. **The Base Point ($G$):** The algorithm has a publicly known, predefined starting point on a specific mathematical curve,
   called the Generator Point or Base Point ($G$).
3. **The Calculation:** To get the public key ($Q$), the private key ($k$) is multiplied by the base point:

$$Q = k \times G$$

In elliptic curve math, "multiplication" means jumping along the curve in a specific geometric pattern a certain number of
times. 4. **The Result:** The public key ($Q$) is a specific coordinate $(x, y)$ on the elliptic curve.

Because of the "Elliptic Curve Discrete Logarithm Problem," an attacker looking at the final coordinate $(x, y)$ cannot
figure out how many times ($k$) you jumped to get there.

---

### 2. RSA (Rivest–Shamir–Adleman)

RSA is the older, traditional method (like the 2048-bit keys we discussed earlier). Its calculation relies on the difficulty
of **factoring large prime numbers**.

1. **The Setup:** First, two massive, secret random prime numbers are chosen, let's call them $p$ and $q$.
2. **The Modulus ($n$):** These two primes are multiplied together:

$$n = p \times q$$

This result, $n$, is called the **modulus**. It is made completely public and forms part of both the public and private
keys. 3. **The Public Exponent ($e$):** A number $e$ (usually `65537`) is chosen as the public exponent. 4. **The Public
Key:** The combination of $(e, n)$ becomes your Public Key. 5. **The Private Key:** The private key exponent ($d$) is then
calculated using a specific mathematical formula (modular inverse) involving $p$ and $q$.

In RSA, the private key isn't technically "calculated first"—instead, the core math pieces ($p$ and $q$) are generated, and
the public key components are shared, while the private exponent ($d$) is kept strictly secret. An attacker cannot calculate
the private key from the public key unless they can factor the massive modulus ($n$) back into its original primes $p$ and
$q$, which takes too much computing power to be feasible.

---

### Summary

- In **ECC**, your private key is a secret number of "steps," and your public key is the final destination on a graph.
- In **RSA**, your public key is the product of two giant secret prime numbers multiplied together.
