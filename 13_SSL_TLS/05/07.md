A **Certificate Signing Request (CSR)** is a block of encoded text that you generate on your server when applying for an
SSL/TLS certificate. It acts as the formal application you send to a Certificate Authority (like Let's Encrypt, DigiCert, or
GlobalSign) to prove who you are and what public key should be tied to your domain.

Here is a breakdown of how a CSR works and what it contains.

---

## What is Inside a CSR?

A CSR contains the information that will be embedded into your final SSL certificate, along with the cryptographic proof
required to validate it. It typically includes:

- **The Public Key:** This is the most crucial part. When you create a CSR, your server generates a key pair: a **Private
  Key** (which stays securely on your server and must never be shared) and a **Public Key** (which is embedded into the CSR
  and given to the world).
- **Common Name (CN):** The fully qualified domain name (FQDN) you want to secure (e.g., `www.example.com` or `*.example.com`
  for a wildcard cert).
- **Organization Details:** Information about the entity requesting the certificate (typically used for Organization
  Validation or Extended Validation certs):
- _Organization (O):_ Legal name of your company.
- _Organizational Unit (OU):_ The department (like "IT" or "Web Security").

- **Geographic Location:** \* _Country (C):_ The two-letter ISO code (e.g., "US", "CA", "GB").
- _State/Province (ST)_
- _Locality/City (L)_

- **Digital Signature:** The CSR is signed with your corresponding private key. This proves to the Certificate Authority that
  you hold the private key associated with the public key inside the request.

---

## How the CSR Process Works

The creation and submission of a CSR follow a specific lifecycle:

```
[ Your Server ]
  ├── 1. Generates Private Key & Public Key
  ├── 2. Packages Public Key + Domain Info into a CSR
  └── 3. Sends CSR ───────────────────────────────► [ Certificate Authority ]
                                                           │
                                                    4. Validates domain
                                                    5. Signs & issues Certificate
                                                           │
[ Your Server ] ◄──────────────────────────────────────────┘
  └── 6. Installs Certificate (Pairs it with Private Key)

```

1. **Generation:** You use a tool on your server (like OpenSSL, IIS, or an automated client like Certbot) to generate the
   private key and the CSR.
2. **Submission:** You copy the text of the CSR (which looks like a scrambled block of text starting with
   `-----BEGIN CERTIFICATE REQUEST-----`) and paste it into the CA’s submission form.
3. **Verification:** The CA verifies that you own the domain listed in the CSR.
4. **Issuance:** If everything checks out, the CA uses their own trusted private key to sign a new SSL certificate containing
   your public key and domain info, then sends it back to you.
5. **Installation:** You install the issued certificate on your server, where it pairs up with the original private key to
   enable HTTPS.

---

## Why separating the Private Key and CSR matters

The entire security of HTTPS relies on the **Private Key** remaining completely secret. Because the CSR only contains your
_Public Key_ and identifying information, it is perfectly safe to send over the internet to a third party. A Certificate
Authority never sees, handles, or stores your private key.
