When you combine **Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC)** with the **Diffie-Hellman (DH)** key exchange, you get the backbone of
modern internet security.

Here is how these pieces fit together to create a secure connection, culminating in the gold standard of TLS 1.3: **ECDHE
paired with ECDSA**.

---

## 1. Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman (ECDH)

Standard Diffie-Hellman uses massive prime numbers to mix secrets. **ECDH** does the exact same thing, but it swaps out the
prime numbers for an elliptic curve.

Instead of multiplying numbers, the two parties (Client and Server) agree on a public elliptic curve and a starting point
($G$) on that curve.

### How the ECDH Exchange Works:

1. **The Client** chooses a secret random number, $d_C$ (its private key), bounces a line around the curve that many times,
   and gets a final point, $Q_C$ (its public key). It sends $Q_C$ to the server.
2. **The Server** does the same thing. It chooses its own secret random number, $d_S$ (private key), bounces a line around
   the curve, and gets its public key, $Q_S$. It sends $Q_S$ to the client.
3. **The Magic:** \* The client multiplies the server's public key by its own private key: $d_C \times Q_S$

- The server multiplies the client's public key by its own private key: $d_S \times Q_C$

Due to the laws of elliptic curve math, both calculations land on the **exact same coordinate** on the curve. This shared
coordinate becomes their shared session key.

### The "E" stands for Ephemeral (ECDHE)

In the real world, we use **ECDHE**. The "E" means _Ephemeral_ (temporary). Instead of using the same curve points forever,
the client and server generate brand-new, throwaway curve points for **every single connection**. Once you close your browser
tab, those keys are deleted forever, ensuring Perfect Forward Secrecy.

---

## 2. The Missing Link: Authentication

ECDHE is brilliant, but it has one fatal flaw: **It doesn't prove identity.** If you try to connect to your bank, a hacker
sitting on your Wi-Fi network could intercept your request and perform an ECDHE exchange with you, pretending to be the bank.
You would successfully establish a secure, encrypted connection... straight to a hacker.

To fix this, ECDHE needs a way to verify _who_ is on the other side. Enter **ECDSA**.

---

## 3. ECDHE with ECDSA: The Dynamic Duo

**ECDSA** stands for _Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm_. It is used to generate a digital signature that proves
authenticity.

When you combine **ECDHE** (for secrecy) with **ECDSA** (for trust), you get the standard mechanism used to secure modern TLS
1.3 connections.

### How They Work Together Step-by-Step:

1. **The Key Exchange (ECDHE):** The server generates its ephemeral elliptic curve public key share.
2. **The Identity Proof (ECDSA):** Before sending that key share to you, the server signs it using its private master
   identity key (which matches its website Certificate). This signature is the **ECDSA signature**.
3. **The Transmission:** The server sends you its ECDHE public key share _plus_ the ECDSA digital signature.
4. **The Verification:** Your browser looks at the server's Certificate, uses the server's public identity key to verify the
   ECDSA signature, and says: _"Yes, this signature is valid. This key share definitely came from the real website, not an
   attacker."_
5. **The Secret Encryption:** Now that you trust the server, your browser completes the ECDHE math. Both sides generate the
   shared secret, and your secure browsing session begins.

---

## Summary of Roles

Think of a secure website connection as a high-security delivery:

- **ECDSA** is the ID badge and wax seal on the envelope. It proves exactly who sent the message and that it hasn't been
  tampered with.
- **ECDHE** is the secret code invented on the fly to scramble the letter inside the envelope so that only you and the
  recipient can read it.
