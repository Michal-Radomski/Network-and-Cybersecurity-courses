## What is a Root CA?

A **Root Certificate Authority (Root CA)** is a trusted organization that validates the identities of websites, companies,
and individuals on the internet. Think of a Root CA as the ultimate digital notary or passport office of the web.

To make this trust system work, Root CAs issue **digital certificates** (like SSL/TLS certificates) that prove a website
really belongs to who it claims to belong to.

---

## What are Root Certificates in the OS?

A **Root Certificate** is a special digital certificate that belongs to the Root CA itself. It contains the CA's public key
and is self-signed, meaning the CA vouches for its own identity.

For your computer to trust a website, it must first trust the CA that issued the website's certificate. Because of this,
every major operating system (Windows, macOS, Linux, iOS, Android) comes pre-installed with a built-in list of trusted root
certificates. This list is often called the **OS Trust Store**.

### How the Trust Chain Works

When you visit a secure website (like `https://example.com`):

1. **The Handshake:** The website sends its digital certificate to your browser.
2. **The Verification:** Your operating system or browser checks who signed that certificate.
3. **The Chain:** If it was signed by an "Intermediate CA," the system checks who signed _that_ intermediate certificate,
   tracing the path all the way back up.
4. **The Ultimate Check:** If the chain leads back to a Root Certificate that is pre-installed in your OS Trust Store, your
   browser shows the secure padlock icon. If it doesn't, you get a "Connection is not private" warning.

## Managing Root Certificates in Your OS

Operating system updates regularly add new trusted CAs and remove old or compromised ones. However, you can view and manage
these certificates manually:

### Windows (Certificate Manager)

1. Press `Windows Key + R`, type `certmgr.msc`, and press **Enter**.
2. Expand the folder named **Trusted Root Certification Authorities** and click **Certificates**.
3. Here, you will see a list of hundreds of trusted authorities (like DigiCert, Let's Encrypt, or GlobalSign).

### macOS (Keychain Access)

1. Open **Keychain Access** via Spotlight (`Cmd + Space`).
2. Select **System Roots** in the sidebar.
3. You will see the master list of certificates trusted by Apple.

### Linux

In Linux, root certificates are typically managed as a bundle of text files stored in system directories such as
`/etc/ssl/certs/` or `/etc/pki/tls/certs/`.

---

## Why are they a Security Target?

Because the operating system blindly trusts anything signed by a certificate in its root store, attackers heavily target this
mechanism:

- **Malware:** Some advanced malware attempts to inject a malicious root certificate into your OS trust store. If successful,
  the hackers can decrypt your traffic, read your passwords, or serve fake websites without your browser triggering any
  warnings.
- **Corporate Inspection:** Many corporate networks install a custom root certificate on company-owned laptops. This allows
  the company's firewall to intercept, decrypt, and inspect employee internet traffic for security threats.
