The **Chain of Trust** is the backbone of internet security. It is a hierarchical system that allows your web browser to
trust a website it has never seen before, using a chain of cryptographic signatures that leads back to a universally trusted
source.

Here is exactly how that chain is built, piece by piece.

---

## The Three Main Components

To understand the chain, you need to know the three types of certificates involved:

1. **The Root Certificate:** The ultimate source of trust. These certificates belong to major Root Certificate Authorities
   (CAs) like DigiCert or Let's Encrypt. They are self-signed and come pre-installed in your Operating System's trust store.
2. **The Intermediate Certificate:** The "middlemen." Root CAs rarely sign website certificates directly because if a Root
   key is compromised, the entire internet trust system breaks. Instead, they sign Intermediate certificates, which are used
   to sign everyday website certificates.
3. **The End-Entity (Leaf) Certificate:** This is the actual SSL/TLS certificate installed on the website you are visiting
   (e.g., `google.com`).

---

## Step-by-Step: How the Chain is Built and Verified

When you open a browser and connect to a secure website, a process called a **TLS Handshake** happens in milliseconds. Here
is how your computer verifies the Chain of Trust:

### 1. The Website Presents its Certificates

The website's server sends your browser its own certificate (the **Leaf**), along with any **Intermediate** certificates
required to link it back to a root.

### 2. The Browser Verifies the Leaf Certificate

Your browser looks at the website's certificate and checks the digital signature. It asks: _“Who signed this?”_ _ The
certificate says: _"I was signed by Intermediate CA 'X'."\*

- The browser uses the public key of Intermediate CA 'X' to mathematically verify that the signature is valid.

### 3. The Browser Verifies the Intermediate Certificate

Now the browser shifts its focus to Intermediate CA 'X'. It asks: _“But who signed you?”_

- The intermediate certificate says: _"I was signed by Root CA 'Y'."_
- The browser uses the public key of Root CA 'Y' to mathematically verify that the intermediate signature is valid.

### 4. Reaching the Anchor of Trust

Finally, the browser looks at Root CA 'Y'. It checks its own internal, pre-installed **OS/Browser Trust Store**.

- If Root CA 'Y' is found in that local database, the chain is complete and verified.
- The browser trusts the Root, which means it trusts the Intermediate, which means it trusts the Website. You get the secure
  padlock icon.

---

## Why This Structure Matters

- **Scalability:** Root CAs don't have to handle millions of individual website requests. They can delegate that authority to
  various intermediates.
- **Security Isolation (Sandboxing):** If an intermediate certificate's private key is somehow stolen or compromised, the
  Root CA can simply revoke that single intermediate certificate. The main Root certificate remains safe, and the rest of the
  internet continues working normally.
