**Symmetric Key Encryption** is a method of securing data where the **exact same cryptographic key** is used to both encrypt
(lock) and decrypt (unlock) the information.

Think of it like a traditional physical lock and key: you use a key to lock a wooden chest, send the chest to a friend, and
they must use an exact copy of that same key to open it. If anyone intercepts the chest along the way, they can't get inside
unless they have the key.

---

## How It Works

The lifecycle of symmetric encryption follows a straightforward path:

1. **Plaintext:** You start with the original, readable message (e.g., "Hello World").
2. **The Key & Algorithm:** You pass the plaintext through a mathematical formula (the **algorithm**) along with a secret
   string of data (the **key**).
3. **Ciphertext:** The algorithm scrambles the plaintext into unreadable data.
4. **Decryption:** The recipient takes the ciphertext, applies the _exact same key_ and algorithm in reverse, and recovers
   the original plaintext.

---

## Pros and Cons of Symmetric Encryption

### The Good: Speed and Efficiency

- **Fast:** It requires relatively simple mathematical calculations, making it incredibly fast.
- **Great for Bulk Data:** Because it is so fast, it is ideal for encrypting large amounts of data quickly, such as entire
  hard drives, database backups, or high-definition video streams.

### The Challenge: Key Distribution

- **The Key Exchange Problem:** The biggest weakness of symmetric encryption is that both parties _must_ already possess the
  secret key before they can communicate securely. If you need to send the key over the internet to a friend, and an attacker
  intercepts the key, your entire security system is broken.

> **Note:** In modern systems (like HTTPS), this problem is solved using **Asymmetric Encryption** (which uses a public and
> private key pair) just to securely share a symmetric key. Once the symmetric key is safely shared, the system switches to
> symmetric encryption for the rest of the session because it is much faster.

---

## Common Symmetric Key Algorithms

There are several mathematical algorithms used to perform symmetric encryption. They generally fall into two categories:
**Block Ciphers** (which encrypt data in fixed-size chunks, like 128 bits at a time) and **Stream Ciphers** (which encrypt
data continuously, one bit or byte at a time).

Here are the most widely used algorithms:

### 1. AES (Advanced Encryption Standard)

- **Status:** The gold standard of encryption today.
- **How it works:** It is a block cipher that supports key lengths of 128, 192, and 256 bits.
- **Usage:** It is used by governments, banks, and apps worldwide to secure everything from classified data to your WhatsApp
  messages. **AES-256** is considered mathematically un-breakable by modern computing standards.

### 2. DES and 3DES (Data Encryption Standard)

- **Status:** Legacy / Outdated.
- **How it works:** DES was created in the 1970s and uses a short 56-bit key. Because computers became fast enough to guess a
  56-bit key through brute force, it became insecure. **3DES (Triple DES)** was created to patch this by running the DES
  algorithm three times sequentially, but it is slow and has largely been replaced by AES.

### 3. ChaCha20

- **Status:** Modern and highly efficient.
- **How it works:** It is a stream cipher designed to be incredibly fast, especially on devices that do not have dedicated
  hardware acceleration for AES (like older smartphones or smart-home IoT devices).
- **Usage:** Widely used in modern internet protocols like TLS (HTTPS) and WireGuard VPNs.
