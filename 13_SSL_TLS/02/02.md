**Asymmetric Key Encryption** (also known as **Public Key Cryptography**) is a method of securing data that uses a **pair of
mathematically linked keys** instead of just one.

Unlike symmetric encryption—where the same key locks and unlocks the data—asymmetric encryption uses one key to encrypt the
data and a completely different key to decrypt it.

Think of it like a mail drop box on a street corner:

- **The Public Key (The Mail Slot):** Anyone can walk up to the box and drop a letter through the slot. The slot is open to
  the public.
- **The Private Key (The Collector's Key):** Only the postal worker has the specific physical key to open the back of the box
  and read the letters. Knowing where the mail slot is doesn't help you get the letters back out.

---

## How It Works

The two keys are mathematically tied together, but you cannot realistically guess or calculate the private key just by
knowing the public key.

1. **Key Generation:** Alice generates a key pair: a **Public Key** and a **Private Key**.
2. **Distribution:** Alice shares her Public Key with the entire world (or sends it directly to Bob). She keeps her Private
   Key completely secret and secure.
3. **Encryption:** Bob wants to send Alice a secret message. He takes Alice’s **Public Key** and uses an algorithm to encrypt
   the message. It is now scrambled.
4. **Decryption:** Bob sends the scrambled message over the internet. Even if a hacker intercepts it, they cannot read it.
   Once Alice receives it, she uses her **Private Key** to decrypt it.

---

## Digital Signatures: The Reverse Process

Asymmetric encryption can also be used in reverse to prove **identity** (authentication).

If Alice encrypts a message using her _Private Key_, anyone can decrypt it using her _Public Key_. Because only Alice has the
private key, successfully decrypting the message proves that it definitively came from Alice and hasn't been altered. This is
how **Digital Signatures** work.

---

## Pros and Cons of Asymmetric Encryption

### The Good: Solves Key Distribution

- You never have to worry about securely sharing a secret key over the internet. You can hand out your public key to anyone,
  safely knowing they can only use it to lock data for you, not read your data.

### The Challenge: Speed and Complexity

- Asymmetric encryption relies on incredibly intense, complex mathematics involving massive prime numbers. Because of this,
  it is **significantly slower** (often 100 to 1,000 times slower) than symmetric encryption.

> **How modern tech uses both:** Because asymmetric is slow but secure, and symmetric is fast but hard to share, systems like
> HTTPS use **asymmetric encryption just to establish a connection and safely share a symmetric key**. Once that temporary
> symmetric key is shared, they switch over to symmetric encryption for the rest of the conversation.

---

## Common Asymmetric Key Algorithms

The algorithms behind asymmetric encryption rely on mathematical problems that are easy to calculate in one direction, but
incredibly difficult to reverse unless you have a special piece of information (the private key).

### 1. RSA (Rivest-Shamir-Adleman)

- **Status:** The pioneer and most widely used legacy algorithm.
- **How it works:** Its security is based on the extreme difficulty of factoring giant numbers that are the product of two
  massive prime numbers.
- **Usage:** Commonly used for securing web traffic, emails, and code signing, though it requires very long keys (like 2048
  or 4096 bits) to remain secure today.

### 2. ECC (Elliptic Curve Cryptography)

- **Status:** Modern, highly efficient standard.
- **How it works:** Instead of relying on prime numbers, ECC uses the algebraic structure of elliptic curves over finite
  fields.
- **Why it's better:** It provides the exact same level of security as RSA but with much smaller key sizes. For example, a
  **256-bit ECC key** offers the same security as a **3072-bit RSA key**. Smaller keys mean faster calculations, less battery
  drain on smartphones, and lower bandwidth usage.

### 3. Diffie-Hellman (DH)

- **Status:** Specifically designed for key exchange.
- **How it works:** It isn't typically used to encrypt actual data files; rather, it allows two parties who know nothing
  about each other to safely create a shared, secret symmetric key over an insecure public channel.
- **Usage:** It is a foundational component of the TLS handshakes that secure modern HTTPS browsing.
