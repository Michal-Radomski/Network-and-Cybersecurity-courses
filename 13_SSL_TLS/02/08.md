A digital signature on a TLS certificate doesn't look like a handwritten signature or a stamped seal. Instead, it looks like
a long block of random characters—a string of hexadecimal numbers.

Behind the scenes, it is a cryptographic proof generated using the Certificate Authority's (CA) **private key** and a hash of
the certificate's details.

---

## What it Looks Like in Your Browser

If you inspect a website's TLS certificate through your browser (by clicking the padlock icon $\rightarrow$ Connection is
secure $\rightarrow$ Certificate is valid), you can scroll down to find fields labeled **Signature Algorithm** and
**Certificate Signature Value**.

It looks like this:

```text
Signature Algorithm: sha256WithRSAEncryption

Certificate Signature Value:
    a4:7f:8b:22:c1:9d:3e:44:f9:a1:0b:bc:cd:ee:ff:11:
    22:33:44:55:66:77:88:99:aa:bb:cc:dd:ee:ff:00:11:
    a3:b4:c5:d6:e7:f8:09:1a:2b:3c:4d:5e:6f:7a:8b:9c:
    [... truncated for length, usually 256 to 512 bytes long ...]
    d4:e5:f6:a1:b2:c3:d4:e5:f6:07:18:29:3a:4b:5c:6d

```

---

## How it Logically Works (The Concept)

To understand exactly what that block of characters represents, look at how the Certificate Authority creates it and how your
browser reads it:

### 1. How the CA creates it (Signing)

When a trusted CA (like DigiCert) approves a certificate for Google, they don't just send the file. They lock it with a
digital signature:

- The CA takes all the text inside the certificate (domain name, owner, public key, expiration date).
- They run this data through a hashing algorithm (like **SHA-256**) to get a unique digital fingerprint of the certificate.
- The CA encrypts that fingerprint using their own secret **Private Key**.

This encrypted fingerprint is the **Certificate Signature Value** you see above.

### 2. How your browser checks it (Verification)

When you visit Google, your browser receives the certificate and wants to ensure no hacker has altered the domain name or
hijacked the public key.

- Your browser looks at the signature and decrypts it using the CA's globally public **Public Key** (which is safely
  pre-installed in your browser/operating system). This reveals the original hash the CA calculated.
- Your browser independently hashes the certificate details itself.
- Your browser compares the two hashes. If they match perfectly, it guarantees that **the certificate has not been altered by
  even a single letter** since the CA signed it.
