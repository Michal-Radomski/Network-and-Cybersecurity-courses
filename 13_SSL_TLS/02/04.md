## SHA Hashing Algorithms

**SHA (Secure Hash Algorithm)** is a family of cryptographic hashing functions created by the National Institute of Standards
and Technology (NIST) and the NSA.

As discussed with MD5, the job of a SHA algorithm is to take any input and turn it into a unique, fixed-size digital
fingerprint. However, SHA was built to succeed MD5 and fix its critical security flaws.

Over the years, the SHA family has evolved to keep up with faster computers and more sophisticated hacking methods:

### The SHA Family Tree

- **SHA-0 & SHA-1:** Released in the 1990s, SHA-1 produces a 160-bit hash. Much like MD5, **SHA-1 is now broken**.
  Cryptanalysts figured out how to force "collisions" (making two different files have the exact same hash), so it is no
  longer used for security.
- **SHA-2:** This is the current industry workhorse. It includes **SHA-256** (which creates a 256-bit string) and **SHA-512**
  (512-bit). It is incredibly secure and powers everything from SSL/TLS certificates (HTTPS) to Bitcoin.
- **SHA-3:** Released in 2015, SHA-3 is the newest standard. It wasn't created because SHA-2 was broken, but rather as a
  backup plan. It uses a completely different internal mathematical structure (called a "sponge construction") just in case
  someone ever finds a secret flaw in SHA-2.

An example of a SHA-256 hash:

> Input: `The quick brown fox jumps over the lazy dog` SHA-256 Hash:
> `d7a8fbb307d7809469ca9abcb0082e4f8d5651e46d3cdb762d02d0bf37c9e592`

## HMAC (Keyed-Hash Message Authentication Code)

While SHA-256 can prove that a file hasn't been accidentally corrupted, it has a major flaw when used over the internet: **it
cannot prove _who_ sent the message.** If Alice sends Bob a file along with its SHA-256 hash, an attacker (Eve) could
intercept the file, alter it, calculate a _new_ SHA-256 hash for her malicious file, and forward it to Bob. Bob would check
the hash, see that it matches the file, and assume everything is fine.

To solve this, we use **HMAC**.

### How HMAC Works

An HMAC is a specific type of message authentication code that **combines a cryptographic hash function (like SHA-256) with a
secret cryptographic key.**

Instead of just hashing the message, the process looks like this:

1. **The Secret Key:** Alice and Bob secretly agree on a private key beforehand.
2. **The Mix:** Alice takes the message and mixes it together with the secret key.
3. **The Hash:** She runs this combined mixture through a hashing algorithm (creating, for example, an **HMAC-SHA256**).
4. **The Verification:** When Bob receives the message and the HMAC, he runs the message and his copy of the secret key
   through the same process. If his calculated HMAC matches the one Alice sent, he knows two things:

- The message was not altered in transit (**Integrity**).
- The message definitely came from Alice, because only she knows the secret key (**Authenticity**).

### A Real-World Analogy

Think of a standard **SHA-256 hash** as a wax seal on an envelope. It tells you if someone opened the envelope, but anyone
with access to wax could have replaced the whole envelope and stamped it with their own seal.

An **HMAC** is like a wax seal stamped with a **custom, highly unique ring** that only you and your recipient possess. It
proves the letter hasn't been opened _and_ guarantees exactly who sent it.
