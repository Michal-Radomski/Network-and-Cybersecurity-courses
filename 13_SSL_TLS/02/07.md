A **TLS Certificate** (often still called an **SSL Certificate**) is a digital file installed on a website's server that
serves two crucial purposes: **security** and **identity verification**.

Think of a TLS certificate as a combination of a **secured security lock** and a **digitally unforgeable ID card** issued by
a government authority. It tells your browser, _"This website is exactly who it claims to be, and our connection is fully
encrypted."_

---

## What Information is Inside a TLS Certificate?

A certificate contains important cryptographic and identifying data, including:

- **The Domain Name:** The exact website address the certificate is registered to (e.g., `www.google.com`).
- **The Public Key:** The public cryptographic key used by visitors to kick off the encrypted TLS handshake.
- **The Issuing Authority:** The name of the trusted company that verified and created the certificate.
- **The Digital Signature:** Proof from the issuing authority that the certificate hasn’t been altered.
- **Expiration Date:** TLS certificates are temporary (usually lasting about 1 year or 90 days) and must be constantly
  renewed.

---

## How Does a TLS Certificate Work?

When you type an HTTPS address into your browser, the certificate is used to establish trust:

1. **The Handshake:** Your browser connects to the website and asks to speak securely.
2. **The Presentation:** The website's server responds by sending over its TLS certificate.
3. **The Authenticity Check:** Your browser checks the certificate against a pre-installed list of trusted **Certificate
   Authorities (CAs)** built right into your operating system or browser. It checks:

- Is the certificate expired?
- Does the domain name match the website I'm actually visiting?
- Is the digital signature valid?

4. **The Safe Connection:** If everything checks out, your browser shows the secure padlock icon in the address bar. It then
   uses the **public key** inside that certificate to encrypt a temporary symmetric key, safely establishing an encrypted
   browsing session.

---

## Who Issues These Certificates?

You cannot make a valid TLS certificate yourself. If you do, browsers will display a giant, scary **"Warning: Potential
Security Risk Ahead"** message to visitors.

Instead, certificates must be issued by a **Certificate Authority (CA)**. CAs are globally trusted third-party organizations
(like _DigiCert_, _GlobalSign_, or the free, automated _Let's Encrypt_) that strictly verify that the person requesting the
certificate actually owns and controls the website domain.

---

## Types of TLS Certificates

Depending on how much vetting a website goes through, there are three main types of certificates:

1. **Domain Validation (DV):** The most common and easiest to get. The CA only checks that you control the domain name. It
   takes minutes to issue and is often free.
2. **Organization Validation (OV):** The CA steps up verification by checking that the company running the website is a
   legally registered, legitimate business.
3. **Extended Validation (EV):** The highest level of vetting. The CA thoroughly investigates the organization's legal,
   physical, and operational existence. Historically, this used to turn the browser address bar green with the company's
   legal name, though modern browsers now handle them similarly to OV certificates visually, keeping the rigorous background
   checks behind the scenes.
