**RSA** (named after its creators **R**ivest, **S**hamir, and **A**dleman) is one of the first and most widely used
**asymmetric (public-key) cryptography** algorithms in the world.

Invented in 1977, its primary job is to solve the "key distribution problem" by allowing two parties to securely share
information or generate an encryption key without ever having to meet or safely exchange a secret password beforehand.

---

## How RSA Works (The Math Behind It)

The security of RSA relies completely on a branch of mathematics called **modular arithmetic**, and specifically on a concept
known as **prime factorization**.

It relies on a simple premise: **It is incredibly easy to multiply two giant prime numbers together, but it is brutally
difficult to do the reverse (factor them back out).**

Here is the simplified breakdown of how the keys are made:

1. **Pick Two Primes:** The algorithm chooses two distinct, massive prime numbers, usually called $p$ and $q$.
2. **Multiply Them:** These numbers are multiplied together to get a product, $n$ ($n = p \times q$). This number $n$ is
   called the **modulus**.
3. **Generate the Keys:** Through a formula called Euler's totient function, the algorithm generates two mathematically
   linked exponents:

- **The Public Exponent ($e$):** Used for encryption.
- **The Private Exponent ($d$):** Used for decryption.

### The Public and Private Keys

- Your **Public Key** consists of the numbers $(e, n)$. Anyone can see this.
- Your **Private Key** consists of the numbers $(d, n)$. You must keep this secret.

If a hacker wants to steal your private key, they only have the public numbers $e$ and $n$. To find the secret number $d$,
they must figure out what the original prime numbers $p$ and $q$ were. For modern RSA keys, the number $n$ is typically 2048
or 4096 bits long (a number hundreds of digits long). For a standard computer, figuring out the prime factors of a number
that size would take **billions of years**.

---

## The RSA Process: An Example

Imagine Alice wants to send a secret message to Bob using RSA:

1. **Bob shares his Public Key:** Bob puts his public key $(e, n)$ out in the open.
2. **Alice Encrypts:** Alice takes her message (converted into a number, $M$) and raises it to the power of Bob's public
   exponent $e$, then takes the remainder when divided by $n$.

$$\text{Ciphertext } (C) = M^e \pmod n$$

3. **Alice Sends It:** She sends this scrambled ciphertext $C$ over the internet.
4. **Bob Decrypts:** Bob receives the ciphertext. He uses his secret private key $d$ to undo the math:

$$\text{Message } (M) = C^d \pmod n$$

Because of the unique properties of modular arithmetic, this instantly recovers Alice's original message.

## What is RSA Used For Today?

Because RSA involves calculating massive exponents on giant numbers, it is computationally heavy and relatively slow.
Therefore, we rarely use RSA to encrypt actual large data files (like a whole webpage or a video file).

Instead, RSA is used for:

- **Symmetric Key Exchange:** During a TLS handshake (HTTPS), a browser might use RSA to safely encrypt and send a temporary,
  fast _symmetric_ key to a web server. Once that key is shared, RSA steps aside.
- **Digital Signatures:** RSA is widely used to verify identity. If Bob encrypts a piece of data with his _private_ key,
  anyone can decrypt it using his _public_ key. If it decrypts successfully, it mathematically proves that Bob (the owner of
  the private key) signed it and that the file hasn't been altered.

---

## Is RSA Still Safe?

Yes, but it requires much larger keys than it used to.

As computers have gotten faster, older 1024-bit RSA keys have become vulnerable to being cracked. Today, **2048-bit keys**
are the bare minimum standard for security, and **4096-bit keys** are recommended for highly sensitive data.

Furthermore, many modern systems are shifting away from RSA toward **ECC (Elliptic Curve Cryptography)** because ECC can
provide the exact same level of security as RSA but with vastly smaller keys, making connections much faster and lighter on
device batteries.
