Let’s zoom in on what happens right after your browser finds Google's IP address. Before any actual web data (like the Google
logo or search bar) can be sent, your browser and Google's server need to agree on _how_ they will talk.

This happens in two distinct steps: first, they establish a reliable connection (**TCP**), and second, they secure that
connection (**TLS**).

---

## Step 2: The TCP Handshake (Establishing the Connection)

Think of the TCP handshake as a polite phone call introduction. Its main job is to ensure both sides are ready to send and
receive data reliably. This is known as a **Three-Way Handshake** because it takes three steps:

1. **SYN (Synchronize):** Your browser sends a packet to Google's server. This packet contains a random sequence number and
   essentially says, _"Hey, I want to connect with you. Here is my starting sequence number so we can track our messages."_
2. **SYN-ACK (Synchronize-Acknowledge):** Google's server receives the request and replies back. It acknowledges your request
   and sends its own starting number: _"I hear you! I'm ready to connect too. Here is my sequence number, and I acknowledge
   yours."_
3. **ACK (Acknowledge):** Your browser receives Google's response and sends one final packet back: _"Got it! We are now
   connected and ready to go."_

At this point, a stable, open highway exists between your computer and Google, but anyone sitting on the same network could
still read the traffic. That brings us to the next step.

---

## Step 3: The TLS Handshake (Securing the Connection)

Now that the highway is built, the TLS handshake puts a locked, armored car on it. This step uses asymmetric encryption (a
public key that anyone can see, and a private key that only the server knows) to securely share a secret key.

1. **Client Hello:** Your browser sends a message to Google's server saying, _"I want to speak securely. Here are the
   versions of TLS I support and the encryption algorithms (Cipher Suites) I know how to use."_
2. **Server Hello & Certificate:** Google responds by choosing the best encryption algorithm they both support. Crucially,
   Google also sends over its **SSL/TLS Certificate**. This certificate is verified by an official third party and proves
   that the server actually belongs to Google (preventing you from being tricked by a fake website).
3. **Key Exchange:** Your browser verifies the certificate. Then, using Google's public key (which is inside the
   certificate), your browser generates a random, unique **Session Key** and sends it back to the server. Because it's
   encrypted with Google's public key, _only_ Google's private key can decrypt it.
4. **Finished:** Google's server decrypts the session key. Now, both your browser and Google have the exact same secret key.
   They send a "Finished" message to each other, encrypted with this new key.

---

### The Result

From this exact millisecond forward, all data sent between your browser and Google is fully encrypted using that shared
**Session Key**. If a hacker intercepts the data, it will look like unreadable gibberish. Your browser is now ready to safely
send the actual request for Google's homepage.
