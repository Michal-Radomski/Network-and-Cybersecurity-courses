In cryptography, an **attack** isn't usually someone hacking into a mainframe with green text flying across the screen.
Instead, it’s a collection of clever mathematical, statistical, or structural methods used to find vulnerabilities in a
cryptographic system.

The ultimate goal of a cryptographic attack is to break the confidentiality, integrity, or authenticity of secured
data—usually by finding the secret key or reading the encrypted message (ciphertext) without authorization.

Here is a breakdown of the most common types of cryptographic attacks, categorized by how they work.

---

## 1. Classical Mathematical & Textbook Attacks

These attacks focus entirely on the data itself (the plaintext and the ciphertext) to figure out the underlying key.

- **Brute-Force Attack:** The simplest (but often most time-consuming) method. The attacker tries every possible combination
  for the key until the correct one is found. The strength of modern cryptography relies on keys being so long that a
  brute-force attack would take billions of years.
- **Known-Plaintext Attack (KPA):** The attacker has access to both a piece of the original message (plaintext) and its
  encrypted version (ciphertext). They use this match to reveal the secret key.
- **Chosen-Plaintext Attack (CPA):** The attacker can choose arbitrary plaintexts to be encrypted and gets to see the
  resulting ciphertexts. This helps them analyze how the algorithm behaves to deduce the key.
- **Ciphertext-Only Attack:** The most difficult type. The attacker only has access to a set of encrypted messages and must
  rely purely on statistical analysis (like frequency analysis) to crack the code.

---

## 2. Implementation & Physical Attacks

Sometimes the math behind a cipher is flawless, but the way it is built into a computer or microchip creates leaks.

- **Side-Channel Attack:** Instead of attacking the math, the attacker measures physical characteristics of the hardware
  while it performs cryptographic operations. Examples include measuring **how much power** the chip consumes, looking at
  **electromagnetic radiation**, or tracking **timing differences** (how long an operation takes).
- **Fault Injection Attack:** The attacker physically tampers with the hardware (e.g., micro-zapping it with a laser or
  dropping the voltage) to cause an error during a cryptographic calculation, which accidentally leaks bits of the secret
  key.

---

## 3. Protocol & Network-Based Attacks

These attacks target how cryptographic systems communicate with each other over a network.

- **Man-in-the-Middle (MitM) Attack:** An attacker secretly intercepts and alters the communication between two parties who
  believe they are talking directly to each other. For example, the attacker establishes independent connections with both
  victims, relaying messages while making them think a private connection exists.
- **Replay Attack:** An attacker intercepts a valid encrypted message sent over the network (like a login credential or a
  financial transaction approval) and later sends it again to the receiver to trick the system into performing unauthorized
  actions.

---

## Summary of Goals

To put it simply, modern cryptographic attacks usually try to bypass the heavy math by exploiting human error, bad software
design, or physical hardware flaws.
