The Domain Name System (DNS) is essentially the "phonebook of the internet," and it functions through a structured,
hierarchical tree. This hierarchy ensures that no single server has to store every single web address; instead, the workload
is distributed across millions of servers.

### The Hierarchical Structure

The DNS hierarchy is read from **right to left**, starting from an invisible "Root" at the top and branching down into
specific subdomains.

---

### 1. The Root Level ( . )

At the very top of the hierarchy is the **Root Zone**. While you don't type it in your browser, every fully qualified domain
name (FQDN) technically ends with a dot (e.g., `google.com.`).

- **Managed by:** ICANN (Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers).
- **Function:** The Root servers don't know the IP of "google.com," but they know exactly where to find the servers that
  manage the `.com` extension.

### 2. Top-Level Domains (TLD)

One step below the root are the TLDs. These categorize domains based on purpose or geography.

- **Generic TLDs (gTLD):** `.com`, `.org`, `.net`, `.edu`.
- **Country Code TLDs (ccTLD):** `.uk`, `.jp`, `.ca`.
- **Function:** TLD name servers maintain information for all domains sharing that extension.

### 3. Second-Level Domains (SLD)

This is the part of the domain name that individuals or companies register.

- **Example:** In `google.com`, "google" is the SLD.
- **Function:** This is the level where specific **Authoritative Name Servers** are assigned. These servers hold the actual
  mapping of the name to an IP address.

### 4. Subdomains and Hostnames

Users can further divide their SLDs into subdomains to organize different services.

- **Example:** `mail.google.com` or `blog.example.org`.
- **Function:** These are managed entirely by the owner of the Second-Level Domain.

---

### How the Hierarchy Resolves a Query

When you type a URL into your browser, the "Recursive Resolver" (usually provided by your ISP) climbs this hierarchy:

1. **Check Root:** "Where is the server for `.com`?"
2. **Check TLD:** "I'm at `.com`. Where is the server for `example.com`?"
3. **Check Authoritative Server:** "I'm at `example.com`. What is the IP address for `www`?"
4. **IP Returned:** The resolver gets the IP (e.g., `192.0.2.1`) and hands it back to your browser.

### Zones vs. Domains

It’s important to distinguish between a **Domain** and a **Zone**:

- **Domain:** The entire subtree (e.g., `example.com` and all its subdomains like `api.example.com` and `dev.example.com`).
- **Zone:** A specific portion of the DNS namespace managed by a specific administrator. A company might manage `example.com`
  as one zone, but delegate the management of `uk.example.com` to a different server, creating a separate zone.
