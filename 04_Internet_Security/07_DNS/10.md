It’s easy to think of these as "either/or," but in a standard Ubuntu setup, they actually work **together** as a team. One is
the "Local Manager" and the other is the "External Source."

Here is the breakdown of when each one is used.

---

## 1. The Local Manager: `127.0.0.53`

This address is used **constantly and by default** for every single DNS request your computer makes.

When you type a URL into your browser, the Operating System looks at `/etc/resolv.conf`, sees `127.0.0.53`, and sends the
request to the **systemd-resolved** service running on your machine.

**Why Ubuntu uses this first:**

- **Caching:** If you visit a site twice, `127.0.0.53` remembers the IP so it doesn't have to ask the internet again.
- **Network Stability:** Whether you switch from Wi-Fi to Ethernet or turn on a VPN, your apps always talk to `127.0.0.53`.
  Only the background settings of that service change.

---

## 2. The External Source: `8.8.8.8` (Google)

`8.8.8.8` is used when your local manager (`127.0.0.53`) **doesn't know the answer** and needs to ask the "outside world."

However, your computer only uses Google’s DNS if you (or your network admin) have specifically configured it as an **Upstream
DNS Server**.

### The Chain of Command:

1. **Your Browser** asks **127.0.0.53** (systemd-resolved).
2. **127.0.0.53** checks its internal cache.
3. **If not found:** `127.0.0.53` acts as a client and forwards the request to your **Upstream DNS**.

- If you haven't changed any settings, this is usually your **Router** (e.g., `192.168.1.1`).
- If you manually configured your DNS, this is where **8.8.8.8** comes in.

---

## Comparison: When are they "In Charge"?

| Scenario                                       | Who is "In Charge"?                                                                                                               |
| ---------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **You visit a site you visited 1 minute ago.** | **127.0.0.53** (Answer is served from local cache).                                                                               |
| **You visit a brand new website.**             | **127.0.0.53** asks **8.8.8.8** for the answer.                                                                                   |
| **You are offline.**                           | **127.0.0.53** still receives the request but fails to reach **8.8.8.8**.                                                         |
| **Manual Override.**                           | If you delete `systemd-resolved` and write `nameserver 8.8.8.8` directly into your config, you bypass the local manager entirely. |

---

## How to see which "External" DNS you are using

Since `/etc/resolv.conf` only shows the local `127.0.0.53` address, you need a different command to see where the signal goes
next. Run this in your terminal:

```bash
resolvectl status

```

Look for the line that says **"DNS Servers"** under your active interface (like `wlp3s0` or `eth0`). That is the "upstream"
server (like Google's `8.8.8.8` or your ISP's server) that your local manager is talking to.

### Summary

- **`127.0.0.53`** is the **Front Desk** inside your house.
- **`8.8.8.8`** is the **Information Center** across town.
- The Front Desk handles what it can; otherwise, it calls the Information Center for you.
