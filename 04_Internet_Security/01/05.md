Private IP addresses are the "internal" addresses used within a local network—like your home, office, or a cloud VPC. They
are not routable on the public internet, which means a server in London cannot "see" your laptop's private IP address
directly.

Think of it like an **intercom system** in an apartment building. You can use the intercom to call Apartment 202 (Private
IP), but someone standing on the street outside can't dial "202" to reach you; they have to dial the building's main address
(Public IP).

---

## 1. The Reserved Ranges

To prevent confusion, the Internet Assigned Numbers Authority (IANA) reserved three specific blocks of IP addresses that can
**never** be used on the public internet.

| Class       | Private Range                     | Total Addresses | Common Use Case                                      |
| :---------- | :-------------------------------- | :-------------- | :--------------------------------------------------- |
| **Class A** | `10.0.0.0` – `10.255.255.255`     | ~16.7 Million   | Large corporate networks and Cloud VPCs (AWS/Azure). |
| **Class B** | `172.16.0.0` – `172.31.255.255`   | ~1 Million      | Schools, hospitals, or mid-sized internal networks.  |
| **Class C** | `192.168.0.0` – `192.168.255.255` | 65,536          | Home Wi-Fi routers and small offices.                |

---

## 2. How Private IPs Talk to the Internet (NAT)

Since these addresses are private, how does your laptop (likely `192.168.1.x`) browse Google?

It uses a process called **NAT (Network Address Translation)**. Your router acts as a middleman:

1. Your laptop sends a request to the router.
2. The router swaps your **Private IP** for its one **Public IP**.
3. The router sends the request to the internet.
4. When the answer comes back, the router remembers it was for you and sends it back to your internal address.

---

## 3. Why Use Private IPs?

- **Security:** Since your private IP is hidden behind a router/firewall, hackers cannot initiate a direct connection to your
  machine from the outside. They can only hit your "front door" (the router).
- **IP Conservation:** We ran out of public IPv4 addresses years ago. Private IPs allow billions of devices to share a very
  small number of public addresses.
- **Flexibility:** You can have the same private IP address (`192.168.1.1`) as your neighbor, and it doesn't cause a conflict
  because your networks are physically and logically separated by your respective routers.

---

## 4. Importance for Developers

When you are building your **Node.js** and **React** app, you’ll encounter private IPs in two main ways:

- **Local Development:** When you run your app, it usually hosts on `localhost` or `127.0.0.1` (the "Loopback" address, a
  special type of private IP that means "this machine").
- **Database Security:** In production, you should put your Database (MongoDB/Postgres) on a **Private Subnet**. You give it
  a private IP so that only your Node.js backend can talk to it. You **never** want your database to have a Public IP
  address, as that would leave it exposed to the entire world.
