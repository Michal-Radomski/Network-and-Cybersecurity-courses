To calculate a custom subnet, we use a process called **Subnetting**. It involves "stealing" bits from the host portion of
the IP address and giving them to the network portion.

If you need exactly **50 IP addresses** for a cluster of servers, here is how you would figure out the correct Net Mask and
CIDR.

---

## 1. The Power of Two Rule

IP addresses are binary, so subnets always grow in powers of 2 ($2^n$). To find the right size, you find the smallest power
of 2 that is greater than your required number of hosts.

1.  **Requirement:** 50 hosts.
2.  **The Math:** \* $2^5 = 32$ (Too small)
    - **$2^6 = 64$ (Perfect fit)**
3.  **The "Minus 2" Rule:** In every subnet, you lose two addresses:
    - The **Network Address** (the very first one).
    - The **Broadcast Address** (the very last one).
    - _Result:_ $64 - 2 = 62$ usable IP addresses. This comfortably covers your 50 servers.

---

## 2. Calculating the CIDR (The Slash)

An IPv4 address has **32 bits** total. To find your CIDR notation, you subtract the number of bits you used for your hosts
from the total.

- Total bits: **32**
- Host bits used: **6** (from our $2^6$ calculation)
- **$32 - 6 = 26$**
- **Your CIDR is: `/26`**

---

## 3. Calculating the Net Mask

To turn `/26` back into a traditional Net Mask (like `255.x.x.x`), you write out twenty-six **1s** in binary and convert it
to decimal.

- **Binary:** `11111111.11111111.11111111.11000000`
- **The conversion:**
  - The first three octets are all 1s: `255.255.255`
  - The last octet is `11000000`. In decimal, $128 + 64 = 192$.
- **Your Net Mask is: `255.255.255.192`**

---

## 4. Practical Application: How it looks in your Config

If you were setting up a private network for your Node.js servers starting at `10.0.0.0`, your configuration would look like
this:

| Field                 | Value         |
| :-------------------- | :------------ |
| **Network Address**   | `10.0.0.0`    |
| **First Usable IP**   | `10.0.0.1`    |
| **Last Usable IP**    | `10.0.0.62`   |
| **Broadcast Address** | `10.0.0.63`   |
| **CIDR Block**        | `10.0.0.0/26` |

### Why do this?

By using a `/26` instead of a standard `/24` (256 IPs), you are practicing **Network Segmentation**.

If a hacker breaches one area of your network, they can only "see" the other 61 addresses in that specific subnet. It’s like
putting your servers in a small, locked room rather than a giant open warehouse.
