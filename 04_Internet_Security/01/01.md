Think of it this way: **Internet Security** is the protective bubble around the entire world of online communication, while
**Web Security** is the specific armor you put on when you visit or build a website.

---

## 1. Internet Security: The "Wide Lens"

Internet Security is a broad umbrella term. it covers everything involving the sending and receiving of data across the web,
including email, file transfers, and device connectivity (IoT).

- **Objective:** To protect the **infrastructure** and the **transport** of data across various protocols.
- **Key Tools:** Firewalls, Antivirus software, VPNs, and Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).
- **Focus Areas:**
  - **Email Security:** Blocking phishing and spam (SMTP protocol).
  - **Network Security:** Protecting routers and Wi-Fi from unauthorized access.
  - **Endpoint Security:** Ensuring your laptop or phone isn't infected with malware.

---

## 2. Web Security: The "Targeted Lens"

Web Security (or Web Application Security) is a subset of Internet Security. It focuses specifically on **browsers**,
**websites**, and **web applications** (like Facebook, Gmail, or your banking portal).

- **Objective:** To protect the **data and logic** inside a specific application from being manipulated.
- **Key Tools:** HTTPS (SSL/TLS), Web Application Firewalls (WAF), and secure coding practices.
- **Focus Areas:**
  - **The Browser:** Preventing scripts from stealing your cookies (XSS).
  - **The Server:** Making sure hackers can't trick the database (SQL Injection).
  - **The User Session:** Ensuring a hacker can't "hijack" your login (Session Hijacking).

---

## 3. Key Differences at a Glance

| Feature               | Internet Security                             | Web Security                                   |
| :-------------------- | :-------------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------- |
| **Scope**             | Broad (Email, IoT, Apps, Networks).           | Narrow (Websites and Browsers).                |
| **Primary Protocols** | TCP/IP, SMTP, FTP, DNS, HTTP.                 | Primarily **HTTP** and **HTTPS**.              |
| **Common Threat**     | Phishing, Ransomware, Botnets.                | SQL Injection, XSS, CSRF, Clickjacking.        |
| **Analogy**           | Securing the entire airport and flight paths. | Securing a specific store inside the terminal. |

---

## How They Work Together

In your case (the React and Node.js app you mentioned earlier), you are mostly operating in the world of **Web Security**.
You are ensuring that your HTTP requests are secure and your application code isn't vulnerable.

However, if you host that app on a server that doesn't have a firewall or uses an outdated operating system, you have an
**Internet Security** problem that could bypass all your hard work on the web application.
