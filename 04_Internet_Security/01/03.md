A **Net Mask** (or Subnet Mask) is a 32-bit number that acts like a "filter" for an IP address. Its sole purpose is to tell a
computer which part of an IP address refers to the **Network** (the neighborhood) and which part refers to the **Host** (the
specific house).

Without a Net Mask, a computer wouldn't know if `192.168.1.5` is trying to talk to a device in its own room or someone across
the world.

---

## 1. How It Works (The Binary "Secret")

Computers don't see IP addresses as dots and numbers; they see binary (1s and 0s). A Net Mask uses a string of continuous
**1s** to "mask" the network portion and **0s** to leave the host portion open.

- **1s** = "This bit is part of the Network ID. Do not change."
- **0s** = "This bit is part of the Host ID. This can be assigned to a device."

### The Logic Gate

When your computer wants to send data, it performs a mathematical **AND** operation between its IP and the Net Mask. If the
result matches the destination's network ID, it knows the device is local. If it doesn't match, it sends the data to the
**Default Gateway** (your router) to be sent out to the internet.

---

## 2. Common Net Masks

You have likely seen these numbers in your computer's "Network Settings":

| Net Mask            | CIDR Equivalent | Description                                                                |
| :------------------ | :-------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **255.0.0.0**       | `/8`            | Used for massive networks (Class A). Only the first number is the network. |
| **255.255.0.0**     | `/16`           | Used for large corporate networks (Class B).                               |
| **255.255.255.0**   | `/24`           | The "Home Standard." The first three numbers are the network.              |
| **255.255.255.252** | `/30`           | Used for point-to-point links (only 2 usable IPs).                         |

---

## 3. Why is it called a "Mask"?

Think of a piece of paper with a hole cut in it. When you lay that paper (the mask) over a long string of numbers (the IP),
the numbers you can see through the hole are the **Host ID**, and the numbers covered by the paper are the **Network ID**.

### Example:

- **IP Address:** `192.168.1.50`
- **Net Mask:** `255.255.255.0`
- **Result:** The computer "masks" `192.168.1`. Any device starting with those three numbers is on the same local network.
  The `.50` is the specific identity of that machine.

---

## 4. Net Mask vs. CIDR

In modern networking (and especially in cloud config for your Node.js apps), we rarely type out `255.255.255.0` anymore. We
use **CIDR notation** (like `/24`) because it's shorter.

The number after the slash simply counts how many **1s** are in the Net Mask:

- `255.255.255.0` has **twenty-four** 1s in binary, so it becomes **/24**.
- `255.255.0.0` has **sixteen** 1s in binary, so it becomes **/16**.

---

**Quick Tip for your Dev Work:** If you are setting up a database (like MongoDB or PostgreSQL) and you want to allow access
_only_ from your web server, you would use a mask of `255.255.255.255` (or `/32`). This tells the firewall: "Only match this
**one specific IP** and nothing else."
