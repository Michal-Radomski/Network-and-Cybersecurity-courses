While **Masquerading** is about letting internal devices "hide" behind a single public IP to go _out_ to the internet, **Port
Forwarding** is about letting the outside world come _in_ to a specific device on your private network.

If Masquerading is the "intercom" you use to call out, Port Forwarding is like the hotel manager telling the front desk, "If
anyone asks for the IT department, send them straight to Room 404."

---

## 1. How Port Forwarding Works

A router's firewall normally blocks all incoming requests to keep you safe. Port Forwarding creates a specific "hole" in that
firewall. You tell the router: "Any traffic arriving on **Public Port X** should be sent to **Private IP Y** on **Private
Port Z**."

### The Components:

- **Public/External Port:** The port the rest of the world sees (e.g., `80` for HTTP or `443` for HTTPS).
- **Private/Internal IP:** The specific address of your Node.js server (e.g., `192.168.1.50`).
- **Private/Internal Port:** The port your app is actually running on (e.g., `3000`).

---

## 2. Port Forwarding vs. Port Triggering

You might see both options in your router settings. They serve different purposes:

| Feature          | Port Forwarding (Static)                             | Port Triggering (Dynamic)                               |
| :--------------- | :--------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Connectivity** | Always open.                                         | Opened only when an internal device "triggers" it.      |
| **Use Case**     | Hosting a permanent web server or database.          | Gaming (Xbox Live/PSN) or BitTorrent.                   |
| **Security**     | Slightly riskier (the port is always open to scans). | Safer (the port closes when not in use).                |
| **IP Limit**     | Forwards to **one** specific internal IP.            | Can be used by **any** internal device (one at a time). |

---

## 3. Security Risks: The "Open Door" Policy

Port forwarding is convenient, but it is a major target for hackers.

1.  **Scanning:** Bots constantly scan public IPs for open ports like `22` (SSH), `3306` (MySQL), or `27017` (MongoDB).
2.  **Exploitation:** If your Node.js version has a vulnerability and your port is forwarded, an attacker can hit your server
    directly.

### The Modern Alternative: Reverse Proxies

In a professional React/Node.js setup, we rarely use simple Port Forwarding. Instead, we use a **Reverse Proxy** like Nginx
or a Cloud Load Balancer.

- **The Setup:** You open port `443` (HTTPS) to an **Nginx** server.
- **The Logic:** Nginx "scrubs" the request, handles the SSL/HTTPS encryption, and _then_ passes the clean traffic to your
  Node.js app on a private network.

---

## 4. Why Developers Use It

You would typically use Port Forwarding in these scenarios:

- **Self-Hosting:** You want to show a client a demo running on your home office machine.
- **Webhooks:** You are integrating with Stripe or GitHub, and their servers need to send "Push" notifications to your local
  development environment.
- **IoT:** You need to access a security camera or a smart-home hub from your phone while you're away.
