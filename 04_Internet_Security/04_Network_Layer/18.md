In networking, managing how devices share a single communication channel is crucial to prevent data from crashing into each
other—a phenomenon known as a **collision**. Carrier Sense Multiple Access (CSMA) is the underlying protocol that requires a
device to "listen" to the cable before sending data.

Here is how the two primary variations, **CSMA/CD** and **CSMA/CA**, handle traffic.

---

## 1. CSMA/CD (Collision Detection)

Used primarily in **wired Ethernet** networks (specifically older half-duplex hubs), CSMA/CD focuses on reacting to
collisions after they happen.

- **Sense:** The device listens to the line. If it's quiet, it starts transmitting.
- **Detect:** While sending, the device continues to monitor the signal levels on the wire. If it detects a voltage spike
  (indicating two signals have overlapped), it knows a collision occurred.
- **Jam:** The device immediately stops transmitting and sends a **jam signal** to notify all other devices on the segment
  that a collision happened.
- **Back off:** Every device waits for a random amount of time (using the **Binary Exponential Backoff** algorithm) before
  trying to sense the line again.

> **Note:** Modern switched Ethernet is full-duplex and uses dedicated paths, making CSMA/CD largely obsolete in modern wired
> office networks.

---

## 2. CSMA/CA (Collision Avoidance)

Used in **Wireless networks (Wi-Fi/802.11)**. Wireless signals cannot easily detect collisions while they are transmitting
because their own signal drowns out any other incoming signals. Therefore, they focus on preventing collisions before they
occur.

- **Sense:** The device listens. if the medium is busy, it waits.
- **Wait (IFS):** Even if the medium is clear, the device waits for a short period called the **Inter-Frame Space (IFS)**.
- **Contention Window:** The device picks a random "back-off" number and counts down while the medium is idle. This prevents
  multiple devices from jumping on the line the exact millisecond it becomes free.
- **Request/Clear to Send (RTS/CTS):** Optionally, the device sends a small "Request to Send" packet. The Access Point
  responds with "Clear to Send," telling all other devices to stay silent for a specific duration.
- **Acknowledgment (ACK):** Because the sender can't "hear" a collision, it assumes the data arrived safely only when the
  receiver sends back an ACK packet.

---

## Key Differences at a Glance

| Feature                 | CSMA/CD (Detection)                      | CSMA/CA (Avoidance)                         |
| :---------------------- | :--------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------ |
| **Primary Environment** | Wired (Ethernet)                         | Wireless (Wi-Fi)                            |
| **Action**              | Reduces recovery time after a collision. | Tries to prevent collisions before sending. |
| **Detection Method**    | Monitors cable voltage levels.           | Uses timers and "Ready-to-Send" signals.    |
| **Recovery**            | Uses a Jam signal and random back-off.   | Relies on the absence of an ACK to retry.   |
| **Efficiency**          | High (less overhead per packet).         | Lower (higher overhead due to signaling).   |
