To understand the backbone of modern networking, it’s helpful to view **Ethernet** and **Wi-Fi** as the two primary ways
devices "talk" to a local network. While they both follow the **IEEE 802** standards, they handle data delivery in very
different environments.

---

## 1. Ethernet Protocol (IEEE 802.3)

Ethernet is the standard for **wired** local area networks (LANs). It uses physical cables (typically twisted-pair copper or
fiber optics) to transmit data packets.

### How it Works

Ethernet uses a method called **CSMA/CD** (Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Detection).

- **Carrier Sense:** The device listens to the wire to see if it's clear.
- **Multiple Access:** Multiple devices share the same medium.
- **Collision Detection:** If two devices talk at once, they stop, wait a random amount of time, and try again.

> **Note:** In modern networks using **Switches**, collisions are virtually eliminated because the switch creates a dedicated
> path for each device.

### Key Characteristics

- **Reliability:** Extremely stable with minimal interference.
- **Speed:** Currently scales from 1 Gbps up to 400 Gbps in enterprise environments.
- **Security:** High, as a physical connection is required to intercept data.

---

## 2. Wi-Fi Protocol (IEEE 802.11)

Wi-Fi is the protocol for **wireless** networking. Instead of cables, it uses radio waves (typically 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, or 6 GHz
bands) to transmit data.

### How it Works

Unlike Ethernet, Wi-Fi cannot "detect" a collision while transmitting because the radio is busy sending. Instead, it uses
**CSMA/CA** (Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision **Avoidance**).

- **The Handshake:** Before sending data, a device often sends a "Ready to Send" (RTS) signal. The Access Point responds with
  "Clear to Send" (CTS), telling other devices to stay quiet for a millisecond.

### Key Characteristics

- **Mobility:** Allows devices to move freely within range.
- **Half-Duplex:** Generally, Wi-Fi devices cannot send and receive at the exact same time on the same channel (though
  technologies like MU-MIMO have improved this).
- **Interference:** Subject to "noise" from walls, microwaves, and other electronic devices.

---

## Comparison at a Glance

| Feature                | Ethernet (802.3)                     | Wi-Fi (802.11)                         |
| :--------------------- | :----------------------------------- | :------------------------------------- |
| **Medium**             | Physical Cables (Copper/Fiber)       | Radio Waves (Air)                      |
| **Collision Handling** | Detection (CSMA/CD)                  | Avoidance (CSMA/CA)                    |
| **Connection Type**    | Full-Duplex (Send & Receive at once) | Half-Duplex (Mostly)                   |
| **Latency**            | Very Low                             | Higher/Variable                        |
| **Common Standard**    | Cat5e, Cat6, Cat6a                   | Wi-Fi 6 (802.11ax), Wi-Fi 7 (802.11be) |

---

### The OSI Model Context

Both protocols operate primarily at the **Data Link Layer (Layer 2)** and the **Physical Layer (Layer 1)** of the OSI model.
They both use **MAC addresses** (Media Access Control) to identify unique pieces of hardware on the network.
