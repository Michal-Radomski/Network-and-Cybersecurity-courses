The **Transport Layer** (Layer 4) is often called the "heart" of the OSI model. While the lower layers focus on moving data
between machines, the Transport Layer is responsible for **end-to-end communication** between specific applications.

It acts as a bridge between the hardware-oriented lower layers and the software-oriented upper layers, ensuring that data
arrives completely, in order, and without errors.

---

## Core Responsibilities

The Transport Layer handles several critical functions to ensure data integrity:

- **Segmentation and Reassembly:** Large data chunks from the session layer are broken down into smaller units called
  **segments**. On the receiving end, it puts these segments back together in the correct order.
- **Service-Point Addressing (Port Numbers):** To ensure data reaches the correct application (e.g., your browser vs. your
  email client), the Transport Layer uses **port numbers**.
- **Flow Control:** It manages the data transmission speed. If a fast sender is overwhelming a slow receiver, the Transport
  Layer signals the sender to slow down to prevent data loss.
- **Error Control:** It uses mechanisms like checksums to detect if data was corrupted during transit. If a segment is
  missing or damaged, it requests a retransmission.
- **Connection Control:** It can be either **connection-oriented** (establishing a handshake before sending) or
  **connectionless** (sending data immediately).

---

## Primary Protocols

There are two dominant protocols at this layer, each suited for different tasks:

### 1. Transmission Control Protocol (TCP)

TCP is **connection-oriented** and prioritizes reliability over speed.

- **Reliability:** Uses a "Three-way Handshake" to establish a connection.
- **Acknowledgements:** The receiver sends a confirmation for every packet received.
- **Use Cases:** Web browsing (HTTP/S), Email (SMTP), and File Transfers (FTP).

### 2. User Datagram Protocol (UDP)

UDP is **connectionless** and prioritizes speed and efficiency over reliability.

- **Best-Effort Delivery:** It sends data without checking if the receiver is ready or if the data arrived.
- **Low Overhead:** No handshakes or retransmissions, making it much faster.
- **Use Cases:** Video streaming, Online gaming, and VoIP (Voice over IP).

---

## Summary Comparison Table

| Feature         | TCP (Connection-Oriented)  | UDP (Connectionless)                          |
| :-------------- | :------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------- |
| **Reliability** | Guaranteed delivery        | No guarantee                                  |
| **Ordering**    | Maintains message sequence | No sequence (packets may arrive out of order) |
| **Speed**       | Slower (due to overhead)   | Faster (minimal overhead)                     |
| **Data Unit**   | Segments                   | Datagrams                                     |

---

> **Key Takeaway:** Think of the Transport Layer as a **post office supervisor**. While the Network Layer (Layer 3) finds the
> path to the right house, the Transport Layer ensures the mail is opened by the right person, all pages of the letter are
> there, and they are read in the correct order.
