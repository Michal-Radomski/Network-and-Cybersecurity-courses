The **Heartbleed vulnerability** (officially indexed as CVE-2014-0160) was one of the most significant security flaws in the
history of the modern internet. Discovered in April 2014, it resided in **OpenSSL**, a widely used open-source cryptographic
library that helps power the SSL/TLS encryption used to secure websites, emails, and instant messaging.

---

## How Heartbleed Works

The vulnerability was not a flaw in the SSL/TLS protocols themselves, but rather a **coding error** in the implementation of
the "Heartbeat" extension.

### 1. The "Heartbeat" Concept

In a secure connection, a "Heartbeat" allows one computer to confirm the other is still online without renegotiating the
entire connection.

- **The Request:** Computer A sends a small packet of data (a "payload") and a number indicating the length of that data.
- **The Response:** Computer B receives the data and sends back an exact copy to prove the connection is active.

### 2. The Exploit (The "Missing Bounds Check")

The flaw occurred because OpenSSL failed to verify if the **payload length** requested by the sender actually matched the
**actual size** of the data sent.

An attacker could send a tiny piece of data (1 byte) but lie and say the payload was **64 kilobytes**. Because the software
didn't check the math, it would "reach" into its own memory, grab the 1 byte of data provided, and then grab the next
**65,535 bytes** of whatever happened to be sitting in the server's RAM to fill the requested size.

---

## What Could Be Stolen?

Because the server returned whatever was in its active memory, an attacker could repeatedly "bleed" the server to obtain:

- **Primary Key Material:** The server’s private encryption keys, allowing attackers to decrypt past and future traffic.
- **Secondary Content:** Usernames, passwords, and session cookies.
- **Personal Data:** Credit card numbers, medical records, or private communications.

The most dangerous aspect of Heartbleed was that it left **no trace** in the server logs. An attacker could siphon data
indefinitely without the administrator ever knowing the server had been breached.

---

## Impact and Resolution

At the time of its discovery, it was estimated that roughly **17% (half a million)** of the internet's "secure" web servers
were vulnerable.

### The Fix

- **Patching:** OpenSSL released a fixed version (1.0.1g) that added the necessary bounds check.
- **Revocation:** Since private keys could have been compromised, administrators had to revoke their old security
  certificates, generate new ones, and force users to change their passwords.

> **Note:** While Heartbleed is over a decade old and patched on all modern systems, it serves as a primary example of how a
> simple "buffer over-read" in a fundamental piece of infrastructure can put the entire digital world at risk.
