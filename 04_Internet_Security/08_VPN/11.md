Think of a VPN (Virtual Private Network) as a secure, private tunnel that runs right through the middle of the public highway
we call the Internet.

Normally, your data is like a postcard: anyone handling it can see who it’s from, where it’s going, and what’s written on it.
A VPN puts that postcard inside a locked armored car.

### The Life of a Packet (The Step-by-Step)

When you click a link while connected to a VPN, your data packet goes through a specific transformation:

1. **Encapsulation & Encryption:** Your VPN software takes your original data packet and wraps it inside a new, outer packet.
   It also scrambles the inner content using **encryption**. At this stage, your ISP (Internet Service Provider) can see you
   are sending data, but they can't see _what_ it is or its final destination.
2. **The Transit:** The packet travels to the VPN server. To the rest of the internet, it looks like you are just talking to
   one specific IP address (the VPN's).
3. **Decapsulation:** Once the packet reaches the VPN server, the server "unlocks" the armored car. It removes the outer
   layer, decrypts the data, and sees where the packet actually wanted to go (e.g., Google.com).
4. **The Hand-off:** The VPN server sends your request to the website. Crucially, the website sees the request coming from
   the **VPN server’s IP address**, not yours. Your identity stays hidden.
5. **The Return Trip:** The website sends data back to the VPN server, which repeats the process in reverse: encrypting it
   and tunneling it back to your device.

---

### The Core Components

To make this happen, a VPN relies on two main "layers":

- **The Tunneling Protocol:** This is the set of rules used to package the data. Common ones include **OpenVPN**,
  **WireGuard**, or **IKEv2**.
- **The Encryption:** This is the mathematical lock. Most modern VPNs use **AES-256**, which is a standard so strong that it
  would take a supercomputer billions of years to crack by brute force.

### Why go through the trouble?

- **Privacy:** Your ISP can't track your browsing habits to sell to advertisers.
- **Security:** On public Wi-Fi (like at a cafe), a VPN prevents hackers on the same network from "sniffing" your traffic.
- **Spoofing:** By using a server in another country, you appear to be browsing from that location, which helps bypass local
  censorship or geo-blocks.
