To put it simply, **IP Tunneling** is the process of wrapping one data packet inside another data packet.

It acts like a "delivery envelope" for your data. If you have a letter (the original IP packet) that cannot travel across a
specific network on its own, you put it inside a larger, specialized envelope (the encapsulating IP packet) that has the
correct addressing to reach the other side.

### How Encapsulation Works

The core mechanism of tunneling is **encapsulation**.

1. **The Payload:** Your original data packet, including its source and destination IP headers.
2. **The Tunnel Header:** A new header added by the tunneling protocol. This header contains the entry and exit points of the
   tunnel.
3. **Transit:** The "outer" packet moves through the intermediate network. Routers along the way only look at the outer
   header.
4. **Decapsulation:** When the packet reaches the end of the tunnel, the outer header is stripped off, and the original
   packet is delivered to its final destination.

### Common Types of IP Tunnels

| Protocol                                | Description                                                | Best For...                                                      |
| --------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **GRE (Generic Routing Encapsulation)** | A simple, flexible protocol developed by Cisco.            | Encapsulating a wide variety of network layer protocols.         |
| **IP-in-IP**                            | Wraps one IP packet directly inside another IP packet.     | Low overhead; connecting two private networks over the internet. |
| **IPsec**                               | Adds strong encryption and authentication to the tunnel.   | Secure VPNs and protecting sensitive data.                       |
| **6to4 / 4to6**                         | Tunnels IPv6 traffic over an IPv4 network (or vice versa). | Transitioning between different internet protocol versions.      |

### Why Do We Need It?

#### 1. Connecting "Islands"

If you have two private office networks (LANs) separated by the public internet, they cannot "talk" to each other directly
because private IP addresses aren't routable on the web. An IP tunnel creates a virtual bridge between them.

#### 2. Bypassing Incompatibility

Sometimes a network doesn't support the protocol you're using. For example, if you are using the modern **IPv6** but your
Internet Service Provider (ISP) only supports the older **IPv4**, you can "tunnel" your IPv6 packets inside IPv4 packets to
get them across.

#### 3. Security (VPNs)

Most Virtual Private Networks (VPNs) use IP tunneling combined with encryption. The tunnel ensures the data gets to the right
place, while the encryption ensures that if anyone "sniffs" the packet while it's in transit, they only see the encrypted
outer shell, not your actual data.

---

### IP Tunneling vs. SSH/SOCKS

While they all involve "moving data inside other data," there is a key difference in **where** they live:

- **SSH/SOCKS** usually work at the **Application or Session layer**. They are often used for specific apps (like a web
  browser).
- **IP Tunneling** works at the **Network layer**. It handles the actual IP packets, meaning it can carry _all_ types of
  traffic from a device or network, regardless of the application.
