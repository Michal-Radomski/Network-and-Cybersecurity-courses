A **Virtual Private Network (VPN)** is a service that creates a secure, encrypted "tunnel" between your device and the
internet. It masks your IP address and encrypts your data, making your online activities private and allowing you to appear
as if you are connecting from a different geographic location.

---

## How a VPN Works

Normally, when you access a website, your data travels from your device to your Internet Service Provider (ISP), and then to
the website's server. During this trip, your ISP and any third parties can see your IP address and what you are doing.

With a VPN, the process changes:

1. **Encryption:** Your VPN client (the app) encrypts your data before it even leaves your device.
2. **Tunneling:** The data travels through a secure "tunnel" to the VPN server.
3. **IP Masking:** The VPN server decrypts your data and sends it to the website. To the website, the request looks like it
   came from the **VPN server's IP address**, not yours.

---

## Key Benefits of Using a VPN

- **Privacy from ISPs:** Your ISP can see that you are connected to a VPN, but they cannot see which websites you visit or
  what data you send.
- **Security on Public Wi-Fi:** Public networks (like those in coffee shops) are often unencrypted. A VPN protects your
  passwords and credit card info from "sniffing" attacks.
- **Geo-Spoofing:** By connecting to a server in another country, you can access content (like streaming libraries or news
  sites) that might be blocked in your actual location.
- **Bypassing Censorship:** In countries with heavy internet restrictions, VPNs are often used to access the open web.

---

## Common VPN Protocols

Protocols are the sets of rules that determine how the data actually moves through the tunnel.

| Protocol        | Description                                                                                                         |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **OpenVPN**     | The industry standard. Highly secure and open-source, though it can be slightly slower on older devices.            |
| **WireGuard**   | The "new kid on the block." It is much faster and uses state-of-the-art cryptography.                               |
| **IKEv2/IPsec** | Excellent for mobile devices because it handles reconnecting very well when you switch from Wi-Fi to cellular data. |

## Types of VPNs

### 1. Remote Access VPN

This is what most individuals use. You install an app on your phone or laptop to connect to a service provider's network.

### 2. Site-to-Site VPN

Used primarily by businesses. It connects two entire networks together—for example, connecting a branch office in New York to
the main corporate office in London so employees can share files securely.

---

## Important Disclaimers

While VPNs are powerful tools, they aren't magic:

- **No Absolute Anonymity:** If you log into your Facebook or Google account while using a VPN, those companies still know
  exactly who you are.
- **Trust is Key:** You are essentially moving your trust from your ISP to your VPN provider. It is vital to use a provider
  with a **"No-Logs Policy"** that has been independently audited.
- **Speed Impact:** Because your data has to travel to an extra server and go through encryption/decryption, your internet
  speed may drop slightly.

---

### A Quick Tip

If you're looking for a VPN, avoid "free" services whenever possible. Running a global network of servers is expensive; if
they aren't charging you money, they are likely making a profit by selling your browsing data to advertisers—which defeats
the purpose of having a VPN in the first place!
