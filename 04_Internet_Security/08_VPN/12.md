In the world of network security, **Ingress** and **Egress** refer to the direction of data traffic relative to your
network's boundary. Think of your network like a secure building: ingress is who comes in the front door, and egress is who
leaves through the back.

---

## 1. Ingress Firewall (Inbound Traffic)

**Ingress filtering** inspects traffic entering your network from the outside (typically the internet) and headed toward your
internal servers or devices.

- **Primary Goal:** To prevent external attackers from gaining unauthorized access to your private systems.
- **How it works:** It checks the source IP address and the destination port. For example, if you host a website, your
  ingress rules might allow traffic on port **80 (HTTP)** but block traffic on port **22 (SSH)** to prevent hackers from
  trying to log into your server.
- **Key Protections:** \* Blocks Denial of Service (DoS) attacks.
- Prevents "spoofing" (traffic claiming to be from inside your network when it's actually coming from outside).

---

## 2. Egress Firewall (Outbound Traffic)

**Egress filtering** monitors and restricts data leaving your internal network destined for the outside world. This is often
the most overlooked part of security.

- **Primary Goal:** To ensure that internal systems are not sending unauthorized data out.
- **How it works:** It restricts internal devices to only communicating via specific protocols or with approved external
  sites.
- **Key Protections:**
- **Data Exfiltration:** Prevents a rogue employee or malware from uploading sensitive files to a personal cloud drive.
- **Malware Communication:** If a computer is infected with a virus, an egress filter can stop that virus from "phoning home"
  to its Command and Control (C&C) server.
- **Spam Prevention:** Prevents infected internal machines from being used as "bots" to send out millions of spam emails.

---

## Comparison Summary

| Feature            | Ingress                                  | Egress                                             |
| ------------------ | ---------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| **Direction**      | External $\rightarrow$ Internal          | Internal $\rightarrow$ External                    |
| **Analogy**        | The bouncer at the club door.            | The security guard checking bags at the exit.      |
| **Main Threat**    | Hackers, scanners, and exploits.         | Data leaks and malware "phoning home."             |
| **Typical Policy** | "Deny all" except for specific services. | Often "Allow all" (though "Restrictive" is safer). |

### Why you need both

Relying only on **Ingress** filtering is like locking your front door but leaving the windows open. If an attacker manages to
get a piece of malware inside via an email attachment (which bypasses the ingress firewall because the user "invited" the
email in), only an **Egress** filter can stop that malware from actually doing its job and sending your data back to the
hacker.
