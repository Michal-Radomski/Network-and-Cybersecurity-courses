Since you just learned about IP addresses, ports, and how packets are wrapped, a **socket** is the final piece of the puzzle.
It is the "plug" that connects a specific program to the network.

Think of it this way:

- The **IP Address** is the address of the apartment building.
- The **Port** is the specific apartment number.
- The **Socket** is the actual physical door where the data enters or leaves that apartment.

---

### 1. The Anatomy of a Socket

A socket is defined by the combination of an **IP Address** and a **Port Number**. In technical terms, it looks like this:

$$192.168.1.10:80$$

In this example:

- `192.168.1.10` is the host's address.
- `80` is the port (standard for web traffic).
- Together, they form a unique **endpoint** for communication.

---

### 2. How Sockets Work in a Conversation

For two computers to talk, they need a **Socket Pair**. This consists of two sockets: one on the "Client" (your computer) and
one on the "Server" (the website).

| Component      | Client Side (Your PC)             | Server Side (Website)     |
| :------------- | :-------------------------------- | :------------------------ |
| **IP Address** | `10.0.0.5`                        | `142.250.190.46` (Google) |
| **Port**       | `52341` (A random temporary port) | `443` (HTTPS)             |
| **The Socket** | `10.0.0.5:52341`                  | `142.250.190.46:443`      |

When you open a website, your computer creates a temporary socket to send the request, and the server listens on its
permanent socket to send the data back.

---

### 3. Types of Sockets

There are two main "flavors" of sockets you’ll run into while learning:

- **Stream Sockets (TCP):** These are like a phone call. A connection is established, and it's guaranteed that the data
  arrives in the correct order without errors. (Used for Web, Email, File transfers).
- **Datagram Sockets (UDP):** These are like shouting at someone across a room. It's faster, but there’s no guarantee they
  heard you or that the words arrived in order. (Used for Online Gaming, Video Streaming).

---

### 4. Why do we need them?

Without sockets, your computer wouldn't know which app a packet belongs to. If you are browsing the web and listening to
Spotify at the same time, both apps are receiving packets at the same IP address.

The **Socket** is what tells the operating system: _"Hey, this packet is for the Browser (Port 443), and that packet is for
Spotify (Port 4070)."_

---

### A Simple "Learning" Analogy

Imagine a massive office building (the **Computer**).

- The building has one street address (**IP Address**).
- Inside, there are thousands of internal mailboxes (**Ports**).
- The **Socket** is the actual key that opens a specific mailbox so the mail (the **Data**) can be retrieved by the right
  employee (the **Application**).
