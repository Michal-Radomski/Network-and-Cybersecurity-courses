Since you've already seen how a packet is "wrapped" (encapsulation) and how sockets "receive" data, **sniffing** is
essentially the art of intercepting that process before it reaches the intended socket.

To understand how it works, we have to look at the **Network Interface Card (NIC)**—the hardware in your computer that
handles the wires or Wi-Fi signals.

---

### 1. Normal Mode vs. Promiscuous Mode

In a normal situation, your NIC is "polite." It looks at the **MAC Address** on the Layer 2 Frame.

- **Normal Mode:** The NIC says, "Is this packet addressed to me? No? Okay, I'll ignore it." It literally discards the data
  so your CPU doesn't get overwhelmed.
- **Promiscuous Mode:** The sniffer software tells the NIC, "Stop ignoring things! Pass **everything** you see on the wire
  directly to me, even if it's addressed to someone else."

---

### 2. The Sniffing Process (Step-by-Step)

Imagine a "Hub" or a Wi-Fi network where data is being broadcast to everyone.

1.  **Collection:** The NIC captures the raw binary pulses from the wire or air.
2.  **Conversion:** Because the NIC is in promiscuous mode, it doesn't discard "wrong" packets. It converts those pulses back
    into digital **Frames**.
3.  **Decoding (Dissection):** The sniffer software (like Wireshark) looks at the headers we discussed earlier. It
    identifies:
    - "This is an IP packet..."
    - "...carrying a TCP segment..."
    - "...containing HTTP data."
4.  **Display:** The software shows you the "Cleartext" data. If the user is visiting a non-encrypted site, you see their
    password exactly as they typed it.

---

### 3. Sniffing in Different Environments

The "physics" of the network changes how effective sniffing is:

- **On Wi-Fi:** Since Wi-Fi is just radio waves in the air, anyone within range can "sniff" the packets. This is why modern
  Wi-Fi uses encryption (WPA2/WPA3) to scramble the airwaves.
- **On a Switch:** Modern wired networks use "Switches" instead of "Hubs." A switch is smart; it only sends a packet to the
  specific port where the destination device is plugged in.
  - _The Catch:_ This is where **Spoofing** comes back in. An attacker will use **ARP Spoofing** to trick the switch into
    sending everyone's packets to the attacker's port so they can sniff them.

---

### 4. How to Defeat Sniffing

The most important thing to learn as a beginner is that **you cannot always stop someone from sniffing, but you can make the
data useless.**

- **Encryption (SSL/TLS):** This is the ultimate defense. If I sniff your connection to `https://google.com`, I can see that
  you are talking to Google, but the actual message looks like random garbage ($09af23...$) because I don't have the "key" to
  decrypt it.
- **VPNs:** A VPN wraps all your traffic in a second, encrypted "tunnel." Even if someone sniffs your local Wi-Fi, all they
  see is the encrypted VPN tunnel, not your individual packets.

### Summary for your Learning

- **Sniffing** = Setting the NIC to "Promiscuous Mode" to see all traffic.
- **Decoding** = Reading the headers of those packets to understand the data.
- **Encryption** = The "Antidote" that makes sniffed data unreadable.
