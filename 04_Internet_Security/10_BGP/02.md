At its core, the Internet isn't a single monolithic network; it is a "network of networks." To understand how data travels
from a server in London to a phone in Tokyo, you have to look at the building blocks: **Autonomous Systems (AS)** and the
relationships they form through **Peering**.

---

## 1. What is an Autonomous System (AS)?

An **Autonomous System** is a large network or a group of networks that has a unified routing policy. Think of an AS as a
"digital country." Just as a country has its own internal roads and a government that decides how to interact with neighbors,
an AS is managed by a single entity—like an Internet Service Provider (ISP), a large university, or a tech giant (Google,
Amazon, Meta).

- **AS Numbers (ASN):** Every AS is assigned a unique identification number.
- **Routing Control:** Inside an AS, the owner controls all the routing. To talk to the rest of the world, the AS uses the
  **Border Gateway Protocol (BGP)**.

---

## 2. How ASs Connect: Peering vs. Transit

Networks connect in two primary ways. The choice usually depends on money, traffic volume, and geography.

### Peering (The Handshake)

Peering is when two ASs connect and exchange traffic directly for mutual benefit, usually without charging each other.

- **Public Peering:** Happens at **Internet Exchange Points (IXPs)**, where dozens of networks plug into a shared switch.
- **Private Peering:** Two networks (like Netflix and Comcast) lay a physical cable directly between their data centers
  because they swap so much data that it's cheaper than using a middleman.
- **The Goal:** Lower latency and lower costs. "I’ll carry your users' traffic if you carry mine."

### Transit (The Customer Relationship)

Transit is a "provider-customer" relationship. A smaller ISP pays a larger, global network (a **Tier 1 ISP**) to carry their
traffic to the rest of the entire Internet.

- If you are a tiny local ISP, you can’t peer with everyone in the world. You pay a "Transit" provider to handle the
  connections for you.

---

## 3. How Data Moves: The Border Gateway Protocol (BGP)

If the Internet is a collection of "digital countries," **BGP** is the postal service that decides which path a package
should take.

Instead of knowing every single house address in the world, BGP works by exchanging "prefixes" (ranges of IP addresses). An
AS tells its neighbors: _"Hey, I know how to get to these 10,000 IP addresses. Send them to me!"_

### The Path Selection

BGP doesn't always choose the fastest path; it chooses the "shortest" path in terms of **AS hops**.

- If a packet starts in **AS 100** and needs to get to **AS 500**, BGP looks at the map:
- Path A: AS 100 → AS 200 → AS 500 (2 hops)
- Path B: AS 100 → AS 300 → AS 400 → AS 500 (3 hops)

- BGP will typically prefer **Path A**.

---

## 4. The Hierarchy of Connection

Not all Autonomous Systems are created equal. They are generally categorized into three tiers:

| Tier       | Description                                                                                    | Connection Method           |
| ---------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------- |
| **Tier 1** | The "backbone" of the internet. They can reach every other network without paying for transit. | Pure Peering.               |
| **Tier 2** | Large regional providers. They peer with some but must pay Tier 1s for global access.          | Mix of Peering and Transit. |
| **Tier 3** | Local ISPs or small companies. They primarily buy access from Tier 2s.                         | Mostly Transit.             |

---

## Summary

To put it simply: The Internet is a massive puzzle of **Autonomous Systems**. They stay connected by "shouting" their
locations to each other via **BGP**. When they decide to work together for free to save money, they **Peer**; when a smaller
network needs a big brother to reach the world, they buy **Transit**.
