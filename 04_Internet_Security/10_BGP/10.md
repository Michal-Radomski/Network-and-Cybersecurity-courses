Border Gateway Protocol (BGP) is the "postal service" of the internet. It manages how packets are routed across the vast web
of interconnected networks (called Autonomous Systems, or AS). Because BGP was designed in an era when the internet was a
smaller, more "trusting" community, it lacks inherent security features, making it a prime target for exploitation.

BGP attacks generally fall into three categories: **Hijacking**, **Route Leaking**, and **DoS/Stability attacks**.

---

## 1. BGP Route Hijacking

This is the most common and dangerous BGP attack. It occurs when an AS maliciously (or accidentally) claims to own an IP
address range (prefix) that it does not actually control.

- **The Mechanism:** An attacker uses a BGP router to announce a more specific route or a shorter path to a target's IP
  prefix. Because BGP prefers the "best" path, neighboring routers update their routing tables to send traffic to the
  attacker.
- **The Impact:** \* **Blackholing:** Traffic simply drops, causing an outage for the victim.
- **Impersonation/Phishing:** The attacker intercepts traffic, presents a fake website (like a bank), and steals credentials.
- **Man-in-the-Middle (MITM):** The attacker inspects or modifies the traffic before forwarding it to the actual destination,
  often without the user noticing.

---

## 2. BGP Route Leaks

A route leak is often the result of misconfiguration rather than malice, but the results are just as disruptive. It occurs
when an AS receives routing information from one provider and "leaks" it to another provider or peer.

- **The Result:** Traffic that should have stayed on a high-speed backbone is diverted through a smaller, lower-capacity
  network. This leads to massive congestion, latency, and packet loss across large segments of the internet.
- **Example:** In 2019, a small company in Pennsylvania inadvertently became a transit point for a massive chunk of global
  internet traffic (including Cloudflare and Amazon) due to a leaked route, effectively breaking the internet for hours.

---

## 3. BGP Denial of Service (DoS)

BGP can be used as a weapon to take down entire networks or the BGP routers themselves.

- **BGP Peering Session Reset:** If an attacker can guess the TCP sequence numbers of a BGP session between two routers, they
  can send a forged "Reset" packet. This tears down the connection, forcing the routers to recalculate the entire internet
  routing table, which consumes massive CPU cycles and causes temporary outages.
- **AS Path Prepending Attacks:** An attacker can manipulate the AS_PATH attribute to make certain routes look incredibly
  long or loop back on themselves, causing routers to ignore valid paths and isolating certain networks.

---

## 4. BGP Simulation/Large-Scale Instability

Sometimes called a **BGP "flap,"** an attacker can rapidly announce and then withdraw a route.

- **Flapping:** This forces every router on the internet to constantly update their tables. To protect themselves, routers
  use "Route Dampening," where they stop listening to a "flapping" route. An attacker can purposely trigger this to get a
  legitimate business's IP range suppressed by the rest of the world.

---

## Current Defense Mechanisms

The industry is slowly moving toward a more secure framework to prevent these "accidents" and attacks:

| Defense              | Description                                                                                                                                                    |
| -------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **RPKI**             | **Resource Public Key Infrastructure.** A cryptographic method for owners to "tag" their IP prefixes so routers can verify who is authorized to announce them. |
| **BGPsec**           | An extension to BGP that uses digital signatures to verify the entire path of a route, not just the origin.                                                    |
| **Prefix Filtering** | Network operators manually configure lists of which IP ranges their customers are allowed to announce.                                                         |
| **MANRS**            | **Mutually Agreed Norms for Routing Security.** A global initiative that encourages providers to implement basic security filtering and coordination.          |

> **Note:** While RPKI significantly reduces the risk of accidental hijacking, adoption is not yet universal, meaning the
> internet remains vulnerable to "route-leaking" from networks that haven't implemented these standards.
