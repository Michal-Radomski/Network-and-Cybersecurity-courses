To put it simply, if the internet is a massive collection of individual neighborhoods, the **Border Gateway Protocol (BGP)**
is the GPS and postal system that figures out how to get a package from one side of the world to the other.

It is the protocol that makes the internet a "network of networks."

---

## 1. What is BGP?

BGP is a **Path Vector Protocol** used to exchange routing information between different **Autonomous Systems (AS)**.

An Autonomous System is essentially a large network or group of networks managed by a single entity, like an Internet Service
Provider (ISP), a university, or a tech giant like Google or Amazon. Without BGP, these separate "islands" of networks
wouldn't know how to talk to each other.

---

## 2. How It Works: The "Postal Service" Analogy

Think of BGP like a global mail delivery system:

- **IP Addresses** are like house addresses.
- **Autonomous Systems** are like cities or zip codes.
- **BGP Routers** are the regional postal hubs.

When you send data, BGP doesn't look for the "shortest" physical path (like the fewest miles of cable). Instead, it looks for
the **best path** based on a list of attributes, which often include the fewest "hops" between different AS networks and
specific business agreements between ISPs.

---

## 3. Key Components of BGP

### Autonomous Systems (AS)

Each AS is assigned a unique **ASN** (Autonomous System Number). For example, Google's ASN is 15169. BGP uses these numbers
to track the path data takes.

### Internal vs. External BGP

- **eBGP (External BGP):** Used to connect one AS to another. This is the "glue" of the internet.
- **iBGP (Internal BGP):** Used to distribute the external routing information _within_ a single AS.

### The Routing Table

BGP routers maintain a massive table of all possible paths to every block of IP addresses on the internet. They constantly
"advertise" their known routes to their neighbors to keep this table updated.

---

## 4. Why is BGP so important? (And fragile!)

BGP is designed for **scale** and **reliability**, but it has some famous quirks:

- **Convergence:** When a major cable is cut, BGP routers across the globe have to update their tables. This "propagation" of
  new info is called convergence.
- **BGP Hijacking:** Since BGP was built on trust, a rogue network can accidentally (or maliciously) "advertise" that it owns
  a set of IP addresses it doesn't. This can lead to traffic being redirected, causing massive outages or security breaches.
- **Route Flapping:** If a connection is unstable and keeps flickering on and off, it can trigger a storm of BGP updates,
  potentially overwhelming routers.

---

## 5. BGP Decision Process

When a router has multiple ways to get to a destination, it goes through a specific hierarchy to choose the winner. It’s not
just about speed; it follows a "tie-breaker" logic:

1. **Weight:** A local setting (higher is better).
2. **Local Preference:** Tells the AS which exit point to prefer.
3. **AS Path Length:** The path with the fewest AS "hops" usually wins.
4. **MED (Multi-Exit Discriminator):** Suggests a preferred path to external neighbors.

---

> **Note:** BGP is often called the "Protocol that breaks the internet." When you hear about Facebook, WhatsApp, or major
> banks "disappearing" from the web for a few hours, it is almost always due to a BGP configuration error that told the rest
> of the world those services no longer existed.
