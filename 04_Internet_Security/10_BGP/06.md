In the world of networking, **iBGP** (Internal Border Gateway Protocol) and **IGP** (Interior Gateway Protocol) are both used
inside a single Autonomous System (AS), but they serve fundamentally different purposes. Think of them as the difference
between a high-level logistics coordinator and the local delivery drivers.

---

## 1. Defining the Roles

### **Interior Gateway Protocol (IGP)**

IGPs like **OSPF**, **IS-IS**, or **EIGRP** are the "workhorses" of your internal network. Their primary job is to discover
the shortest path to every internal IP address (next-hop reachability).

- **Goal:** Move packets from Point A to Point B within the network as quickly as possible.
- **Scale:** Designed for speed and fast convergence, but cannot handle millions of routes.

### **Internal BGP (iBGP)**

iBGP is a specific mode of BGP used to carry external routing information (from the internet or other ASes) _across_ your
internal network.

- **Goal:** Distribute external prefixes between routers that aren't directly connected to each other.
- **Scale:** Designed for massive scale (the entire internet routing table).

---

## 2. Key Differences at a Glance

| Feature              | IGP (OSPF/IS-IS)                              | iBGP                                                                        |
| -------------------- | --------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Primary Function** | Internal reachability and path calculation.   | Carrying external prefixes across the AS.                                   |
| **Next-Hop Logic**   | Knows how to reach the next hop directly.     | Relies on the IGP to find the path to the BGP next hop.                     |
| **Algorithm**        | Dijkstra (SPF) or DUAL.                       | Path Vector (Attributes like Local Preference).                             |
| **Relationship**     | Forms adjacencies with direct neighbors.      | Can form "neighbor" relationships with distant routers.                     |
| **Loop Prevention**  | Link-state database or distance vector rules. | Split Horizon (does not advertise iBGP-learned routes to other iBGP peers). |

---

## 3. Why do we need both?

You might wonder, "If iBGP is inside my network, why can't I just use OSPF for everything?"

### **The "Why Not Just IGP?" Problem**

IGPs are not built for the internet's scale. If you tried to inject 900,000+ internet routes into OSPF, the CPU on your
routers would likely crash trying to run the Shortest Path First (SPF) algorithm. iBGP handles the volume, while the IGP
handles the "underlay" connectivity.

### **The Recursive Lookup**

This is the "secret sauce" of how they work together:

1. **iBGP** tells a router: _"To reach 8.8.8.8, go to exit router 10.1.1.1."_
2. **IGP** tells the router: _"To reach 10.1.1.1, take the path through Interface Gigabit0/1."_

---

## 4. Design Requirements for iBGP

Because of the **iBGP Split Horizon** rule (a router will not pass a route learned via iBGP to another iBGP peer), you
generally need one of two things to ensure all routers stay updated:

- **Full Mesh:** Every iBGP router must have a direct logical connection to every other iBGP router.
- **Route Reflectors (RR):** A central hub that "reflects" routes to other routers, breaking the split-horizon rule in a
  controlled way to save on configuration.

---

> **Summary:** The **IGP** builds the roads inside your city, while **iBGP** is the shipping manifest telling you which city
> exit leads to a different country.
