On February 24, 2008, the internet experienced a major technical "hiccup" that took YouTube offline for most of the world for
several hours. This wasn't a malicious hack by a bored teenager, but rather a side effect of **BGP Hijacking**—an incident
that serves as a classic case study in how the internet's core routing system can be accidentally broken.

### What Happened?

The government of **Pakistan** issued an order to **Pakistan Telecom** to block YouTube within the country due to content
deemed offensive. To comply, Pakistan Telecom used a method called "null routing."

Instead of letting traffic go to YouTube, they told their internal routers to send any requests for YouTube's IP addresses
into a "black hole." However, they accidentally broadcast this routing instruction to their international provider, **PCCW**
in Hong Kong.

### The BGP Domino Effect

The internet relies on the **Border Gateway Protocol (BGP)** to find the most efficient path between networks. BGP operates
on a system of trust: if a network claims it has the shortest path to a destination, other networks believe it and update
their maps.

1. **The Claim:** Pakistan Telecom announced a very specific route for YouTube's IP range.
2. **The Propagation:** PCCW accepted this route and shared it with the rest of the global internet.
3. **The Hijack:** Because Pakistan Telecom’s route was "more specific" than YouTube’s own broad announcement, global traffic
   (from the US, Europe, and elsewhere) was sucked toward Pakistan.
4. **The Result:** Since Pakistan’s routers couldn't handle the entire planet's YouTube traffic—and were programmed to drop
   it anyway—YouTube effectively vanished for nearly two-thirds of the global web.

### How It Was Resolved

YouTube’s engineers eventually noticed the redirection and fought back by:

- Issuing even **more specific** route announcements to reclaim their traffic.
- Working with global ISPs to ignore the "fake" routes coming from Pakistan.
- It took approximately **two hours** for the services to stabilize globally.

---

### Key Lessons

This incident exposed a massive vulnerability in the internet’s design: **BGP has no built-in security.** It assumes everyone
is telling the truth. Since 2008, there has been a significant push for **RPKI (Resource Public Key Infrastructure)**, a
cryptographic method that allows network owners to prove they actually "own" the IP addresses they are announcing.

While things have improved, BGP leaks and hijacks still happen today, proving that the "World Wide Web" is often held
together by a surprisingly fragile handshake.
