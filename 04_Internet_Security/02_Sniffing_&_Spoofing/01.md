In the world of networking, sniffing and spoofing are two distinct techniques often used together in a "Man-in-the-Middle"
(MITM) attack. One is about **listening**, while the other is about **lying**.

---

## 1. Sniffing (The "Eavesdropper")

**Sniffing** is the process of capturing and monitoring data packets as they flow across a network. It is the digital
equivalent of wiretapping a phone line.

- **How it works:** Normally, a network card only looks at data addressed to it. A "sniffer" puts the network interface into
  **promiscuous mode**, allowing it to see every single packet passing through the segment, regardless of the destination.
- **The Tool:** Software like **Wireshark** or **Tcpdump** are common packet sniffers.
- **What is captured:** If the data isn't encrypted (like standard HTTP or FTP), a sniffer can see passwords, usernames, and
  private messages in "cleartext."

---

## 2. Spoofing (The "Impersonator")

**Spoofing** is the act of disguising a communication from an unknown source as being from a known, trusted source. It is
digital identity theft at the protocol level.

- **How it works:** The attacker modifies the "From" field in a packet header (like an IP address or MAC address) to trick
  the receiving system into thinking the data is coming from a legitimate device.
- **Common Types:**
  - **IP Spoofing:** Sending packets from a fake IP address to bypass firewalls.
  - **ARP Spoofing:** Linking an attacker’s MAC address with the IP address of a legitimate gateway (like the router) to
    redirect traffic through the attacker's machine.
  - **DNS Spoofing:** Diverting a user from a real website (like `bank.com`) to a fraudulent one by corrupting DNS server
    records.

---

## Comparison at a Glance

| Feature        | Sniffing                             | Spoofing                                          |
| :------------- | :----------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------ |
| **Action**     | Passive observation / capturing.     | Active deception / impersonation.                 |
| **Goal**       | To steal information (data theft).   | To gain access or redirect traffic.               |
| **Visibility** | Very hard to detect (passive).       | Can be detected by monitoring traffic anomalies.  |
| **Analogy**    | Listening to a private conversation. | Wearing a mask and pretending to be someone else. |

---

### How they work together

A typical attack looks like this:

1.  The attacker uses **ARP Spoofing** to tell the Victim's computer, "I am the Router," and tell the Router, "I am the
    Victim."
2.  All traffic between the Victim and the Internet now flows through the attacker's laptop.
3.  The attacker then uses **Sniffing** to read all the sensitive data passing through before forwarding it to the real
    destination so the victim doesn't suspect a thing.

### Prevention

- **Encryption:** Use VPNs, HTTPS (SSL/TLS), and SSH. Even if an attacker sniffs the data, they won't be able to read it.
- **Static ARP:** Manually mapping MAC addresses to IPs (though difficult to manage in large networks).
- **Packet Filtering:** Routers can be configured to drop packets that appear to be coming from "inside" the network but
  actually originate from the "outside."
