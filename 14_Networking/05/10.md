**DNS (Domain Name System)** is often called the "phonebook of the internet." Its primary job is to translate human-readable
domain names (like `google.com`) into machine-readable IP addresses (like `142.250.190.46`) so your browser can load the
website.

When you type a URL into your browser, a fast-paced game of "hot potato" happens between your client device and a hierarchy
of DNS servers.

---

## The Step-by-Step DNS Lookup Journey

Here is exactly what happens behind the scenes when a client requests a website:

### 1. The Local Cache Check (Client Side)

Before reaching out to the internet, your computer tries to find the answer locally to save time.

- **Browser Cache:** Your browser checks if you’ve visited the site recently.
- **OS Cache:** If the browser doesn't know, it asks your computer's Operating System.
- **Router Cache:** If the OS doesn't know, it checks your home router.

### 2. Asking the Recursive Resolver (The ISP)

If the IP isn't in any local cache, the client sends a request to a **DNS Recursive Resolver** (usually provided by your
Internet Service Provider, or services like Cloudflare `1.1.1.1` and Google `8.8.8.8`).

The resolver acts like a personal assistant. It says, _"I don't know the IP, but give me a second and I will go find it for
you."_

### 3. The Root Nameserver (`.`)

The resolver starts at the very top of the DNS hierarchy. It asks a **Root Nameserver**: _"Where can I find `example.com`?"_

- The Root server doesn't know the IP, but it knows who manages the extension.
- It replies: _"I don't know, but go ask the **.com** Top-Level Domain (TLD) server at this address."_

### 4. The TLD Nameserver (`.com`)

The resolver then travels to the **TLD Nameserver** (in this case, the one responsible for all `.com` domains).

- The TLD server replies: _"I don't have the exact IP, but I know who manages `example.com`. Go ask their **Authoritative
  Nameserver**."_

### 5. The Authoritative Nameserver

This is the final stop. The **Authoritative Nameserver** is the official keeper of the DNS records for that specific website
(usually managed by the website's hosting provider or registrar like GoDaddy or Namecheap).

- The resolver asks: _"What is the IP for `example.com`?"_
- The Authoritative server replies: _"I have the exact record right here! The IP address is `93.184.216.34`."_

### 6. Back to the Client

The Recursive Resolver takes that IP address, runs back to your computer, hands it over, and caches it for next time so it
doesn't have to do the whole journey again.

### 7. Loading the Webpage

Now that your browser finally has the destination IP address, it can form a direct connection with the web server hosting the
site and load the page.

---

## Recursive vs. Iterative Queries

You might notice there are two different ways servers talk to each other during this process:

- **Recursive Query (Client $\rightarrow$ Resolver):** The client demands a final answer. It tells the resolver, _"Get me the
  exact IP, or tell me the site doesn't exist. Don't give me homework."_
- **Iterative Query (Resolver $\rightarrow$ DNS Hierarchy):** The resolver plays a game of hot potato with the Root, TLD, and
  Authoritative servers. It asks, _"Do you know the answer?"_ and the servers reply with, _"No, but try asking this guy
  next."_
