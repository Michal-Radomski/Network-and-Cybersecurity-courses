An **FQDN**, or **Fully Qualified Domain Name**, is the complete, absolute internet address for a specific computer, host, or
server on the internet.

Unlike a standard domain name (like `google.com`), an FQDN is completely unambiguous. It specifies the exact location of a
device within the global Domain Name System (DNS) hierarchy, leaving no room for guessing.

---

## The Structure of an FQDN

An FQDN is built from right to left, moving from the most general category to the most specific device.

Here is how a full FQDN like **`www.support.example.com.`** breaks down:

### 1. The Root Zone (`.`)

- **What it is:** The very end of the FQDN. In a strictly absolute FQDN, there is a final trailing dot (period).
- **Example:** The final `.` in `com.`
- _Note:_ Web browsers automatically assume this dot is there, so you rarely have to type it yourself.

### 2. Top-Level Domain or TLD (`com`)

- **What it is:** The highest level in the DNS hierarchy after the root.
- **Examples:** `.com`, `.org`, `.net`, `.edu`, `.gov`, or country codes like `.uk`.

### 3. Second-Level Domain (`example`)

- **What it is:** The unique name registered by an individual or organization.
- **Example:** `google`, `wikipedia`, `apple`.

### 4. Subdomain (`support`)

- **What it is:** An optional division created by the domain owner to organize different departments or sections of their
  site.
- **Examples:** `blog`, `shop`, `mail`.

### 5. Hostname (`www`)

- **What it is:** The specific server, device, or service providing the resource. While `www` is the most famous hostname
  used for web servers, it can be anything.
- **Examples:** `ftp`, `imap`, `mail-server-01`.

---

## FQDN vs. PQDN (Partially Qualified Domain Name)

To understand an FQDN, it helps to look at its opposite: a **PQDN**. A PQDN is incomplete and relies on the local network
context to be understood.

- **FQDN (Absolute):** `mail.corporate.google.com.`
- Anyone, anywhere in the world, typing this will reach the exact same mail server.

- **PQDN (Relative):** `mail` or `mail.corporate`
- If you are already logged into Google’s internal network, typing just `mail` might work because the network can guess the
  rest. However, if an outsider types `mail`, their computer won't know where to go.

---

## A Real-World Analogy

Think of mailing a letter:

- **PQDN:** "John's Desk" (Works fine if you are already inside John's office building, but useless to the mailman outside).
- **FQDN:** "John Doe, Desk 4, 3rd Floor, 123 Main Street, New York, NY, USA." (Contains every single piece of hierarchical
  information needed to deliver the letter from anywhere in the world).
