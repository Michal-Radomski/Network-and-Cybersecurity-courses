An **Autonomous System (AS)** is a massive collection of IP networks and routers under the control of a single administrative
entity—such as an Internet Service Provider (ISP), a massive tech corporation (like Google or Microsoft), a university, or a
government agency.

If you think of individual networks as internal office hallways, an Autonomous System is the entire skyscraper. The global
internet is essentially a giant web made up of thousands of these interconnected Autonomous Systems.

---

## How Autonomous Systems Work

Within a single AS, the organization can run whatever internal routing protocols it wants (like OSPF or EIGRP) to move data
between its own buildings and servers. However, when an AS needs to talk to _another_ AS, they must all speak the exact same
external language.

That language is **BGP (Border Gateway Protocol)**.

BGP allows different Autonomous Systems to advertise which IP addresses they own and exchange traffic across the globe. When
you request a video from Netflix, the data travels out of Netflix's AS, hops through one or more transit ISPs' Autonomous
Systems, and finally lands inside your home ISP's Autonomous System.

---

## Autonomous System Numbers (ASNs)

To keep track of all these massive networks, every single AS is assigned a unique identifying number called an **ASN
(Autonomous System Number)**.

- ASNs are assigned by **IANA** (Internet Assigned Numbers Authority) and regional registries like ARIN or RIPE.
- They used to be 16-bit numbers (1 to 65,535), but have expanded to 32-bit numbers to accommodate growth, allowing for over
  4 billion unique ASNs.
- **Examples:** \* Google's primary ASN is `AS15169`.
- Apple uses `AS714`.
- Central networks like Level 3 (a massive internet backbone provider) use `AS3356`.

## The Three Types of Autonomous Systems

Autonomous Systems generally fall into one of three categories based on how they connect to the rest of the world:

### 1. Multihomed AS

A multihomed AS connects to two or more different ISPs. This is done for redundancy; if one ISP goes offline, the company's
network stays connected through the other. However, a multihomed AS **refuses to pass traffic** between those two ISPs. It
only allows traffic that is starting or ending inside its own network.

- _Example:_ A large bank or retail corporation.

### 2. Stub AS

A stub AS is connected to only **one** other Autonomous System. It has only one exit ramp to the rest of the internet, making
its routing incredibly simple.

- _Example:_ A small local ISP or a mid-sized university network.

### 3. Transit AS

A transit AS is a network designed to pass data traffic on behalf of other networks. It acts as the global highway system. It
connects multiple Autonomous Systems together and allows data to travel _through_ it, even if the data didn't start or end
within its borders.

- _Example:_ Tier 1 internet backbone providers (like AT&T, Lumen, or Telia).

---

## Why Do We Need Autonomous Systems?

- **Scalability:** The internet is too big for every single router to know the path to every single computer. By grouping
  millions of devices into a single AS, the rest of the world only needs to know how to route data to the _edge_ of that AS.
- **Administrative Control:** It allows an organization to set its own internal network security policies, choose its own
  hardware, and manage its own infrastructure without affecting the rest of the world.
- **BGP Routing:** It provides a clear, organized map of the global internet, allowing BGP to calculate routes based on "AS
  Hops" rather than counting millions of individual routers.
