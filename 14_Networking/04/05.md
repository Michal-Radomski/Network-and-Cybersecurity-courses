A **network socket** is an internal endpoint used by an operating system to send and receive data between devices on a
network.

Think of a network socket as a **software-defined plug or jack**. Just as a physical electrical plug connects an appliance to
the power grid, a network socket connects a specific application on your device to the vast highway of the internet.

---

## How a Network Socket Works

To establish a connection over the internet, a network socket combines two vital pieces of information:

$$\text{Network Socket} = \text{IP Address} + \text{Port Number}$$

1. **The IP Address (The Apartment Building):** Tells the data which specific device on the global network it needs to go to.
2. **The Port Number (The Apartment Number):** Tells the data which specific application or service _inside_ that device
   should receive it.

> **Example:** If you are browsing a website, your browser might open a socket using the server's IP address (e.g.,
> `192.0.2.1`) and the standard web port (`443` for HTTPS). The resulting socket address looks like this: `192.0.2.1:443`.

---

## The Handshake: Client vs. Server Sockets

For communication to happen, two types of sockets must interact:

- **Server Socket (Listening):** This socket sits open on a specific port, waiting passively for an incoming request (like a
  store clerk waiting for a customer to walk in).
- **Client Socket (Initiating):** This socket is actively opened by your device (like your phone or computer) to reach out
  and connect to the server's listening socket.

Once the connection is accepted, a dedicated communication channel is bound between the two endpoints, allowing data to flow
back and forth.

---

## Common Types of Sockets

Sockets operate based on the type of transport protocol they use:

- **Stream Sockets (TCP):** These use the Transmission Control Protocol. They are highly reliable, connection-oriented, and
  ensure that data arrives in the exact order it was sent without errors. (Used for web browsing, emails, and file
  transfers).
- **Datagram Sockets (UDP):** These use the User Datagram Protocol. They are connectionless and "unreliable," meaning they
  prioritize speed over perfection. Data is sent in packets without checking if they arrive. (Used for online gaming, live
  video streaming, and voice calls).
