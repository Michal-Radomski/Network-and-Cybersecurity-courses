While they sound like the same thing, **bandwidth** and **throughput** measure two very different aspects of network
performance.

The easiest way to understand the difference is to use the **highway analogy**:

- **Bandwidth** is the number of lanes on the highway.
- **Throughput** is the actual number of cars moving past a point on that highway.

---

## 1. What is Bandwidth?

**Bandwidth** is the _maximum potential capacity_ of your network connection. It is the absolute limit of how much data can
theoretically move from one point to another in a given amount of time (usually measured in Megabits per second, or Mbps).

- **The Reality:** When an internet service provider (ISP) sells you a "300 Mbps internet plan," they are selling you 300
  Mbps of _bandwidth_. They are promising that your network pipe is wide enough to handle up to that much data at once.

---

## 2. What is Throughput?

**Throughput** is the _actual amount of data_ that successfully travels through your network over a given period. It tells
you how much data is actually arriving at its destination right now.

- **The Reality:** Even if you have 300 Mbps of bandwidth, if you run a speed test while your sibling is downloading a
  massive game, your laptop's actual _throughput_ might only be 25 Mbps.

---

## Why is Throughput Almost Always Lower Than Bandwidth?

It is rare for throughput to perfectly match your bandwidth limit. Several real-world factors drag throughput down:

- **Network Congestion:** If too many devices are on the same Wi-Fi network or using the same neighborhood ISP hub, data
  packets get backed up.
- **Latency & Distance:** Data takes time to travel. If you are playing a video game hosted on a server across the world,
  your throughput will drop because of the physical distance.
- **Protocol Overhead:** As we learned with PDUs, data has to be wrapped in headers (IP addresses, MAC addresses). These
  headers take up space in your "bandwidth pipe," reducing the space left for the actual data you want.
- **Hardware Limitations:** An old router, a weak Wi-Fi signal through concrete walls, or an outdated network card in your
  laptop can act as a bottleneck.

---

## Summary Comparison

| Feature           | Bandwidth                              | Throughput                                       |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| **Definition**    | Maximum _theoretical_ capacity.        | Actual _real-world_ speed.                       |
| **Analogy**       | The width of the water pipe.           | The actual volume of water flowing through it.   |
| **Controlled By** | Your ISP plan and physical cables.     | Network traffic, router quality, and distance.   |
| **Goal**          | Higher is better (more room for data). | Closer to bandwidth is better (high efficiency). |

Think of it this way: **Bandwidth is what you pay for, while throughput is what you actually get.**
