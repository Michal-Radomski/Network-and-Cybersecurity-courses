In the digital world, data is broken down into the smallest possible pieces of information. The two most fundamental building
blocks of this digital universe are **bits** and **bytes**.

Here is how they work and how they differ.

---

## 1. The Bit (The Smallest Unit)

A **bit** (short for **bi**nary digi**t**) is the absolute smallest unit of data a computer can understand.

Think of a bit like a simple light switch. It can only ever be in one of two states:

- **0** (Off / False)
- **1** (On / True)

Because computers operate using electrical signals, these two states are perfect—0 represents low voltage, and 1 represents
high voltage. Every photo, video, game, and text message you send is ultimately just a massive string of these 0s and 1s.

---

## 2. The Byte (The Building Block)

A **byte** is a collection of **8 bits** grouped together.

While a single bit can't tell you much, grouping 8 of them together into a byte creates enough combinations ($2^8 = 256$
different combinations) to represent useful information, like a single letter of the alphabet, a number, or a punctuation
mark.

> **Example:** When you type the capital letter **"A"** on your keyboard, your computer processes it as a single byte that
> looks like this: `01000001`.

---

## Summary of Differences

| Feature                | Bit                                    | Byte                                       |
| ---------------------- | -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| **What it stands for** | Binary Digit                           | Binary Term (usually)                      |
| **Size**               | 1 single value (0 or 1)                | 8 bits                                     |
| **Abbreviation**       | Lowercase **b** (e.g., 50 Mbps)        | Uppercase **B** (e.g., 50 MB)              |
| **Used to measure...** | Data transfer speeds (internet, Wi-Fi) | Storage capacity (hard drives, file sizes) |

---

### Moving Up the Scale

Because bits and bytes are so tiny, we use prefixes (like kilo-, mega-, and giga-) when talking about real-world files:

- **Kilobyte (KB):** ~1,000 bytes (About a page of plain text)
- **Megabyte (MB):** ~1 million bytes (About 1 minute of a high-quality MP3 song)
- **Gigabyte (GB):** ~1 billion bytes (About 1 hour of an HD streaming video)
