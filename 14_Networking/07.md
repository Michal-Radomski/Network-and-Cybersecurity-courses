At its core, **Ethernet** is the traditional technology used to connect devices in a wired Local Area Network (LAN). It
allows computers, routers, switches, and smart TVs to talk to each other and share data over physical cables.

While Wi-Fi handles wireless connections, Ethernet is the backbone that keeps our wired world plugged in.

---

## How Ethernet Works

Ethernet operates primarily at the physical and data link layers of the network (Layers 1 and 2 of the OSI model). It relies
on a few key components:

### 1. The Hardware

- **Ethernet Cables:** The physical wires that carry the data. The most common are twisted-pair copper cables, often called
  **Cat5e, Cat6, or Cat7** cables.
- **Network Interface Card (NIC):** The port on your computer or router where you plug the Ethernet cable in.
- **Switches and Routers:** The traffic cops that direct the Ethernet "frames" to the correct device using physical **MAC
  addresses**.

### 2. The Protocol (CSMA/CD)

Historically, Ethernet used a system called **CSMA/CD** (Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Detection).

- Think of it like a polite dinner conversation: a device listens to the cable to see if it's quiet. If it is, it speaks
  (sends data).
- If two devices speak at the exact same time (a collision), they both stop, wait a random fraction of a second, and try
  again. Modern switched Ethernet networks have mostly eliminated these collisions, but the fundamental logic remains.

## Ethernet vs. Wi-Fi

People often wonder whether they should use a wired Ethernet cable or stick to Wi-Fi. Here is how they stack up:

| Feature            | Ethernet (Wired)                                                                                    | Wi-Fi (Wireless)                                                                      |
| ------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Speed**          | **Extremely Fast & Consistent.** Speeds can easily reach 1 Gbps, 10 Gbps, or higher.                | **Variable.** Speed drops based on distance, walls, and interference.                 |
| **Latency (Ping)** | **Very Low.** Data travels instantly down the wire, making it perfect for gaming or live streaming. | **Higher.** Wireless signals introduce slight delays.                                 |
| **Security**       | **High.** Someone has to physically plug into your network to intercept data.                       | **Lower.** Radio waves broadcast through walls, requiring strong password encryption. |
| **Convenience**    | **Low.** You are tethered to a cord, and running wires through a house can be messy.                | **High.** You can move freely around your home or office with a phone or laptop.      |

## Ethernet Speed Classes (Categories)

If you look closely at an Ethernet cable, you will see text printed on it showing its "Category" (Cat). This tells you how
much data that specific cable can handle:

- **Cat 5e:** Speeds up to 1 Gbps (Gigabit Ethernet). Standard for older home setups.
- **Cat 6:** Speeds up to 10 Gbps for shorter distances. Great for modern homes and gaming.
- **Cat 6A / Cat 7:** Built for high-frequency data and heavy shielding against interference, used mostly in data centers.
