There are **281,474,976,710,656** (over **281 trillion**) possible unique MAC addresses.

To put that into perspective, that is roughly **35,000 MAC addresses for every single person on Earth**.

Here is how that number is calculated and how they are used.

---

## The Math Behind the Number

A standard MAC address (known as an EUI-48 address) is **48 bits long**. Since each bit can only be a `0` or a `1`,
calculating the total number of unique combinations is a matter of simple exponents:

$$2^{48} = 281,474,976,710,656$$

When you look at a MAC address on a device, it is written in **hexadecimal** format (base-16), using 12 characters ranging
from `0-9` and `A-F` (e.g., `00:1A:2B:3C:4D:5E`). Because each of those 12 slots can hold one of 16 possible characters, the
math can also be written as:

$$16^{12} = 281,474,976,710,656$$

---

## How the Address Space is Split

A MAC address is divided perfectly down the middle into two 24-bit halves:

1. **Organizationally Unique Identifier (OUI):** The first 24 bits (6 hex characters) identify the manufacturer of the
   hardware (like Apple, Intel, or Samsung). There are about **16.7 million** possible manufacturer codes.
2. **Network Interface Identifier:** The last 24 bits are assigned by that specific manufacturer to individual devices as
   they roll off the assembly line. This allows each manufacturer to create **16.7 million** unique devices per OUI.

> **Fun Fact:** If a massive company like Apple runs out of unique numbers for their devices, they simply purchase a
> brand-new OUI prefix from the IEEE (the organization that manages the pool) and start counting from zero again.

---

## Are We Ever Going to Run Out?

It is highly unlikely we will run out anytime soon, for two major reasons:

- **They only need to be locally unique:** Unlike an IP address (which must be globally unique to travel the public
  internet), a MAC address only needs to be unique _inside your local network_ (your home Wi-Fi or office network).
  Manufacturers occasionally reuse old MAC addresses on new devices, knowing it is nearly impossible for two identical MAC
  addresses to end up plugged into the exact same home router.
- **The Backup Plan (EUI-64):** For newer massive technologies like 5G or smart home systems (IoT), networking standards have
  already created a **64-bit MAC address** system. A 64-bit address space ($2^{64}$) allows for **18 quintillion**
  combinations, which is more than enough to give an address to every single grain of sand on the planet.
