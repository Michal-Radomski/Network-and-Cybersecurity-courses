Think of an IP address as a massive apartment building. If every single room in the building is on the exact same floor with
no hallways or signs, finding your friend's room would be a chaotic nightmare.

**Subnetting** is the process of dividing a single large network into smaller, more manageable sub-networks (called
**subnets**). It’s like taking that massive building and splitting it into distinct floors and apartment numbers so traffic
flows efficiently.

---

## Why Do We Subnet?

Before diving into how it works, here is why network administrators do it:

- **Security:** You can isolate sensitive departments (like Finance) from the rest of the company network.
- **Performance:** It reduces "broadcast traffic." Instead of a device shouting a message to every single computer in the
  company, the noise is contained within its own small subnet.
- **Organization:** It makes troubleshooting way easier. If a loop occurs, it only brings down one floor, not the whole
  enterprise.

---

## Anatomy of an IP Address

To understand subnetting, you have to look under the hood of an IPv4 address. Every IP address is made up of **32 bits**
(four groups of 8 bits, called octets) and is divided into two main parts:

1. **Network ID:** Identifies the specific network the device belongs to (like the street name).
2. **Host ID:** Identifies the specific device on that network (like the house number).

### The Subnet Mask: The Decoder Ring

How does a computer know where the Network ID ends and the Host ID begins? It uses a **Subnet Mask**.

A standard subnet mask looks like `255.255.255.0`.

- Wherever you see `255`, that part of the IP address belongs to the **Network**.
- Wherever you see `0`, that part belongs to the **Hosts**.

---

## How Subnetting Actually Works (Borrowing Bits)

When you subnet, you are essentially **stealing bits from the Host ID part and giving them to the Network ID part.**

Let's look at a classic example using **CIDR notation** (Classless Inter-Domain Routing), which uses a forward slash `/` to
show how many bits belong to the network.

> **The Default Setup (`/24`)**
>
> - **IP Address:** `192.168.1.0`
> - **Subnet Mask:** `255.255.255.0` (or `/24` because the first 24 bits are turned "on" as 1s).
> - This gives you **1 single network** that can hold **254 usable devices (hosts)**.

What if you don't want 254 devices on one network? What if you want 2 separate networks? You move the boundary line over by
borrowing 1 bit from the host side, turning your network into a `/25`.

### The Math Behind the Curtain

When you borrow bits, you use basic powers of 2 to figure out your new setup:

- **Number of Subnets created:** $2^n$ (where $n$ is the number of bits borrowed).
- **Number of Hosts per subnet:** $2^h - 2$ (where $h$ is the number of host bits left over).

_Why do we subtract 2?_ Because every subnet reserves the very first address for the **Network Address** (the name of the
subnet) and the very last address for the **Broadcast Address** (used to talk to everyone on that subnet simultaneously).
Devices cannot use these two.

---

## A Quick Real-World Scenario

Imagine you have the network `192.168.1.0 /24`. You decide to borrow **2 bits** to create 4 smaller subnets for different
departments.

Your network bits change from 24 to 26 (a `/26` mask, which is `255.255.255.192`).

Using our formulas:

- **Subnets created:** $2^2 = 4$ subnets.
- **Hosts per subnet:** $2^6 - 2 = 62$ usable devices per subnet.

Your original massive room is now four neatly organized blocks:

1. **Subnet 1:** `192.168.1.0` to `192.168.1.63`
2. **Subnet 2:** `192.168.1.64` to `192.168.1.127`
3. **Subnet 3:** `192.168.1.128` to `192.168.1.191`
4. **Subnet 4:** `192.168.1.192` to `192.168.1.255`

Now, if a computer in Subnet 1 malfunctions and starts flooding the network with data, Subnets 2, 3, and 4 won't feel a
thing.
