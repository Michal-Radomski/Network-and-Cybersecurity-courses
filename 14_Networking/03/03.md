**Nested Subnetting**—more commonly known in the networking world as **VLSM (Variable Length Subnet Masking)**—is the
practice of subnetting a subnet.

In standard subnetting, you chop a network into equal-sized pieces (like cutting a cake into 4 identical quarters). With
nested subnetting, you can take one of those pieces and cut it down even further into smaller bite-sized pieces.

---

## The Problem with Fixed-Size Subnetting

Imagine you are the network administrator for a company with three departments, plus a single point-to-point connection
between two routers:

- **HQ Office:** 100 devices
- **Branch Office:** 50 devices
- **Public Wi-Fi:** 25 devices
- **Router-to-Router Link:** 2 devices

If you use standard subnetting, you have to choose a subnet size that accommodates your _largest_ department (100 devices).
That means you'd have to make four subnets of 126 usable hosts each.

Look at what happens to the router-to-router link: it only needs **2 IP addresses**, but you are forced to give it a subnet
with **126 IP addresses**. You have just wasted 124 IPs!

---

## The Solution: Nested Subnetting (VLSM)

Nested subnetting allows you to use _different_ subnet masks for different parts of your network based on how many devices
actually need to live there. You start with your largest group and work your way down.

Let’s see how we can break down a `192.168.1.0 /24` network cleanly:

### Step 1: Carve out the largest block (HQ Office)

- **Requirement:** 100 devices.
- **The Nest:** You split your network in half using a `/25` mask.
- **Result:** You give **Subnet A** (`192.168.1.0 /25`, which holds 126 hosts) to the HQ Office.
- _Remaining Space:_ You have the other half (`192.168.1.128 /25`) left over.

### Step 2: Nest the next block inside the remaining space (Branch Office)

- **Requirement:** 50 devices.
- **The Nest:** Instead of leaving the remaining space alone, you take `192.168.1.128 /25` and split _it_ in half by
  borrowing another bit, making it a `/26`.
- **Result:** You give **Subnet B** (`192.168.1.128 /26`, which holds 62 hosts) to the Branch Office.
- _Remaining Space:_ You still have `192.168.1.192 /26` left over.

### Step 3: Nest the next block (Public Wi-Fi)

- **Requirement:** 25 devices.
- **The Nest:** Take that leftover `/26` space and split it in half again into `/27` blocks.
- **Result:** You give **Subnet C** (`192.168.1.192 /27`, which holds 30 hosts) to the Public Wi-Fi.
- _Remaining Space:_ You have `192.168.1.224 /27` left over.

### Step 4: Nest the final tiny block (Router Link)

- **Requirement:** 2 devices.
- **The Nest:** You take a piece of your remaining space and chop it all the way down to a `/30` mask (which provides exactly
  2 usable host IPs).
- **Result:** You give **Subnet D** (`192.168.1.224 /30`) to the routers.

---

## Summary of the Final Network

Look at how efficient your network is now. Every department gets exactly what it needs, and masks are tailored to fit:

| Department        | Network Address | Subnet Mask               | Usable Host Range |
| ----------------- | --------------- | ------------------------- | ----------------- |
| **HQ Office**     | `192.168.1.0`   | `255.255.255.128` (`/25`) | `.1` to `.126`    |
| **Branch Office** | `192.168.1.128` | `255.255.255.192` (`/26`) | `.129` to `.190`  |
| **Public Wi-Fi**  | `192.168.1.192` | `255.255.255.224` (`/27`) | `.193` to `.222`  |
| **Router Link**   | `192.168.1.224` | `255.255.255.252` (`/30`) | `.225` to `.226`  |

By "nesting" smaller subnets inside the unused spaces of larger subnets, you prevent IP address waste and keep your routing
tables incredibly clean.

To connect routers in a nested subnetting (VLSM) environment, you need to configure their routing tables so they know how to
pass traffic to subnets they aren't physically plugged into.

Because the subnets are different sizes, routers rely completely on the **Subnet Mask** to determine exactly where to send
each packet.

Here is how you physically and logically connect them using the previous example.

---

## 1. The Physical Connection (The WAN Link)

To connect the HQ Router to the Branch Router, you use a dedicated physical cable (like an Ethernet or fiber cable) plugged
into a specific port on each router (e.g., `GigabitEthernet0/0`).

- This direct link is its own isolated subnet.
- From our previous math, we allocated **`192.168.1.224 /30`** for this link.
- You manually configure the ports on each side:
- **HQ Router Port:** IP `192.168.1.225` | Mask `255.255.255.252`
- **Branch Router Port:** IP `192.168.1.226` | Mask `255.255.255.252`

---

## 2. The Logical Connection (Routing)

Right now, the HQ Router only knows about the subnets physically attached to it (HQ Office and the Router Link). It has **no
idea** that the Branch Office or Public Wi-Fi subnets exist on the other side of the cable.

If a computer in HQ tries to send a packet to the Branch Office, the HQ router will drop it. You have to tell the routers how
to reach those nested subnets using one of two methods:

### Option A: Static Routing (Manual)

You explicitly program the directions into each router. You tell the router: _"If you want to get to Subnet X, look at the
subnet mask to identify it, and throw the packet to the other router's IP."_

- **On the HQ Router, you add these rules:**
- To reach `192.168.1.128` with mask `255.255.255.192` (Branch), send it to `192.168.1.226`.
- To reach `192.168.1.192` with mask `255.255.255.224` (Wi-Fi), send it to `192.168.1.226`.

- **On the Branch Router, you add this rule:**
- To reach `192.168.1.0` with mask `255.255.255.128` (HQ), send it to `192.168.1.225`.

### Option B: Dynamic Routing Protocols (Automatic)

In larger networks, writing static routes is exhausting. Instead, you turn on a routing protocol—like **OSPF (Open Shortest
Path First)** or **EIGRP**—on both routers.

Because we are using nested subnets (VLSM), the routing protocol **must be classless**. Classless routing protocols
automatically include the unique subnet mask (`/25`, `/26`, `/30`) whenever they share network updates with neighboring
routers.

The routers will automatically chat over the link, share their unique masks, and build the paths themselves.

---

## How a Packet Traverses the Network

1. A device in the **HQ Office** (`192.168.1.5`) sends a file to a server in the **Branch Office** (`192.168.1.130`).
2. The HQ router looks at the destination (`192.168.1.130`).
3. It checks its routing table for a matching mask. It sees that `130` falls perfectly into the `192.168.1.128 /26` subnet
   rule.
4. The HQ router fires the packet across the link to `192.168.1.226`.
5. The Branch router receives it, recognizes the IP is on its local Branch subnet, and delivers it to the server.
