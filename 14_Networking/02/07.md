When you look at Cat 5e, Cat 6, and Cat 7 cables, they all use the exact same underlying raw material for the actual
conductors: **pure solid or stranded copper**.

The difference in their performance isn't about using a "different" material altogether, but rather **how much copper is used
(thickness)**, **how the copper wires are twisted**, and **the type of shielding materials** wrapped around those wires.

Here is a breakdown of how these differences affect speed, frequency, and construction.

---

## The Core Differences: Speed & Frequency

As you move up the categories, the cables are engineered to handle tighter frequencies (measured in Megahertz or MHz), which
allows them to carry more data per second over longer distances.

| Cable Category | Max Data Speed      | Max Frequency | Max Distance for Top Speed       |
| -------------- | ------------------- | ------------- | -------------------------------- |
| **Cat 5e**     | 1 Gbps (1,000 Mbps) | 100 MHz       | 100 meters                       |
| **Cat 6**      | 10 Gbps             | 250 MHz       | 37 to 55 meters (100m at 1 Gbps) |
| **Cat 6a**     | 10 Gbps             | 500 MHz       | 100 meters                       |
| **Cat 7**      | 10 Gbps             | 600 MHz       | 100 meters                       |

---

## How the Internal Construction Changes

To handle higher frequencies without the signals bleeding into each other (a problem called **crosstalk**), the physical
design inside the cable changes dramatically.

### 1. The Twists per Inch

An Ethernet cable contains 4 pairs of copper wires. In **Cat 5e**, these wires are twisted relatively loosely. In **Cat 6 and
Cat 7**, the copper pairs are twisted much tighter. Tighter twists naturally cancel out electromagnetic interference from
outside sources and nearby wires.

### 2. The Spline (The Plastic Core)

If you cut open a **Cat 6** cable, you will usually find a longitudinal plastic separator running down the middle (called a
spline). This separates the 4 copper pairs from one another physically to reduce internal crosstalk. Cat 5e cables do not
have this.

### 3. Copper Wire Thickness (AWG)

- **Cat 5e** typically uses thinner **24 AWG** copper wires.
- **Cat 6 and Cat 7** use thicker **23 AWG** copper wires. Thicker copper means less electrical resistance, allowing the
  signal to travel further without degrading.

### 4. Shielding Materials (The Big Cat 7 Difference)

While Cat 5e and Cat 6 are usually unshielded (UTP), **Cat 7 introduces heavy shielding materials** to completely eliminate
interference:

- **Foil and Braiding:** Inside a Cat 7 cable, _each individual pair_ of copper wires is wrapped in aluminum foil. Then, an
  overall braided shield wraps around all four pairs together.
- Because of all this extra metal shielding, Cat 7 cables are much thicker, stiffer, and harder to bend than Cat 5e or Cat 6.

---

## A Note on the RJ45 Connector

While Cat 5e and Cat 6 cables use standard plastic RJ45 connectors, **Cat 7 technically requires a completely different
connector** (called a GG45 or TERA connector) to achieve its full 600 MHz rating.

If you buy a "Cat 7" cable online that has a standard plastic RJ45 clip on the end, it is technically a modified hybrid
cable. The plastic RJ45 connector acts as a performance bottleneck, meaning the cable will essentially behave like a
highly-shielded Cat 6a cable anyway.
