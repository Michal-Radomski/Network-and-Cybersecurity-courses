A password policy is a set of rules designed to enhance computer security by encouraging users to create strong passwords and
then use them correctly. In modern cybersecurity, the focus has shifted from "complexity for the sake of complexity" to
**usability and protection against automated attacks.**

Here is the breakdown of what a modern, robust password policy looks like.

---

## 🏗️ Core Requirements

Most organizations follow standards set by **NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology)**. The current philosophy
emphasizes length over special characters.

- **Minimum Length:** Usually **12 to 16 characters**. Length is the single most important factor in resisting "brute-force"
  attacks.
- **Complexity:** Requiring a mix of uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols.
  > _Note: Modern experts prefer **Passphrases** (e.g., `correct-battery-staple-blue`) because they are easier for humans to
  > remember but harder for computers to guess._
- **Dictionary Check:** The system should automatically reject common passwords (like `Password123`) or words found in a
  dictionary.

---

## 🚫 What’s Outdated (The "Old" Way)

Research has shown that some old habits actually make security **worse**:

- **Frequent Mandatory Resets:** Forcing users to change passwords every 90 days usually leads them to choose predictable
  patterns (e.g., `Summer2025!`, then `Fall2025!`). Modern policies only require a reset if there is **evidence of a
  breach**.
- **Overly Complex Rules:** If the rules are too hard, users will just write their passwords on sticky notes attached to
  their monitors.

---

## 🛡️ Advanced Protective Layers

A password policy is rarely enough on its own. It is almost always paired with these technical controls:

### 1. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

This is the most critical addition. Even if a password is stolen, the attacker cannot gain access without a second factor
(like an authenticator app code, a hardware key, or a biometric scan).

### 2. Rate Limiting & Account Lockouts

To prevent automated "guessing," systems will:

- Slow down login attempts after a few failures (Throttling).
- Temporarily lock the account after 5–10 failed attempts.

### 3. Known Breach Screening

Security-conscious systems check new passwords against databases of "pwned" passwords. If your chosen password has appeared
in a previous data breach elsewhere, the system will force you to pick a different one.

---

## 📋 Sample Policy Comparison

| Feature          | Legacy Policy            | Modern NIST-Style Policy            |
| :--------------- | :----------------------- | :---------------------------------- |
| **Length**       | 8 Characters             | 12+ Characters                      |
| **Rotation**     | Every 90 Days            | Only if compromised                 |
| **Complexity**   | Required symbols/numbers | Passphrases encouraged              |
| **MFA**          | Optional / Rare          | Mandatory for all users             |
| **Common Words** | Allowed                  | Blocklist (e.g., `admin`, `qwerty`) |

---

## 💡 Pro-Tip: Password Managers

The best password policy is one that encourages the use of **Password Managers** (like Bitwarden, 1Password, or KeePass).
This allows users to have unique, 20+ character passwords for every single site without needing to memorize them.
