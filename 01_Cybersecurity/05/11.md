While most cyberattacks target software vulnerabilities, **social engineering** targets "human vulnerabilities." It is the
art of manipulating, influencing, or deceiving someone into giving up confidential information or performing actions that
compromise security.

In short: instead of looking for a software bug to hack into a system, a social engineer hacks the person who has the keys.

---

## Common Social Engineering Techniques

Social engineers rely on psychological triggers like **urgency, fear, curiosity, or authority** to bypass your better
judgment.

### 1. Phishing (The Most Common)

The attacker sends fraudulent communications (usually email) that appear to come from a reputable source.

- **Spear Phishing:** A highly targeted attack aimed at a specific person, using personal details to build trust.
- **Whaling:** Phishing aimed at "big fish," like CEOs or high-level executives.

### 2. Baiting

Just like it sounds, this involves a "trap."

- **Physical:** Leaving a malware-infected USB drive in a public place (like a lobby or parking lot) labeled "Executive
  Salaries."
- **Digital:** Offering a free movie download or "free software" that secretly contains a virus.

### 3. Pretexting

The attacker invents a scenario (a pretext) to confirm a victim's identity or gain information.

- **Example:** Someone calls pretending to be from your HR department or IT support, claiming they need to "verify your
  account details" because of a system glitch.

### 4. Quid Pro Quo

This involves an exchange: "something for something."

- **Example:** An attacker calls random office numbers pretending to be IT support. When they find someone actually having a
  computer problem, they offer to "fix" it in exchange for the person’s login credentials.

### 5. Tailgating (Piggybacking)

This is a physical attack where an unauthorized person follows an authorized employee into a secure area. They might carry a
heavy box or pretend to be on a phone call so that a polite employee holds the door open for them.

---

## The Social Engineering Lifecycle

Most attacks follow a predictable pattern:

1.  **Investigation:** Identifying the victim and gathering background info (often from LinkedIn or social media).
2.  **Hook:** Initiating contact and spinning a story to gain trust.
3.  **The Play:** Executing the attack to get the data or access.
4.  **Exit:** Closing the interaction without raising suspicion.

---

## How to Protect Yourself

Because these attacks target psychology, technical tools like antivirus aren't always enough.

- **Slow Down:** Attackers want you to act fast. If an email or caller creates a sense of extreme urgency, be suspicious.
- **Verify the Source:** If "the bank" calls you, hang up and call the official number on the back of your card.
- **Check the URL:** Before entering a password, ensure the website address is correct and spelled properly (e.g.,
  `micros0ft.com` vs `microsoft.com`).
- **Set Your Social Media to Private:** The less information an attacker can find about your job, friends, and hobbies, the
  harder it is for them to build a "hook."
