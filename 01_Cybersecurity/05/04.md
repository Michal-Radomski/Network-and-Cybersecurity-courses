Cryptographic attacks are generally divided into two categories: **Cryptanalysis**, which targets the mathematical flaws in
the algorithms, and **Implementation Attacks**, which target how the software or hardware is actually built.

Here are the primary methods used to break cryptographic systems:

### 1. Cryptanalysis (Mathematical Attacks)

These attacks vary based on how much information the attacker already has.

- **Ciphertext-only Attack (COA):** The attacker has only the encrypted data. This is the hardest method and usually relies
  on statistical analysis (like frequency analysis in simple ciphers).
- **Known-plaintext Attack (KPA):** The attacker has access to a piece of the original message and its encrypted version.
  > **Historical Note:** During WWII, Bletchley Park used "cribs"—common phrases like weather reports—to crack the Enigma
  > machine because they knew exactly what the plaintext was for certain encrypted segments.
- **Chosen-plaintext Attack (CPA):** The attacker can choose any text they want and see how the system encrypts it. By
  analyzing the output of specific patterns (like a string of all "A"s), they can deduce the encryption key.
- **Chosen-ciphertext Attack (CCA):** The attacker provides encrypted data to the system and observes the decrypted result.
  This is often used to exploit the "error messages" a system sends back, which might reveal if the data was formatted
  correctly.

---

### 2. Brute-Force and Search Attacks

These don't look for mathematical flaws; they just try every possible combination until one works.

- **Brute-Force:** Trying every single possible key. The security of modern encryption (like AES-256) relies on the fact that
  a brute-force attack would take longer than the current age of the universe to complete.
- **Dictionary Attack:** Instead of trying every combination, the attacker uses a list of common passwords, phrases, or
  words.
- **Rainbow Table Attack:** A specialized attack for **hashes**. Attackers use massive precomputed tables of
  "password-to-hash" values to look up a stolen hash instantly rather than calculating it on the fly.

---

### 3. Side-Channel Attacks

These are the "sneaky" attacks. They don't attack the math at all—they watch the physical computer while it's doing the math.

- **Timing Attack:** Measuring exactly how many milliseconds it takes for a computer to decrypt a file. If the computer takes
  longer to process certain bits of a key, the attacker can guess those bits.
- **Power Analysis:** Measuring the tiny fluctuations in electrical power a CPU consumes. Complex mathematical operations use
  different amounts of power than simple ones, leaking the key.
- **Acoustic/EM Attacks:** "Listening" to the high-pitched sounds of capacitors or measuring the electromagnetic radiation
  emitted by a device to reconstruct the data being processed.

---

### 4. Integrity and Protocol Attacks

These attack the _process_ of communication rather than the encryption itself.

- **Man-in-the-Middle (MITM):** The attacker sits between two people, pretending to be each one to the other. They intercept
  the "key exchange" so they have their own secret connection with both parties.
- **Replay Attack:** An attacker captures a valid encrypted message (like a command to "Open Garage Door") and sends it again
  later. Even if they can't decrypt it, the system might see a "valid" command and open the door.
