In cybersecurity, **Cryptography** is the science of protecting information by transforming it into an unreadable format. It
is the "engine" that powers almost every security protocol we use today, from browsing the web to swiping a credit card.

As of 2026, cryptography has entered a pivotal era, shifting from "classical" methods to "quantum-resistant" standards to
protect against the rising power of quantum computers.

---

## 1. The Four Pillars of Cryptography

Modern cryptography does much more than just hide secrets. It fulfills four critical security goals, often referred to as
**PAIN**:

- **P**rivacy (Confidentiality): Ensures only the intended recipient can read the data.
- **A**uthentication: Verifies that the sender is who they claim to be.
- **I**ntegrity: Ensures the data hasn't been tampered with during transit.
- **N**on-repudiation: Prevents a sender from later denying they sent the message.

---

## 2. Core Cryptographic Methods

### Symmetric Encryption (Secret Key)

Uses a **single key** for both encryption and decryption. It is incredibly fast and ideal for bulk data (like encrypting a
hard drive).

- **Common Algorithm:** **AES-256** (Advanced Encryption Standard) remains the global "gold standard."
- **Analogy:** A physical safe where you use the same key to lock it and unlock it.

### Asymmetric Encryption (Public Key)

Uses a **pair of keys**: a **Public Key** (which anyone can see) and a **Private Key** (which only you keep). If someone
encrypts data with your Public Key, only your Private Key can unlock it.

- **Common Algorithms:** RSA, ECC (Elliptic Curve Cryptography).
- **Analogy:** A mailbox where anyone can drop a letter (Public Key), but only the owner has the key to open the box and read
  them (Private Key).

### Hashing

A one-way mathematical function that turns any amount of data into a fixed-length string of characters (a "fingerprint"). You
cannot reverse a hash to see the original data.

- **Purpose:** To verify **Integrity**. If even one bit of the original file changes, the hash changes completely.
- **Common Algorithm:** **SHA-256**.

## 3. The 2026 Landscape: Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC)

We are currently in the middle of a massive global migration. Traditional asymmetric algorithms like RSA are vulnerable to
**Shor's Algorithm**, which a powerful quantum computer could use to "crack" them in minutes.

- **NIST Standards:** In late 2024 and throughout 2025, NIST finalized the first official Post-Quantum standards (**ML-KEM**,
  **ML-DSA**, and **SLH-DSA**).
- **Hybrid Architecture:** In 2026, most secure systems (like updated versions of TLS for web browsing) use a **Hybrid
  Approach**. They combine a classical key (like ECC) with a quantum-resistant key. This ensures security even if the new
  quantum-resistant math is later found to have a flaw.
- **Harvest Now, Decrypt Later:** This is a major 2026 threat where hackers steal encrypted data today, hoping to decrypt it
  once they have a quantum computer in a few years.

---

## 4. Real-World Applications

- **SSL/TLS (HTTPS):** When you see the padlock icon in your browser, your computer and the server are using asymmetric
  encryption to exchange a symmetric key for a fast, private session.
- **Digital Signatures:** Used in software updates and legal documents to prove the file came from a trusted source and
  hasn't been altered.
- **End-to-End Encryption (E2EE):** Used in apps like Signal or WhatsApp, ensuring that not even the service provider can
  read your messages.
