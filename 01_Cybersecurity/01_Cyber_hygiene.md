Think of **cyber hygiene** exactly like personal hygiene. Just like brushing your teeth or washing your hands prevents
illness, practicing good digital habits keeps your devices healthy and your personal data safe from hackers, malware, and
identity theft.

Here is a breakdown of the core cyber hygiene essentials you should build into your daily routine.

---

## 1. Bulletproof Password Management

Passwords are your first line of defense, but standard ones are easily cracked.

- **The Blueprint:** Use passwords or passphrases that are at least 12 characters long, combining uppercase and lowercase
  letters, numbers, and symbols.
- **The Golden Rule:** **Never reuse passwords.** If a hacker breaches one minor website where you reused a password, they
  now have the keys to your email, social media, and banking apps.
- **The Fix:** Use a reputable **password manager** (like Bitwarden or 1Password) to generate and securely store complex
  passwords so you don't have to memorize them.

## 2. Activate Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA / 2FA)

MFA adds an extra layer of defense. Even if a cybercriminal successfully steals your password, they still cannot access your
account without a second form of verification.

- Whenever available, turn on MFA for your accounts.
- **Pro-Tip:** Lean toward using **authenticator apps** (like Google Authenticator or Microsoft Authenticator) or security
  keys rather than SMS/text message codes, as text messages can sometimes be intercepted via "SIM-swapping" scams.

## 3. Keep Software and Operating Systems Updated

Software developers constantly find security vulnerabilities in their systems. When they patch these flaws, they release them
via software updates. If you hit "Remind me later," you leave a backdoor wide open for hackers.

- Turn on **automatic updates** for your smartphone, computer operating system (Windows, macOS), and web browsers.
- Don't forget to update your apps and your home Wi-Fi router's firmware.

## 4. Think Before You Click (Phishing Awareness)

Phishing is when cybercriminals trick you into giving away sensitive details or downloading malware by pretending to be a
trusted source (like your bank, a delivery service, or a streaming platform).

- Be suspicious of urgent, high-pressure emails, texts, or DMs asking you to click a link or download an attachment.
- Always check the actual sender's email address (not just the display name) to ensure it's legitimate. When in doubt, look
  up the company's official website separately instead of clicking the link provided.

## 5. Use Reliable Security Software

While built-in security has improved significantly, having specialized defense layers is crucial.

- Ensure your device's native **firewall** is turned on.
- Keep a reputable antivirus and anti-malware scanner running in the background to catch and isolate malicious files before
  they can execute.

## 6. Back Up Your Data Regularly

Imagine if your device suddenly broke, was stolen, or got hit with ransomware (malware that locks your files until you pay a
fee). Regular backups ensure you never lose your irreplaceable photos, schoolwork, or documents.

- Follow the **3-2-1 backup strategy**: Keep **3** copies of your data, on **2** different types of media (e.g., your
  computer and an external hard drive), with **1** copy stored securely offsite or in the cloud.

## 7. Secure Your Network and Device Access

Your physical device and local network need boundaries just as much as your online accounts.

- **Lock your screens:** Always protect your phone and laptop with a strong PIN, password, or biometric login
  (FaceID/Fingerprint).
- **Home Wi-Fi:** Change the default admin username and password on your home internet router, and ensure your Wi-Fi is using
  strong encryption (WPA2 or WPA3).
- **Public Wi-Fi Danger:** Avoid logging into banking apps or typing passwords while connected to unsecured public Wi-Fi
  networks (like at a coffee shop or airport). If you must use them, use a **VPN (Virtual Private Network)** to encrypt your
  traffic.
