**IAAA—Identification, Authentication, Authorization, and Accountability—extends access control beyond the CIA triad by
managing who accesses systems and how their actions are tracked.** It forms a sequential framework for secure identity and
access management (IAM), ensuring only verified users perform approved actions with full audit trails.
[cyberguidesolutions](https://cyberguidesolutions.com/identification-authentication-authorization-and-accountability/)

## Identification

This initial step claims a user's identity using unique attributes like usernames, IDs, or email addresses. It precedes
verification but doesn't prove legitimacy on its own—for instance, entering "admin" at login. Weak identification risks
impersonation if not paired with stronger checks.
[testkarts](https://www.testkarts.com/blog/post/identification-authentication-authorization-accountability-with-example)

## Authentication

Here, users prove their claimed identity via credentials such as passwords, biometrics, tokens, or multi-factor methods. It
validates "you are who you say," blocking unauthorized entry like failed login attempts. Modern systems favor MFA to counter
threats like phishing. [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yVgkeQJOozY)

## Authorization

Once authenticated, this determines what resources or actions the user can access, enforcing least privilege—e.g., a viewer
role sees data but can't edit it. Policies like role-based access control (RBAC) implement this, preventing overreach.
[mayurpahwa](https://mayurpahwa.com/blog/cybersecurity/identification-authentication-authorization-and-accountability?gi=58a789bd61039f301f1b2f2232237500841ae4e9)

## Accountability

Final logs track all actions to specific users, including timestamps, resources, and outcomes, enabling audits and incident
forensics. Without it, breaches go untraced; tools like SIEM systems enforce this. It ties back to CIA by supporting
integrity and confidentiality through traceability. [youtube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lsAiGuIE4Ro)
