Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) is a method of regulating access to system resources based on the roles users hold within an
organization, implementing the authorization step of the IAAA model by assigning permissions to roles rather than
individuals.
[mayurpahwa](https://mayurpahwa.com/blog/cybersecurity/identification-authentication-authorization-and-accountability?gi=58a789bd61039f301f1b2f2232237500841ae4e9)

## Core Components

RBAC revolves around users, roles, permissions, and resources (like files or databases). Roles represent job functions—such
as "manager" or "editor"—that bundle specific permissions, like read, write, or delete. Users inherit these permissions
through role assignments, enabling scalable management in large environments.
[fortinet](https://www.fortinet.com/resources/cyberglossary/role-based-access-control)

## How It Works

When a user requests access, the system first verifies their role, then checks if that role permits the action on the
resource, enforcing least privilege to minimize risks. For example, an HR manager role might allow updating employee records,
while a viewer role only permits reading them. [pathlock](https://pathlock.com/blog/role-based-access-control-rbac/)

## Key Benefits

RBAC simplifies administration by updating roles instead of individual permissions during staff changes, supports separation
of duties to prevent fraud, and aligns with standards like NIST for compliance. It outperforms discretionary models by
centralizing control, though it may need extensions like hierarchical RBAC for complex inheritance.
