Risk management in cybersecurity involves systematically identifying, assessing, prioritizing, and mitigating threats to
protect systems, data, and operations from potential harm. It acknowledges that risks can't be fully eliminated, so
organizations focus on those with the highest impact, often tying into frameworks like the CIA triad and access controls such
as RBAC or ABAC discussed earlier. [imperva](https://www.imperva.com/learn/data-security/cybersecurity-risk-management/)

## Core Process

The standard process follows four stages: identify risks by scanning assets and threats (e.g., vulnerabilities or malware);
assess likelihood and impact qualitatively (high/medium/low) or quantitatively (e.g., expected monetary loss); mitigate via
controls like firewalls, encryption, or policies; and monitor/review continuously to adapt to new threats. Frameworks like
NIST RMF guide this with steps including categorization, control selection, implementation, and ongoing monitoring.
[sailpoint](https://www.sailpoint.com/identity-library/cyber-risk-management)

## Risk Strategies

Organizations respond by avoiding risks (e.g., discontinuing unsafe practices), mitigating them (deploying patches and
training), transferring via insurance or outsourcing, or accepting low-level ones within tolerance. For instance, a
ransomware risk might be mitigated with backups and RBAC to limit access.
[hyperproof](https://hyperproof.io/resource/cybersecurity-risk-management-process/)
