Multi-factor authentication (MFA) enhances security by requiring multiple verification methods to confirm a user's identity,
building directly on the authentication step of the IAAA model discussed earlier.
[rsa](https://www.rsa.com/resources/blog/multi-factor-authentication/what-is-mfa/)

## How MFA Works

Users provide two or more factors during login: typically a password (something you know), plus a second like a texted code,
app-generated token (something you have), or fingerprint (something you are). This layered approach blocks attackers who
compromise one factor, such as via phishing.
[fortinet](https://www.fortinet.com/resources/cyberglossary/multi-factor-authentication)

## Common Factors

- **Knowledge**: Passwords, PINs, or security questions.
- **Possession**: SMS codes, authenticator apps, or hardware keys.
- **Inherence**: Biometrics like face ID or voice recognition.
  [staysafeonline](https://www.staysafeonline.org/articles/multi-factor-authentication)

## Benefits and Adoption

MFA significantly reduces unauthorized access risks, making it a standard in enterprise systems and personal accounts. While
slightly more effort for users, it vastly outperforms passwords alone.
[huntress](https://www.huntress.com/blog/what-is-multi-factor-authentication)
