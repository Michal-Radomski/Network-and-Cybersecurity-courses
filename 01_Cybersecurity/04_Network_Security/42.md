In cybersecurity and data management, **RAID** stands for **Redundant Array of Independent Disks**. It is a virtualization
technology that combines multiple physical disk drive components into one or more logical units for the purposes of **data
redundancy**, **performance improvement**, or both.

From a security perspective, RAID is a cornerstone of the **Availability** pillar in the CIA Triad (Confidentiality,
Integrity, and Availability). It ensures that a hardware failure doesn't result in a total system blackout or permanent data
loss.

---

## Core Concepts of RAID

RAID works through three primary mechanisms:

1.  **Mirroring:** Copying data to more than one disk to ensure that if one fails, the other has a complete copy.
2.  **Striping:** Spreading data across multiple disks to increase speed (read/write operations happen in parallel).
3.  **Parity:** Using mathematical calculations to rebuild lost data if a drive fails.

---

## Common RAID Levels

Different "levels" offer different balances of speed, capacity, and security.

| RAID Level  | Strategy             | Minimum Drives | Cybersecurity Benefit                                                                                     |
| :---------- | :------------------- | :------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **RAID 0**  | Striping             | 2              | **None.** High performance, but if one disk fails, all data is lost.                                      |
| **RAID 1**  | Mirroring            | 2              | **High Availability.** Total redundancy. If Disk A dies, Disk B keeps the system running.                 |
| **RAID 5**  | Striping + Parity    | 3              | **Resilience.** Can survive the failure of one drive without data loss. Good balance of speed and safety. |
| **RAID 6**  | Double Parity        | 4              | **High Resilience.** Can survive the simultaneous failure of two drives.                                  |
| **RAID 10** | Mirroring + Striping | 4              | **Best of Both.** Combines the speed of RAID 0 with the redundancy of RAID 1.                             |

## Why RAID Matters for Cybersecurity

While RAID is often handled by IT infrastructure teams, it is vital for security professionals for several reasons:

### 1. Business Continuity

Cybersecurity isn't just about stopping hackers; it’s about keeping the lights on. RAID prevents "Denial of Service" caused
by simple hardware wear-and-tear. If a database drive fails during peak hours, RAID 1 or 5 ensures the service stays online
while the drive is replaced.

### 2. Data Integrity

Advanced RAID levels (like RAID 6) help protect against bit rot or write errors. By comparing parity data, the system can
sometimes detect and correct minor data corruptions before they compromise a file.

### 3. Protection Against "Physical" Data Loss

In the event of a physical disk crash—which can happen due to age, heat, or electrical surges—RAID provides the immediate
"failover" necessary to prevent permanent data deletion.

---

> ### ⚠️ The Golden Rule: RAID is NOT a Backup
>
> A common misconception is that because RAID 1 mirrors data, you don't need backups. This is false.
>
> - **RAID protects against hardware failure.**
> - **Backups protect against data loss.**
>
> If a user accidentally deletes a file or a ransomware strain encrypts your data, that change is mirrored **instantly**
> across all disks in a RAID array. RAID will faithfully preserve your corrupted or deleted files. Always maintain separate,
> off-site, or immutable backups.

---

## Implementation: Hardware vs. Software

- **Hardware RAID:** Uses a dedicated controller card. It is faster because it doesn't tax the main CPU and is generally more
  reliable for enterprise-grade security.
- **Software RAID:** Managed by the Operating System. It’s cheaper and easier to set up but can be a bottleneck for system
  performance under heavy loads.
