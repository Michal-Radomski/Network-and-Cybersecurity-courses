A firewall is a security barrier that controls network traffic, while a DMZ (Demilitarized Zone) is a specific subnetwork
that acts as a "buffer zone" between your private internal network and the public internet.

## 1. Firewall: The Gatekeeper

A firewall sits between two networks and inspects every "packet" of data trying to pass through. It decides whether to allow
or block traffic based on a set of security rules.

- Packet Filtering: Checks the source IP, destination IP, and port number (e.g., blocking everything except web traffic on
  Port 80).
- Stateful Inspection: Remembers active connections. If you request a website, the firewall knows the returning data is part
  of your request and lets it in.
- Hardware vs. Software: You can have a physical appliance (like a Cisco or Palo Alto firewall) or software running on an OS
  (like ufw or iptables on Ubuntu).

## 2. DMZ (Demilitarized Zone): The Buffer

A DMZ is a small, isolated network created to host services that must be accessible from the internet (like a web server,
email server, or a jump box).

- Isolation: If a hacker successfully breaks into a server inside the DMZ, they are still trapped behind a second firewall.
  They cannot easily "jump" into your private internal network where your sensitive data and personal computers are stored.
- The "Sandwich" Setup: A common design uses two firewalls:

1. External Firewall: Sits between the Internet and the DMZ. 2. Internal Firewall: Sits between the DMZ and the Internal
   Trusted Network.

## How They Work Together

Imagine a castle (your internal network):

- The Firewall is the drawbridge and the guards at the gate.
- The DMZ is the courtyard outside the main keep. You let traders (the public) into the courtyard to do business, but they
  aren't allowed inside the actual castle where the gold (data) is kept.
