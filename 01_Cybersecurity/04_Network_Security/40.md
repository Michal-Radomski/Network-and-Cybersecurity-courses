In cybersecurity, electricity is the most fundamental layer of the "stack." We often focus on software, but if the power
fails or is manipulated, every other security control—firewalls, encryption, and databases—instantly becomes irrelevant.

In the industry, we look at electricity through three lenses: **Availability**, **Physical Security**, and **Signal
Intelligence**.

---

## 1. Availability: The "A" in the CIA Triad

The **CIA Triad** (Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability) is the foundation of security. If a hacker can't steal your
data, they might just try to shut you down.

- **UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply):** These are essentially giant batteries that kick in the millisecond the main power
  fails. They give servers enough time to shut down gracefully without corrupting data.
- **Backup Generators:** For data centers, these are the second line of defense. They can keep a facility running for days
  during a total grid collapse.
- **Redundant Power Feeds:** High-security facilities often pull electricity from two different "substations" on the power
  grid. If a transformer blows on one side of town, the other side keeps the lights on.

---

## 2. Power as an Attack Vector (Side-Channel Attacks)

This is where cybersecurity gets "James Bond" levels of technical. Attackers can sometimes steal data just by measuring how
much electricity a computer is using.

- **Power Analysis:** When a CPU performs a complex task, like decrypting a file, it draws more power. By monitoring these
  tiny fluctuations in electricity, a sophisticated attacker can actually "reverse engineer" the encryption key.
- **EMI (Electromagnetic Interference):** Every electrical wire gives off a tiny radio signal. Attackers can use specialized
  antennas to "listen" to the electrical hum of a monitor or keyboard to see what is being typed. This is known as
  **TEMPEST** in government circles.

---

## 3. Protecting the Power Grid (ICS/SCADA)

Modern power plants and electrical grids are controlled by computers known as **Industrial Control Systems (ICS)**.

- **The Threat:** If a hacker gains access to the "Control Room" of a power utility, they can physically destroy equipment by
  rapidly opening and closing circuit breakers or overloading transformers.
- **Historical Example:** The 2015 Ukraine power grid hack, where attackers used stolen credentials to remotely open
  breakers, leaving 230,000 people in the dark.

---

## 4. Hardware "Juice Jacking"

At a consumer level, electricity and data often travel over the same cable (like USB).

- **The Risk:** Public charging stations (like at airports) can be modified by hackers. When you plug in your phone to get
  "juice" (electricity), the hidden hardware in the port attempts to "jack" (steal) your data.
- **The Fix:** Use a **"USB Data Blocker"** (also called a "USB Condom"). It’s a small adapter that physically cuts the data
  pins in the cable while allowing the power pins to connect.

---

## Summary: The Physical Hardening Checklist

If you are securing a high-value system, you must "harden" the electricity:

- **Surge Protection:** To prevent hardware fry-outs from lightning or grid spikes.
- **Line Conditioning:** To ensure the "noise" in the electricity doesn't interfere with sensitive hardware.
- **Physical Isolation:** Ensuring power cables are buried or in locked conduits so they can't be cut or tapped.
