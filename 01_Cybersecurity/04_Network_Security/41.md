In cybersecurity, **backups** are your "ultimate undo button." While firewalls and antivirus tools are designed to prevent
attacks, a backup is what saves you when those defenses fail.

If a hacker encrypts your data with ransomware or a server's hardware physically dies, a backup ensures that your business or
personal life doesn't come to a grinding halt.

---

## 1. The "Golden Rule": The 3-2-1 Strategy

The industry standard for backups is the **3-2-1 Rule**. If you don't follow this, you don't truly have a backup; you just
have a "copy" that is equally vulnerable.

- **3 Copies of Data:** The original plus two backups.
- **2 Different Media:** Store them on different types of hardware (e.g., one on an external hard drive, one on a cloud
  server).
- **1 Off-site:** At least one copy must be in a different physical location (e.g., the cloud or a different building) to
  protect against fire or theft.

---

## 2. Types of Backups

You don't always want to back up every single file every single day because it takes too much time and storage. Instead, we
use different "strategies":

| Type             | Description                                           | Pros                             | Cons                                                     |
| :--------------- | :---------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Full Backup**  | Copies everything on the system.                      | Easiest to restore from.         | Slow and uses lots of storage.                           |
| **Incremental**  | Only copies what has changed since the _last_ backup. | Very fast to create.             | Slowest to restore (you need every "link" in the chain). |
| **Differential** | Copies what has changed since the last _Full_ backup. | Faster restore than incremental. | Grows larger every day until the next full backup.       |

---

## 3. Immutability: The Modern Essential

Modern hackers are smart—when they break into a network, the first thing they try to do is **delete your backups** so you
_have_ to pay the ransom.

**Immutable Backups** are backups that are "locked." Once the data is written, it cannot be changed, deleted, or encrypted
for a set period (e.g., 30 days). Even if a hacker gets "Admin" access to your server, they cannot touch the immutable
backup.

---

## 4. RTO and RPO (The "Success" Metrics)

In a professional setting, a backup is only as good as the speed at which it can be used. Admins use two metrics to measure
this:

- **RPO (Recovery Point Objective):** How much data can you afford to lose? If you back up once a day, your RPO is 24 hours.
- **RTO (Recovery Time Objective):** How long does it take to get back online? If it takes 5 hours to download your backup,
  your RTO is 5 hours.

---

## 5. Offline Backups (Air-Gapping)

An **"Air-Gapped"** backup is one that is physically disconnected from any network.

- **Why?** If a virus is spreading through your Wi-Fi, it cannot reach a hard drive sitting in a drawer.
- **Action:** For critical data, keep an external drive that you only plug in during the backup process and unplug
  immediately after.

---

## The "Restore" Trap

The biggest mistake people make in cybersecurity is **backing up but never testing.** A backup is useless if the file is
corrupted or the password to the backup is lost.

> **Pro Tip:** A backup isn't a backup until you have successfully performed a "test restore" to see if the files actually
> open.
