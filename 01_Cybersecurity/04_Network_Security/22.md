A bot is a single automated software program or script designed to perform repetitive tasks, while a botnet (a portmanteau of
"robot" and "network") is a large collection of these bots working together under a single controller. [1, 2, 3, 4]

## The Bot (The Individual Unit)

In a cybersecurity context, a "bad bot" is a piece of malware that infects a device (computer, smartphone, or IoT device),
turning it into a "zombie". [5, 6, 7]

- Automation: Bots can perform tasks much faster and more accurately than humans, such as guessing passwords or scraping
  data.
- Dual Nature: Not all bots are malicious. "Good bots" include search engine crawlers (like Googlebot) and customer service
  chatbots.
- Silent Operation: Malicious bots typically run in the background without the device owner's knowledge. [1, 3, 8, 9, 10]

## The Botnet (The Networked Army)

A botnet is an army of these infected "zombies" controlled by a single party known as a bot-herder or botmaster. [8, 11, 12]

- Scale: Botnets can range from a few hundred to millions of infected devices worldwide.
- Command and Control (C2): The botmaster manages the network through a C2 server, which sends instructions to all bots
  simultaneously.
- Large-Scale Attacks: By combining the computing power and bandwidth of thousands of devices, botnets can launch massive
  Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) attacks that can crash major websites.
- Other Uses: They are also used for mass spam/phishing campaigns, credential stuffing (brute-forcing logins), and illegal
  cryptocurrency mining. [1, 2, 3, 10, 12, 13, 14, 15]
