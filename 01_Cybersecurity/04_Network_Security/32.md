In simple terms, a **proxy server** is an intermediary between your device and the internet. When you use a proxy, your
internet traffic flows through the proxy server on its way to the address you requested. The request then comes back through
that same proxy server, which then forwards the data to you.

---

### How a Proxy Works

Think of a proxy as a **middleman** or a "personal shopper." Instead of you going into a store (a website) yourself, you give
your list to the proxy. The proxy goes into the store, buys the items, and brings them back to you. The store never sees your
face; it only sees the proxy.

**Key functions of a proxy include:**

- **Anonymity:** It masks your IP address, making it appear as though the traffic is coming from the proxy's location.
- **Content Filtering:** Organizations use proxies to block access to specific websites (like social media or adult content).
- **Caching:** Proxies can save copies of frequently visited websites to speed up loading times for others on the same
  network.
- **Bypassing Geo-restrictions:** By using a proxy in another country, you can access content that might be blocked in your
  actual location.

---

### Can a Proxy Act as a Firewall?

**Yes, but with limitations.** While a proxy and a firewall are different tools, their roles often overlap. A proxy can act
as a **basic firewall** by sitting between your network and the internet and deciding which traffic is allowed to pass.

#### Where they overlap:

- **Access Control:** Both can block specific IP addresses or domain names.
- **Traffic Filtering:** A "Proxy Firewall" (also known as an Application-Level Gateway) inspects traffic at the
  **Application Layer** (Layer 7). It doesn't just look at where the data is coming from; it looks at the data itself to
  ensure it's safe before passing it to the user.

#### Where they differ:

- **Scope:** A traditional firewall blocks ports and protocols to protect the entire network. A proxy usually focuses on
  specific types of traffic, like web (HTTP) or mail (SMTP).
- **Performance:** Because a proxy has to "act" as the destination and re-establish connections for every request, it is
  generally slower than a dedicated network firewall.

---

### The Verdict

In modern cybersecurity, the line is blurry. Most **Next-Generation Firewalls (NGFWs)** actually include proxy capabilities.

If you are a casual user, a proxy is great for privacy and bypassing blocks. If you are a business, you wouldn't rely on a
proxy _instead_ of a firewall; you would use them together—or use a firewall that has a built-in proxy—to create a
"defense-in-depth" strategy.
