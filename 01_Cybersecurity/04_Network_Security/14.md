**LAN topologies** describe how devices in a **Local Area Network (LAN)** are physically or logically arranged and connected.

### 🔷 Main types of LAN topologies

#### ⭐ Bus topology

- All devices share a **single central cable (backbone)**
- Data travels in both directions

**Pros:** simple, cheap **Cons:** if the main cable fails → entire network goes down

---

#### ⭐ Star topology

- All devices connect to a **central device** (switch or hub)

**Pros:**

- Easy to manage
- Failure of one device doesn’t affect others

**Cons:**

- If the central device fails → network stops

👉 Most modern LANs use this topology

---

#### ⭐ Ring topology

- Devices are connected in a **closed loop (circle)**
- Data travels in one direction (or both in advanced setups)

**Pros:** predictable performance **Cons:** one break can disrupt the whole network

---

#### ⭐ Mesh topology

- Devices are connected to **multiple other devices**

**Pros:**

- Very reliable (multiple paths)
- Fault-tolerant

**Cons:**

- Expensive and complex

---

#### ⭐ Tree topology

- Combination of **star + bus**
- Organized in hierarchical structure (like branches of a tree)

**Pros:** scalable, structured **Cons:** backbone issues affect many devices

---

### 🧠 Simple summary

- **Bus** → one shared cable
- **Star** → everything connected to a center
- **Ring** → circular connection
- **Mesh** → many interconnections
- **Tree** → hierarchical structure

👉 In short: **Topology defines how devices are connected and how data flows in a LAN.**
