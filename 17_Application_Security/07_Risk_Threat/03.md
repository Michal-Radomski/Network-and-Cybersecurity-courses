Both **DREAD** and **MITRE ATT&CK** are essential frameworks in cybersecurity, but they serve entirely different purposes.

Think of **DREAD** as a qualitative calculator used to grade how dangerous a specific bug is, while **MITRE ATT&CK** is a
massive, real-world encyclopedia of how actual hackers behave.

Here is how they break down and how they work together.

---

## 1. DREAD: The Risk Rating Framework

DREAD is a legacy risk-scoring model originally popularized by Microsoft. It helps teams calculate a mathematical score for a
threat by evaluating five distinct categories.

Each category is rated on a scale from **1 (Low)** to **3 (High)**, and the final risk score is the average of all five.

$$\text{Risk Score} = \frac{D + R + E + A + D}{5}$$

### The DREAD Breakdown:

- **D - Damage Potential:** If the attack succeeds, how bad is the damage?
- _1 = Low impact; 3 = Complete destruction of data or system take-over._

- **R - Reproducibility:** How easy is it for an attacker to repeat this attack?
- _1 = Hard/rarely works; 3 = Works every single time with no effort._

- **E - Exploitability:** How much effort, skill, or money is required to pull this off?
- _1 = Requires an advanced nation-state hacker; 3 = A novice can do it with a free internet tool._

- **A - Affected Users:** If this happens, how many people or systems are impacted?
- _1 = Only one user; 3 = Every single user on the platform._

- **D - Discoverability:** How easy is it for a hacker to find this vulnerability in the first place?
- _1 = Hidden deep in the system; 3 = Glaringly obvious on a public-facing page._

> ⚠️ **Note:** While DREAD is highly intuitive, many modern organizations have transitioned to **CVSS** (Common Vulnerability
> Scoring System) because "Discoverability" in DREAD can be highly subjective.

---

## 2. MITRE ATT&CK: The Hacker Playbook

**MITRE ATT&CK** (Adversarial Tactics, Techniques, and Common Knowledge) is a globally accessible, living database of
real-world hacker behaviors. Instead of guessing what a hacker _might_ do, ATT&CK documents exactly what they _have done_ in
actual cyberattacks.

It organizes this data into a matrix structured by **Tactics** and **Techniques**.

### Tactics vs. Techniques

- **Tactics (The "Why"):** The attacker's immediate goal. (e.g., _Initial Access_—how they get into your network;
  _Exfiltration_—how they steal your data).
- **Techniques (The "How"):** The specific action they take to achieve that goal. (e.g., To achieve _Initial Access_, they
  might use a _Phishing_ technique).

### How Teams Use MITRE ATT&CK:

- **Defenders (Blue Teams):** They look at the matrix to build defenses. If the matrix shows hackers frequently use "Brute
  Force" to get inside a system, the defenders know they need to implement multi-factor authentication (MFA).
- **Attackers/Testers (Red Teams):** They use it as a checklist to simulate realistic cyberattacks against a company to see
  if the security team catches them.
- **Threat Intelligence:** It provides a common language. If a security firm says, _"We are tracking a group using Technique
  T1566 (Phishing),"_ everyone in the world knows exactly what that means.

## How They Compare & Work Together

While they are different tools, they complement each other perfectly during a threat modeling or application security
exercise.

| Feature             | DREAD                                     | MITRE ATT&CK                                            |
| ------------------- | ----------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Primary Purpose** | To score and prioritize a specific risk.  | To map out and understand real-world attacker behavior. |
| **Perspective**     | Internal (Looking at _your_ app's flaws). | External (Looking at _the attacker's_ playbook).        |
| **Output**          | A numerical score (e.g., 2.4 out of 3).   | A map of tactics, techniques, and mitigations.          |

### The Workflow in Action:

Imagine you are building a new mobile banking app.

1. You use **MITRE ATT&CK** to look up how hackers typically target mobile devices (e.g., they look for "Reverse Engineering"
   or "Credential Stuffing").
2. You check your app's design to see if you are vulnerable to those exact techniques.
3. You find a flaw where your app stores data insecurely on the phone. You use **DREAD** to score it (High Damage, High
   Exploitability, etc.) to figure out how fast your developers need to patch it.
