In application security (AppSec), a **risk rating** is the mechanism used to measure and prioritize the severity of a
discovered vulnerability. Because development teams have limited time, they cannot fix every single bug immediately. Risk
ratings tell them what to fix _now_ and what can wait.

Think of it as triage in a hospital emergency room: a broken bone gets treated before a mild cough.

Here is how risk rating works in the security world:

---

## The Core Formula

At its most basic level, security risk is calculated using two main factors:

$$\text{Risk} = \text{Likelihood} \times \text{Impact}$$

- **Likelihood (Probability):** How easy is it for a hacker to find and exploit this vulnerability? (e.g., Does it require
  admin access, or can anyone on the internet do it with one click?)
- **Impact (Consequence):** If the vulnerability is exploited, how bad will the damage be? (e.g., Will it leak the entire
  customer database, or just change the background color of a page?)

---

## The Standard Rating Scale

Most organizations use a four- or five-tier scale to categorize risks. These are often mapped out on a **Risk Matrix** (a
grid crossing Likelihood and Impact):

| Risk Level   | Description                                                                                                     | Action Required                                            |
| ------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Critical** | Easy to exploit and causes catastrophic damage (e.g., remote code execution on a core server).                  | **Fix Immediately.** Stop deployment if necessary.         |
| **High**     | Harder to exploit but causes severe damage, or easy to exploit with moderate damage (e.g., stealing user data). | **Fix ASAP.** Usually within days or a couple of weeks.    |
| **Medium**   | Requires specific conditions or expertise to exploit, with limited impact (e.g., leaking minor system info).    | **Fix in next sprint.** Address during normal maintenance. |
| **Low**      | Very difficult to exploit and causes minimal to no real harm.                                                   | **Fix if time permits.** Often accepted as a known risk.   |

---

## How the Industry Standardizes Ratings

To avoid subjective arguments (like a developer thinking a bug is "Low" while a security engineer thinks it's "High"), the
industry relies on standardized frameworks:

### 1. CVSS (Common Vulnerability Scoring System)

CVSS is the most widely used framework. It assigns a numerical score from **0.0 to 10.0** based on specific technical
characteristics (like whether it requires physical access to the device or if it affects data confidentiality).

- _Example:_ A score of `9.8` is Critical, while a `3.1` is Low.

### 2. OWASP Risk Rating Methodology

The Open Worldwide Application Security Project (OWASP) provides a framework tailored specifically for web applications. It
breaks down the math into deeper categories:

- **Likelihood factors:** Threat agent factors (how skilled is the attacker?) and Vulnerability factors (how easy is it to
  discover?).
- **Impact factors:** Technical impact (loss of data, loss of accountability) and Business impact (financial damage,
  reputation damage, legal non-compliance).

---

## Why Context Matters (The "Business Lens")

A technical vulnerability doesn't exist in a vacuum. A **9.8 Critical** vulnerability found on an isolated test server that
contains fake data and isn't connected to the internet might actually be downgraded to a **Medium** or **Low** risk for the
business.

Conversely, a **Medium** vulnerability on a server that handles live credit card transactions might be upgraded to **High**
because the business impact of a failure there is unacceptable.

Ultimately, risk rating bridges the gap between technical flaws and business reality, ensuring security teams spend their
energy where it matters most.
