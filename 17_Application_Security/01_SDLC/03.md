**User Acceptance Testing (UAT)** is the final phase of the software development lifecycle (SDLC) before a piece of software
goes live.

Think of it as the **"final test drive"** of a car before you buy it. While the engineers and mechanics (the development and
QA teams) have already checked that the engine works and the wheels turn, UAT is when the actual driver (the end-user or
client) sits in the seat to make sure it's comfortable, handles well, and fits their daily needs.

---

### ## Why is UAT Important?

Even if software is completely free of technical bugs, it can still fail if it doesn't do what the business actually needs it
to do. UAT bridges the gap between technical requirements and real-world business expectations.

---

### ## Who Performs UAT?

- **End-Users:** The people who will actually use the software for their day-to-day work.
- **Business Analysts (BAs):** They help coordinate the testing to ensure business requirements are met.
- **Clients/Product Owners:** The stakeholders who paid for or requested the software.

---

### ## The UAT Process

The typical workflow for user acceptance testing follows these steps:

1. **Requirements Analysis:** The testing team reviews the original business requirements to understand what the software is
   supposed to achieve.
2. **UAT Test Planning:** A strategy is created outlining the timeline, who will test, and what scenarios need to be covered.
3. **Designing Test Cases:** Real-world scenarios (called **User Stories** or **Use Cases**) are written out. For example:
   _"Log into the system, add an item to the cart, apply a 10% coupon, and successfully check out."_
4. **Executing Tests:** Users execute the test cases in a dedicated UAT environment that mimics the real production world.
5. **Bug Reporting & Fixing:** Any unexpected behavior, confusing workflows, or missed requirements are logged and sent back
   to developers to fix.
6. **Sign-Off:** Once all major issues are resolved and the users are satisfied, formal sign-off is given. The software is
   now ready for production deployment.

---

### ## Types of UAT

Depending on the product and audience, UAT generally falls into two main categories:

| Type              | What it Means                                                                                                                            | Who Does It?                           |
| ----------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| **Alpha Testing** | Testing done internal to the organization, usually in a controlled environment, to catch major usability issues before outsiders see it. | Internal staff / Business stakeholders |
| **Beta Testing**  | Releasing a pre-production version of the software to a limited group of real, external users to gather feedback in the real world.      | Actual end-users / Public testers      |

---

> 💡 **Summary:** In short, while QA testing asks, _"Did we build the software right?"_, UAT asks, **_"Did we build the right
> software?"_**
