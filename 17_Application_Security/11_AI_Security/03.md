The **AI RMF** (Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework) is a set of voluntary, practical guidelines developed by
the **U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)**.

Released in January 2023 (known as AI RMF 1.0), it is designed to help organizations—ranging from startups to multinational
corporations and government agencies—design, develop, deploy, and evaluate AI systems responsibly.

Instead of treating AI like traditional cybersecurity where you mainly focus on locking down network boundaries, the AI RMF
takes a holistic, "socio-technical" approach. It addresses non-technical impacts alongside security—such as algorithmic bias,
lack of transparency, data privacy, and societal harm.

---

## The 4 Core Functions

The framework is structured around four continuous, interconnected functions:

| Function    | Focus                    | Action                                                                                                                                                                  |
| ----------- | ------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Govern**  | Policy & Culture         | Establishes organizational risk culture, accountability structures, legal compliance, and overarching policies for AI use.                                              |
| **Map**     | Context & Identification | Identifies the specific context, potential risks, user impacts, and technical dependencies of an AI system before and during deployment.                                |
| **Measure** | Testing & Analysis       | Uses quantitative and qualitative metrics to evaluate, test, and track AI risks (e.g., testing for bias, accuracy, and security vulnerabilities like prompt injection). |
| **Manage**  | Action & Response        | Allocates resources to treat, respond to, mitigate, or plan for risks identified during the mapping and measuring phases.                                               |

---

## Characteristics of Trustworthy AI

According to NIST's framework, for an AI system to be considered "trustworthy," organizations should measure it against seven
key characteristics:

- **Valid and Reliable:** The AI performs consistently and accurately under real-world conditions.
- **Safe:** It does not pose unacceptable risks to human life, health, property, or the environment.
- **Secure and Resilient:** It can withstand cyberattacks, unexpected inputs, or operational failures.
- **Accountable and Transparent:** Outputs are traceable, and processes are clear to regulators and end-users.
- **Explainable and Interpretable:** Humans can understand _how_ or _why_ the AI reached a given output.
- **Privacy-Enhanced:** It respects data protection rights and limits user data exposure.
- **Fair (Harmful Bias Managed):** It avoids unfair discrimination or systematic bias against protected groups.

---

> **Key Takeaway:** The AI RMF is **not a legal requirement or certification standard** (like ISO or GDPR), nor does it hand
> out "pass/fail" grades. Instead, it provides a flexible blueprint so organizations can build AI tools that people can
> actually trust.
