**AI Security** sits at the intersection of artificial intelligence and cybersecurity. Because AI systems handle vast amounts
of sensitive data, make autonomous decisions, and interact with critical software, securing them requires a dual approach:
**securing the AI itself** and **using AI as a defense mechanism.**

---

## 1. The Core Pillars of AI Security

AI security generally breaks down into three main categories:

| Dimension                                   | Description                                                                  | Key Focus Areas                                                                 |
| ------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Securing AI Systems** (_Security OF AI_)  | Protecting AI models, pipelines, and underlying infrastructure from attacks. | Guarding against prompt injection, model theft, and data poisoning.             |
| **Securing AI Usage** (_Governance_)        | Managing how humans and applications interact with AI tools.                 | Preventing data leakage, regulating "Shadow AI", and enforcing access controls. |
| **AI in Cybersecurity** (_AI FOR Security_) | Using AI technologies to defend traditional IT environments.                 | Anomaly detection, automated threat hunting, and rapid incident response.       |

---

## 2. Key AI-Specific Security Threats

AI models—especially Large Language Models (LLMs) and autonomous agents—introduce unique attack surfaces that traditional
firewalls and antivirus tools weren't built to handle:

- **Prompt Injection:** Crafting inputs to manipulate an LLM into ignoring its instructions, performing unintended actions,
  or leaking confidential instructions.
- **Data Poisoning:** Corrupting or tampering with the data used to train or fine-tune a model, causing it to output biased,
  inaccurate, or malicious responses.
- **Adversarial Attacks:** Feeding slightly altered inputs (like modified images or noise in data) that trick an AI model
  into making wrong classifications without triggering human suspicion.
- **Model Extraction & Theft:** Querying a model repeatedly via its API to reverse-engineer its parameters, weights, or
  intellectual property.
- **Sensitive Data Leakage:** Users inadvertently typing sensitive personal information, proprietary code, or trade secrets
  into public AI models.

---

## 3. Defensive Strategies & Best Practices

To protect AI deployment throughout its lifecycle, organizations rely on several security measures:

- **Guardrails & Firewalls:** Implementing input and output filters to catch prompt injections, toxic content, or sensitive
  data before it reaches or leaves the model.
- **Data Lineage & Provenance:** Ensuring training data comes from verified sources and undergoes rigorous auditing to
  prevent data poisoning.
- **Zero Trust & Role-Based Access Control (RBAC):** Restricting what data an AI agent or model can access and requiring
  explicit approvals before AI performs actions in downstream systems.
- **Continuous Monitoring:** Tracking logs for unusual API traffic, query volume spikes, or abnormal tool-calling patterns
  that indicate automated extraction or denial-of-service attempts.
