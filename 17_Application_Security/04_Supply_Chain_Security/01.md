## What is Supply Chain Security?

**Supply chain security** focuses on protecting the entire lifecycle of a product or service—from the raw materials and
software components all the way to the final delivery to the end user.

In today's interconnected world, products are rarely built in isolation. Whether it's a physical device (like a smartphone)
or a piece of software (like an app), companies rely on a web of third-party vendors, suppliers, and open-source libraries.
If a hacker or malicious actor compromises just _one_ of those suppliers, they can gain unauthorized access to the final
product and everyone who uses it.

---

## The Two Main Types

### 1. Cyber / Software Supply Chain Security

This deals with protecting software code, digital infrastructure, and data.

- **The Risk:** A developer might inadvertently use an open-source code library that contains malware or an unpatched
  vulnerability.
- **Famous Example:** The **Log4j vulnerability** or the **SolarWinds hack**, where attackers injected malicious code into
  legitimate software updates, affecting thousands of organizations worldwide.

### 2. Physical Supply Chain Security

This focuses on the logistics, manufacturing, and transport of tangible goods.

- **The Risk:** Theft, tampering, counterfeiting, or sabotage of hardware components while they are being shipped across
  borders or stored in warehouses.
- **The Goal:** Ensuring that hardware chips or physical devices haven't been modified with malicious "backdoors" before they
  reach the consumer.

---

## Key Strategies for Prevention

To keep supply chains secure, organizations generally follow the principle of **"never trust, always verify."** Here are the
core practices:

- **Software Bill of Materials (SBOM):** Think of this as an ingredient list for software. It details every component,
  library, and tool used to build an application, making it easy to spot known vulnerabilities.
- **Vendor Risk Assessments:** Before partnering with a third-party supplier, companies audit their security practices to
  ensure they meet strict standards.
- **Least Privilege Access:** Limiting the access that external vendors have to internal networks. A vendor should only see
  the data absolutely necessary to do their job.
- **Continuous Monitoring:** Using automated tools to scan code, track shipments, and flag unusual behavior or anomalies in
  real-time.
