A **Physical Block Address (PBA)** is the actual, concrete location where a piece of data is physically stored on a storage
drive (like an SSD or HDD).

To understand what a PBA is, you have to understand the relationship between your computer's Operating System (OS) and your
storage hardware. They don't actually speak the same language when it comes to locations.

---

## The Translation: LBA vs. PBA

Your computer relies on a two-tier addressing system to read and write data: **Logical Block Addresses (LBA)** and **Physical
Block Addresses (PBA)**.

1. **Logical Block Address (LBA):** This is the "virtual" address used by the Operating System (and the file system, like
   NTFS or ext4). The OS thinks of your storage drive as a long, perfectly organized, unbroken line of blocks numbered from 0
   to $N$. When the OS wants to read a file, it asks the drive, _"Give me the data at LBA #500."_
2. **Physical Block Address (PBA):** This is the _real_ address on the hardware disk. It represents the exact physical
   coordinates—the specific flash memory chip, the specific block, and the specific page inside an SSD.

### The Flash Translation Layer (FTL)

A specialized microcontroller inside the SSD, called the **Flash Translation Layer (FTL)**, acts as a dynamic map. It
constantly maps LBAs to PBAs.

---

## Why split them up? (The SSD Problem)

On an old mechanical Hard Disk Drive (HDD), the LBA and PBA were usually fixed and nearly identical. If the OS wrote to LBA
#500, the drive wrote it to the physical sector #500 on the magnetic platter. If you updated the file, it overwrote physical
sector #500.

On a modern SSD, this is impossible because **SSDs cannot overwrite data directly** (they must erase an entire block before
writing to a page).

Because of this limitation, the PBA must constantly change:

- **The Update Shift:** If you modify a file at **LBA #500**, the SSD cannot rewrite it at its current physical location
  (**PBA #1000**). Instead, it writes the new data to a fresh, empty physical slot (**PBA #2048**), marks the old PBA #1000
  as "invalid," and updates its internal map.
- **The Result:** To your Operating System, the file is still safely sitting at **LBA #500**. The OS has no idea that behind
  the scenes, the physical location (PBA) just moved.

---

## Why PBA Matters: Wear Leveling and Garbage Collection

By decoupling the physical address from the logical address, the SSD controller can perform crucial maintenance tasks without
the computer ever knowing:

- **Wear Leveling:** Flash memory cells degrade every time they are written to. If you frequently update a file, the FTL will
  intentionally cycle the data through different PBAs across the entire drive. This ensures all the physical chips wear out
  at the exact same rate, extending the lifespan of the SSD.
- **Garbage Collection:** As the drive fills up, the SSD controller will quietly move valid data from a messy physical block
  to a clean one. It shuffles the PBAs around in the background and clears out the old, invalid physical blocks so they are
  ready for new writes.

> **Analogy:** Think of the **LBA** as your permanent phone number (it never changes, so people always know how to reach
> you). Think of the **PBA** as the temporary cell tower routing your call at any given second depending on where you are
> standing. You don't care which tower is being used, as long as the call goes through.
