To understand the difference between a **primary partition** and an **extended partition**, you have to look back at the
limitations of the classic storage standard known as **MBR (Master Boot Record)**.

Introduced in 1983 with PC DOS 2.0, MBR was the default partitioning scheme for decades. Because MBR was designed when hard
drives were tiny, its partition table had a hard structural limit: **it could only hold a maximum of 4 partitions.**

To bypass this 4-partition limit without inventing a whole new partition table, engineers created the concept of primary and
extended partitions.

---

## 1. Primary Partition

A **primary partition** is a standard, standalone slice of your storage drive.

- **The Bootable Slot:** Operating systems (like Windows or Linux) generally require their boot files to be located on a
  primary partition.
- **The Limit:** Under an MBR disk layout, you can have a maximum of **4 primary partitions**. If you create 4 primary
  partitions, your disk is considered "full" from a structural standpoint—even if you still have hundreds of gigabytes of
  unallocated, empty space left on the drive.
- **Modern Context:** A primary partition behaves exactly like an independent hard drive to the user, typically mapped to
  letters like `C:` on Windows.

---

## 2. Extended Partition (The Workaround)

An **extended partition** was invented purely as a clever loophole to break the 4-partition barrier.

If you need more than 4 partitions on an MBR disk, you cannot just make 5 primary partitions. Instead, you change the
strategy: you create **3 primary partitions**, and you convert the remaining space on the drive into **1 extended
partition**.

- **The Container:** An extended partition cannot store data directly. It does not get a drive letter (like `D:` or `E:`).
  Instead, it acts like a large structural "container" or a "virtual drive" nested inside your main drive.
- **Logical Drives:** Inside this single extended partition, you can create dozens of sub-partitions called **Logical
  Drives**. To the operating system and the user, these logical drives look, act, and feel exactly like primary partitions
  (you can format them with NTFS/ext4, store files, etc.), but structurally they live inside the extended partition's
  "fence."

---

## Summary Comparison

| Feature              | Primary Partition          | Extended Partition                     | Logical Drive                                               |
| -------------------- | -------------------------- | -------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Max Number (MBR)** | Up to 4                    | Up to 1 (takes the place of 1 primary) | Virtually unlimited (inside the extended)                   |
| **Can Store Data?**  | **Yes**                    | ❌ **No** (It is just a container)     | **Yes**                                                     |
| **Can Boot an OS?**  | **Yes** (Standard default) | ❌ **No**                              | **Yes** (With modern bootloaders, but historically limited) |

---

## Do you need to care about this today? (MBR vs. GPT)

If you are setting up a computer today, you rarely have to deal with primary vs. extended partitions because of the
transition to **GPT (GUID Partition Table)**.

GPT is the modern replacement for MBR. It comes standard with any computer built in the last decade utilizing modern UEFI
firmware instead of the legacy BIOS.

- **GPT has no 4-partition limit:** By default, GPT supports up to **128 partitions** on a single drive.
- **Everything is Primary:** Because GPT can natively handle 128 partitions, **extended partitions and logical drives are
  completely obsolete** on a GPT drive. Every partition you create is simply a primary partition.
