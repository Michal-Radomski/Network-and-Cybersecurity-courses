Understanding how computer memory and storage work can feel like trying to decode a secret language, but it’s actually pretty
straightforward when you look at the jobs they do.

Think of your computer like a digital workspace: **RAM** is the top of your desk, **SSD** is the filing cabinet in the
corner, and **ROM** is the instruction manual glued to the desk.

Here is a breakdown of what they are, what they do, and how they are physically built.

---

## The Quick Comparison

| Feature          | RAM (Random Access Memory)                        | ROM (Read-Only Memory)                                  | SSD (Solid-State Drive)                                 |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Primary Role** | Temporary workspace for active apps.              | Permanent storage for vital boot-up instructions.       | Long-term storage for your files, games, and OS.        |
| **Speed**        | Ultra-fast.                                       | Fast, but mostly used only at startup.                  | Very fast (much faster than old HDDs, slower than RAM). |
| **Volatility**   | **Volatile** (Wipes completely when powered off). | **Non-volatile** (Never forgets, even without power).   | **Non-volatile** (Saves your data when powered off).    |
| **Read/Write**   | Can read and write constantly.                    | Read-only (mostly). Changing it requires special tools. | Can read and write repeatedly.                          |

---

## How They Are Built (The Hardware Inside)

All three of these components are made of semiconductor material (usually silicon) and live on circuit boards, but their
internal architectures are completely different.

### 1. RAM (Random Access Memory)

Most modern computers use **DRAM** (Dynamic RAM).

- **The Build:** Inside a RAM chip, the silicon is packed with millions or billions of tiny **memory cells**. Each individual
  cell is made of just two things: one microscopic **transistor** (a switch) and one microscopic **capacitor** (a bucket that
  holds an electrical charge).
- **How it works:** A charged capacitor represents a `1` (data), and an empty capacitor represents a `0`. Because capacitors
  naturally leak electricity, RAM has to be constantly refreshed with power thousands of times per second to keep its data.
  That’s why your RAM wipes clean the second your computer loses power.

### 2. ROM (Read-Only Memory)

Modern computers usually use a type of ROM called **EEPROM** (Electrically Erasable Programmable Read-Only Memory) or NOR
Flash for the computer's BIOS/UEFI.

- **The Build:** ROM is built using a matrix of **floating-gate transistors**. Unlike regular transistors, these have an
  extra, isolated "gate" that can trap electrons permanently.
- **How it works:** Electrons are forced into this floating gate using a higher voltage than normal operation. Once they are
  trapped, they stay there for years, even if the device is completely unplugged. Because modifying this requires high
  voltages or specific software protocols, it is effectively "Read-Only" during normal use.

### 3. SSD (Solid-State Drive)

SSDs are built using **NAND Flash memory**, which is actually a close relative of ROM, but optimized for massive scale and
constant rewriting.

- **The Build:** Like ROM, an SSD uses **floating-gate transistors** (or newer **charge trap** technology) to store data
  without power. However, instead of being spread out flat, modern SSDs use **3D NAND**, where the memory cells are stacked
  vertically on top of each other—sometimes over 200 layers high—like a digital skyscraper to squeeze terabytes of data into
  a tiny space.
- **The Controller:** An SSD also includes a powerful brain called a **Controller**. This is a mini-processor that manages
  where data is written, ensures the cells wear out evenly, and communicates with the rest of your computer.

---

## Summary: How They Work Together

When you turn on your computer:

1. **ROM** wakes up first and runs the instructions to test your hardware and start the system.
2. The operating system and your games load from the **SSD** (where they are safely stored) into the **RAM**.
3. **RAM** holds that data actively while you play or work because it can talk to your CPU at lightning speeds.
