When it comes to your computer's memory, not all RAM is created equal. **SRAM (Static RAM)** and **DRAM (Dynamic RAM)** are
the two primary types of volatile memory used in modern electronics. While they both hold data, they do it in completely
different ways and serve very different purposes.

Here is a breakdown of how they work and how they compare.

---

## 1. SRAM (Static Random Access Memory)

SRAM is called "static" because once a bit of data is written to it, it stays there as long as the system has power. It
doesn't need to be constantly refreshed.

- **How it works:** SRAM uses a cluster of transistors (typically **6 transistors** per bit) configured as a flip-flop
  circuit to store each bit of data.
- **Where it's used:** Because it is incredibly fast, SRAM is used for **CPU Cache memory** (L1, L2, L3 caches)—the
  ultra-fast storage located right next to or inside the computer's processor.
- **The Catch:** Because each bit requires 6 transistors, SRAM chips are physically large and very expensive to manufacture.
  This is why your CPU cache is usually measured in megabytes (e.g., 32 MB), not gigabytes.

---

## 2. DRAM (Dynamic Random Access Memory)

DRAM is called "dynamic" because it is constantly changing. It cannot hold onto data permanently; it leaks energy and must be
continually topped up.

- **How it works:** DRAM stores each bit of data using a combination of just **one transistor and one capacitor**. Think of
  the capacitor like a tiny bucket that holds an electrical charge (a "1") or stays empty (a "0").
- **The Refresh Cycle:** Because those tiny buckets naturally leak electricity, DRAM has to be "refreshed" (recharged)
  thousands of times every single second to prevent data from disappearing.
- **Where it's used:** This is your standard **System RAM** (the 8 GB, 16 GB, or 32 GB sticks you install on your
  motherboard).
- **The Advantage:** Because it only needs one transistor and one capacitor per bit, DRAM is incredibly dense and cheap to
  make. You can pack billions of bits into a tiny, affordable stick of RAM.

---

## SRAM vs. DRAM: Quick Comparison

| Feature               | SRAM (Static RAM)                          | DRAM (Dynamic RAM)                           |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------- |
| **Speed**             | Extremely fast (nanoseconds)               | Slower than SRAM                             |
| **Structure**         | 6 Transistors per bit                      | 1 Transistor + 1 Capacitor per bit           |
| **Need to Refresh?**  | No                                         | Yes (Thousands of times per second)          |
| **Size/Density**      | Low density (takes up more physical space) | High density (holds more data in less space) |
| **Cost**              | Very expensive                             | Inexpensive                                  |
| **Primary Use**       | CPU Cache                                  | Main System Memory (RAM sticks)              |
| **Power Consumption** | Low (when idle)                            | High (due to constant refreshing)            |

---

> **The Summary:** Think of **SRAM** like writing on a whiteboard with a marker—the message stays there until you actively
> erase it or turn off the lights. Think of **DRAM** like writing on a beach with a stick while the tide is coming in—you
> have to constantly redraw the letters before the water washes them away.
