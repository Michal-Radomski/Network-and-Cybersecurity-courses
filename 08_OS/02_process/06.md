Imagine you are organizing a stack of dinner plates. When you wash a new plate, you place it on the **top** of the stack.
When you need a plate to eat dinner, you take it off the **top** of the stack.

This is the core concept of a **Stack** data structure.

---

## What is a Stack?

A stack is a linear data structure that follows the **LIFO (Last-In, First-Out)** principle. This means the last element
added to the stack will be the very first one to be removed.

Think of it like a one-way street or a canister of tennis balls—you can only add or remove items from one end, known as the
**top**.

### Core Operations of a Stack:

- **Push:** Adds an element to the top of the stack.
- **Pop:** Removes and returns the element at the top of the stack.
- **Peek (or Top):** Looks at the top element without removing it.
- **isEmpty:** Checks if the stack is empty.

### Real-World Examples:

- The **"Undo" mechanism** in text editors (the last action you did is the first one undone).
- **Web browser history** (clicking the back button takes you to the page you visited most recently).
- **Function calls** in programming (the Call Stack).

---

## Stack vs. Queue: The Comparison

While a stack is "Last-In, First-Out," a **Queue** works like a real-life waiting line (e.g., waiting in line for a movie
ticket). It follows the **FIFO (First-In, First-Out)** principle, where the first person to get in line is the first person
served.

[Image comparing stack LIFO and queue FIFO structures]

Here is a quick breakdown of how they compare:

| Feature                 | Stack                                                          | Queue                                                                                             |
| ----------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Core Principle**      | **LIFO** (Last-In, First-Out)                                  | **FIFO** (First-In, First-Out)                                                                    |
| **Access Points**       | Only **one end** (the Top) is used for insertion and deletion. | **Two ends** are used. Insertion happens at the **Rear/Back**, deletion happens at the **Front**. |
| **Insertion Operation** | `Push`                                                         | `Enqueue`                                                                                         |
| **Deletion Operation**  | `Pop`                                                          | `Dequeue`                                                                                         |
| **Analogy**             | A stack of books.                                              | A line at a fast-food restaurant.                                                                 |
| **Common Use Case**     | Reversing data, undo features, backtracking algorithms.        | Task scheduling, handling website traffic, printer queues.                                        |

---

## Summary

- Use a **Stack** when you need to remember the immediate past and backtrack (LIFO).
- Use a **Queue** when you need to process things fairly in the exact order they arrived (FIFO).
