When you turn off your computer, your RAM (system memory) completely clears out. If everything vanished when the power cut,
your computer wouldn't even know how to turn itself back on.

To prevent this, computers use special types of "non-volatile" memory—meaning they keep their data even with no electricity.
Two of the most important types used during the boot process are **ROM** and **NVRAM**.

---

## What is ROM (Read-Only Memory)?

ROM is a type of memory chip that contains permanent or semi-permanent data. In computing, it serves as the absolute
foundation of the system, holding the initial instructions your computer needs to wake up.

### Key Characteristics:

- **"Read-Only":** Under normal operating conditions, the computer can only read data from it; it cannot write new data or
  alter what's already there.
- **Factory Flashed:** The software on a traditional ROM chip is written right when the chip is manufactured.
- **Modern Evolution (EEPROM):** Today, standard ROM has mostly been replaced by **EEPROM** (Electrically Erasable
  Programmable Read-Only Memory) or Flash Memory. This is what stores your motherboard's **BIOS/UEFI**. It acts like ROM
  (permanent), but it allows you to safely update ("flash") it to a newer version if needed.

### What it does during boot:

When you press the power button, the CPU is hardwired to look at a specific address in the ROM chip first. The ROM contains
the **BIOS/UEFI firmware**, which executes the POST (Power-On Self-Test) to check your hardware and get the system ready to
hand off to an operating system.

---

## What is NVRAM (Non-Volatile Random-Access Memory)?

If ROM stores the _programs_ that boot the PC, NVRAM stores the _settings_ those programs need. It is a small amount of
memory that can be quickly read from and written to, but it doesn't forget its data when the PC turns off.

### Key Characteristics:

- **Writable:** Unlike ROM, your computer can easily write new data to NVRAM on the fly.
- **The CMOS Replacement:** Older computers used a tiny amount of RAM powered by a coin-sized CR2032 battery (often called
  CMOS RAM) to remember settings. Modern UEFI systems instead use dedicated NVRAM chips (often built right into the same
  flash memory chip as the UEFI) because they don't require constant battery power to hold onto data for years.

### What it does during boot:

When the UEFI (stored in ROM) boots up, it immediately reads the variables stored in **NVRAM** to figure out _how_ you want
the computer to behave. NVRAM holds:

- **The Boot Order:** It tells UEFI whether to boot from your main SSD, a secondary hard drive, or a USB drive first.
- **Hardware Profiles:** Overclocking settings, RAM speeds (XMP/EXPO profiles), and fan speed curves.
- **Security Keys:** Secure Boot keys that verify whether Ubuntu or Windows is safe to load.
- **System Time:** It keeps track of the current date and time.

---

## Summary: How They Work Together

Think of ROM and NVRAM like a **cookbook** and a **bookmark**:

| Feature            | ROM (Read-Only Memory)                                                                           | NVRAM (Non-Volatile RAM)                                                                                            |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **The Analogy**    | The **Cookbook**: Contains the permanent recipe instructions (BIOS/UEFI code) that never change. | The **Bookmark**: Remembers your custom notes, your favorite page, and where you left off (Boot order, clock time). |
| **Modifiability**  | Cannot be changed during normal use (Requires a special "firmware flash" process).               | Easily changed by the system anytime you alter BIOS settings.                                                       |
| **Primary Job**    | Houses the firmware that initializes hardware and starts the computer.                           | Houses the custom configuration variables the firmware needs to run correctly.                                      |
| **Data Retention** | Permanent.                                                                                       | Permanent, but easily overwritten with new preferences.                                                             |
