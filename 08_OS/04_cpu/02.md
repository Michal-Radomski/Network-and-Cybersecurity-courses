The **FPU**, or **Floating-Point Unit**, is a specialized math coprocessor built directly inside your CPU. Its sole purpose
in life is to handle math involving **floating-point numbers**—which is just a computer science term for fractions and
decimals (like `3.14159` or `0.0005`).

While the standard CPU is great at counting whole numbers, doing complex decimal math is incredibly difficult for it. The FPU
acts like a dedicated scientific calculator tucked inside the main processor.

---

## The Problem: Why the CPU needs an FPU

At their core, standard computer processors are designed to work with integers (whole numbers like `1`, `2`, `42`, or
`-500`). The component responsible for this is the **ALU** (Arithmetic Logic Unit).

If you ask a standard ALU to divide $7$ by $2$, it can't easily give you `3.5`. It treats numbers as hard chunks. To do
decimal math using only an ALU, a computer has to run a long, slow software trick that breaks the decimal down into a series
of massive whole-number equations.

This takes up dozens of CPU cycles. The FPU was invented to solve this by handling decimals natively in hardware in a single
step.

---

## What Does the FPU Handle?

The FPU takes over whenever your computer encounters heavy math that requires high precision.

- **Decimal Arithmetic:** Basic addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division of floating-point numbers.
- **Transcendental Functions:** Advanced trigonometry (Sine, Cosine, Tangent) and logarithms ($\log$).
- **Scientific Notation:** Managing incredibly massive numbers or subatomic-scale fractions using standards like **IEEE 754**
  (the universal standard for floating-point math).

---

## Where is it used in the real world?

Without an FPU, your computer wouldn't just be slow; it would be practically unusable for modern visual tasks. FPUs are
heavily worked during:

### 1. 3D Graphics and Gaming

Video games are essentially massive physics engines. Calculating where a 3D object stands in space, how lighting bounces off
a wall, or how a character's cape flows in the wind requires calculating coordinates down to tiny fractions of a millimeter.

### 2. Audio and Video Editing

Rendering a 4K video or processing digital audio filters involves converting analog waves into millions of floating-point
values and running complex algorithms (like Fourier transforms) over them.

### 3. Machine Learning and AI

Training AI models requires multiplying giant matrices filled with decimal probabilities (weights). Modern CPUs rely heavily
on their FPUs—and modern GPUs rely on thousands of mini-FPUs—to handle this workload.

---

## Historical Fun Fact: The Coprocessor Era

FPUs weren't always built into the CPU. Back in the 1980s and early 1990s (during the days of Intel 8086 and 386 computers),
processors only had an ALU. If you were a scientist, engineer, or digital artist who needed decimal math, you had to go to a
computer store, buy a separate physical chip called a **math coprocessor** (like the Intel 80387), and plug it into a second
socket on your motherboard next to the CPU.

Starting with the Intel 486 DX processor in 1989, chipmakers figured out how to shrink the circuitry enough to bake the FPU
directly onto the main CPU die, making separate math coprocessors obsolete.
