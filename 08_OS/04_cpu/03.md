In computer architecture, a **bus** is a communication system or a pathway that transfers data between components inside a
computer, or between computers.

Think of it as a **highway system** inside your machine. Instead of cars, electronic signals travel along wires to move data
back and forth between the CPU, memory, storage, and peripheral devices.

---

## How a Bus Works

A bus consists of two main parts: the **data lines** (which carry the actual bits) and the **control lines** (which
coordinate who gets to talk and when).

To ensure data reaches the correct destination without colliding, the CPU manages the bus using three distinct sub-types of
pathways:

- **Data Bus:** The highway that actually carries the payload (the data or program instructions) between components.
- **Address Bus:** The pathway used by the CPU to specify _where_ in the memory (RAM) it wants to read or write data. If the
  CPU wants to fetch a file, it puts the memory address on this bus.
- **Control Bus:** The pathway used by the CPU to send commands and keep everything synchronized. It carries signals like
  "Read," "Write," or "Interrupt."

---

## The Types of Buses

Buses are generally categorized by where they sit in the system and how they move data:

### 1. Internal Bus (System Bus)

This connects the internal components of the computer directly to the motherboard, linking the CPU to main memory and
internal cache. It is designed to be blindingly fast.

### 2. External Bus (Expansion Bus)

This connects the CPU to external peripherals, expansion slots, and ports. Examples include:

- **PCIe (PCI Express):** A high-speed expansion bus used to connect graphics cards and modern NVMe SSDs directly to the
  motherboard.
- **USB (Universal Serial Bus):** A common external bus used to connect keyboards, mice, and external hard drives.
- **SATA:** An older bus standard used primarily for connecting traditional hard drives and 2.5-inch SSDs.

---

## Parallel vs. Serial Buses

Buses transmit data in one of two ways:

| Feature          | Parallel Bus                                                                                                     | Serial Bus                                                                                                  |
| ---------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **How it works** | Transmits multiple bits simultaneously across several parallel wires.                                            | Transmits data one bit at a time, sequentially, over a single wire.                                         |
| **Analogy**      | A multi-lane highway where cars drive side-by-side.                                                              | A single-lane road where cars drive bumper-to-bumper.                                                       |
| **The Catch**    | At very high speeds, signals on parallel wires can bleed into each other (called **crosstalk**), causing errors. | Because there is only one lane, there is no risk of crosstalk, allowing it to be clocked at extreme speeds. |
| **Modern Usage** | Mostly legacy internal architecture (like old IDE hard drive cables).                                            | Almost all modern high-speed connections (**PCIe, USB, SATA** are all serial).                              |

## Summary

Without the bus system, a CPU would be completely isolated. The bus is the vital infrastructure that ensures your processor
can talk to your RAM, your graphics card can receive instructions for a game, and your SSD can load files into memory.
