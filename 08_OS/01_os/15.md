When it comes to how a computer's operating system finds and reads data on a hard drive, **CHS** and **LBA** are the two main
addressing methods used in computing history.

Think of them as two different ways to give someone directions to an apartment in a massive complex. One gives you the exact
building, floor, and room number, while the other just gives you a single, sequential apartment number.

Here is the breakdown of how they both work.

---

## 1. CHS (Cylinder-Head-Sector)

CHS is the older, traditional method of addressing data. It is based directly on the physical geometry of mechanical hard
drives.

To find a specific block of data, the computer needs three coordinates:

- **Cylinder:** Hard drives consist of multiple stacked platters. A "cylinder" represents the same circular track across all
  platters, stacked vertically.
- **Head:** This identifies which specific platter surface (and the corresponding read/write head) the data is on.
- **Sector:** This is the specific pie-shaped slice of the track where the data block resides.

> 💡 **The Analogy:** CHS is like finding a book in a library by looking for the **Aisle** (Cylinder), the **Shelf** (Head),
> and the **Book Number** (Sector).

### The Downside of CHS

As hard drives grew larger, CHS ran into a major wall. The original BIOS architecture limited CHS addresses to a maximum of
1024 cylinders, 16 heads, and 63 sectors. This meant CHS could not address hard drives larger than **504 MB**. Extensions
were made to push this to about 8.4 GB, but as drive capacities exploded, a physical addressing system simply couldn't keep
up.

---

## 2. LBA (Logical Block Addressing)

To solve the limits of CHS, **LBA** was introduced. LBA completely discards the physical geometry of the drive and replaces
it with a simple, linear indexing system.

Instead of worrying about tracks and platters, LBA treats the entire hard drive as one long, continuous line of storage
blocks.

- The very first block on the drive is **LBA 0**.
- The second is **LBA 1**.
- The third is **LBA 2**, and so on, all the way to the end of the drive.

> 💡 **The Analogy:** LBA is like a massive apartment complex abandoning floor/wing numbers and simply labeling the doors
> from **1 to 10,000**.

The drive's internal controller takes the LBA number from the operating system and translates it to the physical location
automatically. This abstract approach completely removed the storage cap barriers and is what allowed hard drives to scale
into terabytes (TB) and beyond.

---

## CHS vs. LBA: Quick Comparison

| Feature              | CHS (Cylinder-Head-Sector)                                | LBA (Logical Block Addressing)                            |
| -------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Approach**         | Physical (based on drive hardware geometry)               | Logical (linear, sequential numbering)                    |
| **Complexity**       | High (requires 3 distinct coordinates)                    | Low (requires a single block number)                      |
| **Storage Limits**   | Strictly limited (historically capped at 504 MB / 8.4 GB) | Virtually unlimited (scales to petabytes)                 |
| **Modern Relevance** | Obsolete (only used in legacy/vintage systems)            | Standard (used in all modern HDDs, SSDs, and NVMe drives) |

Today, even though Solid State Drives (SSDs) don't have platters, cylinders, or heads at all, they still use **LBA** because
a simple, linear block numbering system is perfectly suited for modern operating systems.
