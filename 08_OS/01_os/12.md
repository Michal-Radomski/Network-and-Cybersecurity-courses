While both **RAM** and **Storage** are used to hold data, they serve entirely different purposes in a computer.

The easiest way to understand the difference is to imagine working at a desk: **RAM** is the top of your desk where you keep
things you are working on right this second, and **Storage** is the filing cabinet underneath where you put things away when
you are done.

---

## 1. RAM (Random Access Memory)

RAM is your computer's **short-term memory**. When you open an app, a game, or a browser tab, the CPU loads that data from
your slow storage drive into your ultra-fast RAM so it can access it instantly.

- **Speed:** Incredibly fast. It communicates with the CPU in nanoseconds.
- **Volatility (Temporary):** RAM is **volatile memory**. This means it requires electricity to hold onto data. The moment
  you turn off your computer or your battery dies, everything currently in the RAM is completely wiped out.
- **Capacity:** Typically smaller. Most modern computers have between $8\text{GB}$ and $32\text{GB}$ of RAM.
- **What it stores:** Your open browser tabs, the game you are currently playing, background system processes, and anything
  else actively running.

---

## 2. Storage (SSD / HDD)

Storage is your computer's **long-term memory**. This is where your files live permanently when they aren't actively being
used.

- **Speed:** Much slower than RAM (though modern NVMe SSDs are closing the gap, they are still significantly slower than RAM
  circuits).
- **Non-Volatility (Permanent):** Storage is **non-volatile memory**. It saves data mechanically or electronically in a way
  that survives without power. When you turn your computer off and back on tomorrow, your files will still be exactly where
  you left them.
- **Capacity:** Much larger. Most computers come with $512\text{GB}$ to $2\text{TB}$ (Terabytes) of storage space.
- **What it stores:** Your operating system (like Windows or Ubuntu), your installed games, downloaded photos, videos, and
  documents.

---

## The Dynamic Duo in Action

Here is exactly what happens when you double-click a video game icon on your desktop:

1. **The Request:** You tell the computer to open the game.
2. **The Fetch:** The CPU goes to your **Storage** drive, finds the game's files (textures, audio, code), and copies them.
3. **The Load:** The CPU dumps those copied files directly into your **RAM**. (This process is what you see as a "Loading
   Screen").
4. **The Play:** As long as you are playing, the CPU reads data directly out of the **RAM** to keep the game running smoothly
   without stuttering.
5. **The Save:** When you save your progress and close the game, the new data is written back down into your **Storage**
   drive permanently, and the **RAM** clears itself out to make room for the next app you open.
