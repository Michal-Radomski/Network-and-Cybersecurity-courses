In a CPU, the **clock** acts like a digital metronome or a heartbeat. It is an internal crystal oscillator that vibrates at a
steady, precise frequency, sending out regular electrical pulses to all the components inside the processor.

Its main job is to **synchronize** the entire CPU so that everything happens in perfect rhythm.

---

## How the CPU Clock Works

A CPU handles billions of calculations every second. To ensure that data doesn't get jumbled up, every component—like the
ALU, the registers, and the cache—waits for the clock pulse before moving to the next step of an instruction.

A single "tick" of the clock is called a **clock cycle**.

During one clock cycle, the CPU typically performs a tiny fraction of a task (like fetching an instruction or adding two
numbers together). By forcing every component to move only on the clock tick, the CPU prevents faster parts of the chip from
outrunning slower parts, avoiding data corruption.

---

## Clock Speed (Gigahertz)

When you look at computer specs and see a processor rated at **3.5 GHz** or **5.0 GHz**, you are looking at its **Clock
Speed**.

- **Hertz (Hz):** One cycle per second.
- **Megahertz (MHz):** One million cycles per second.
- **Gigahertz (GHz):** One billion cycles per second.

Therefore, a **4.0 GHz** processor has a clock that ticks **4 billion times every single second**. Generally, a higher clock
speed means the CPU can process instructions faster, though the overall performance also depends on how much work the CPU can
get done within a single clock cycle (known as _Instructions Per Cycle_, or IPC).

---

## Overclocking vs. Underclocking

Because the clock dictates how fast the CPU operates, its speed can sometimes be adjusted:

- **Overclocking:** Forcing the clock to run faster than its factory rating to get more performance. This makes the CPU work
  harder and faster, but it also generates significantly more heat and draws more power.
- **Underclocking / Dynamic Scaling:** Slowing the clock down when the computer is idle (like when you are just reading a
  webpage). Modern CPUs do this automatically to save battery life and keep your laptop cool.
