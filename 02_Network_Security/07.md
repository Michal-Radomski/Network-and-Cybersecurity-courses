To transmit data over a copper Ethernet network (like twisted-pair Cat5e or Cat6 cables), digital data (0s and 1s) is
converted into electrical signals. These signals are defined by specific electrical parameters that change depending on the
Ethernet standard (e.g., 10 Mbps, 100 Mbps, 1 Gbps, or 10 Gbps).

Here is a breakdown of the primary electrical parameters involved in Ethernet data transmission.

---

## 1. Voltage (The Signal Carrier)

Voltage is the primary parameter used to represent data in Ethernet networks. Instead of using a simple "high voltage = 1, no
voltage = 0" system, Ethernet uses sophisticated line coding techniques to ensure reliability and clock synchronization.

- **Voltage Levels:** Generally, Ethernet signals operate at relatively low voltages, typically ranging between
  **$\pm1\text{V}$ to $\pm2.5\text{V}$**.
- **Differential Signaling:** Ethernet uses **differential signaling** over twisted pairs. Instead of measuring a single
  wire's voltage against a common ground, the receiver measures the _difference_ in voltage between two wires
  ($V_{\text{positive}} - V_{\text{negative}}$). This makes the signal highly resistant to external electromagnetic
  interference (EMI).

---

## 2. Line Coding and Modulation (How parameters change)

The way voltage and current alter to represent data depends heavily on the speed of the network:

| Ethernet Standard         | Line Coding / Modulation                 | Electrical Characterization                                                                                                                                                                                                                            |
| ------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **10BASE-T** (10 Mbps)    | **Manchester Encoding**                  | A transition in the middle of the bit period represents the data. A low-to-high transition represents a logical `1`, and a high-to-low transition represents a logical `0`. Voltage swings between roughly **$\pm2.5\text{V}$**.                       |
| **100BASE-TX** (100 Mbps) | **MLT-3** (Multi-Level Transmit 3)       | Cycles through three voltage levels: **$-1\text{V}$, $0\text{V}$, and $+1\text{V}$**. A transition represents a logical `1`, and no transition represents a logical `0`. This reduces the frequency of the signal, limiting radio frequency emissions. |
| **1000BASE-T** (1 Gbps)   | **PAM-5** (Pulse Amplitude Modulation 5) | Uses five distinct voltage levels: **$-2\text{V}$, $-1\text{V}$, $0\text{V}$, $+1\text{V}$, and $+2\text{V}$**. By having 5 levels, each electrical pulse can transmit 2 bits of data simultaneously across 4 pairs of wires.                          |
| **10GBASE-T** (10 Gbps)   | **PAM-16**                               | Uses 16 distinct voltage levels. Because the levels are so close together, it requires advanced error correction to distinguish between the subtle voltage differences.                                                                                |

---

## 3. Current and Power

Because Ethernet is designed for data communication rather than power delivery, the electric current used purely for
signaling is very low.

- **Signaling Current:** The current flowing through the loops during data transmission is typically in the milliampere range
  (usually between **10 mA and 50 mA**), depending on the line driver design and voltage level.
- **Impedance:** Ethernet cables are manufactured to have a characteristic impedance of **$100\ \Omega$** (ohms) at the
  operating frequencies. This impedance must match the network equipment interfaces exactly to prevent signal reflections,
  which cause data corruption.

---

## 4. Frequency

As data rates increase, the frequency of the electrical signals (how fast the voltage switches) increases drastically:

- **10BASE-T:** Operates at a fundamental frequency of **10 MHz**.
- **100BASE-TX:** Operates at a fundamental frequency of **31.25 MHz** (thanks to MLT-3 reducing the required bandwidth from
  125 MHz).
- **1000BASE-T:** Operates at **62.5 MHz** per pair across 4 pairs.
- **10GBASE-T:** Requires frequencies up to **400 MHz to 500 MHz**, which is why higher-grade cabling like Cat6A is required
  to prevent signal degradation.

---

## A Note on Power over Ethernet (PoE)

If the network utilizes **Power over Ethernet (PoE)** to power devices like IP cameras or VoIP phones alongside data, the
electrical parameters change significantly _on top_ of the data signals:

- **PoE Voltage:** Typically ranges from **44V DC to 57V DC**.
- **PoE Current:** Can range from **350 mA** (older PoE standards) up to **960 mA** per pair (modern 90W PoE++ standards).
- This power is injected as a common-mode voltage, meaning it doesn't interfere with the differential voltage loops being
  used to transmit data.
