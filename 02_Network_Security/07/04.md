Wireless network standards are a set of technical rules developed by the IEEE (Institute of Electrical and Electronics
Engineers) under the 802.11 protocol [1, 2] . These standards ensure that wireless devices from different manufacturers can
communicate with each other consistently [3] . To make these technical names easier for consumers to understand, the Wi-Fi
Alliance introduced a simplified numerical naming scheme (e.g., Wi-Fi 6 instead of 802.11ax) [4, 5] .

## Active Modern Standards

Most devices currently in use operate on one of these three standards:

- Wi-Fi 7 (802.11be): The newest standard as of 2024, designed for "Extremely High Throughput" [6, 7] .
- Max Speed: Up to 46 Gbps [7, 8] .
  - Key Feature: Multi-Link Operation (MLO) allows devices to send and receive data across multiple frequency bands (2.4, 5,
    and 6 GHz) simultaneously for ultra-low latency [9, 10] .
- Wi-Fi 6 / 6E (802.11ax): Released in 2019, it focuses on efficiency in crowded areas [11, 12] .
- Max Speed: Up to 9.6 Gbps [8, 11] .
  - Wi-Fi 6E: An extension that adds access to the 6 GHz band, providing a "clean" lane for data with virtually no
    interference from older devices [13, 14] .
- Wi-Fi 5 (802.11ac): Released in 2013 and still very common in older home routers [11] .
- Max Speed: Roughly 3.5 Gbps [8, 15] .
  - Constraint: Operates almost exclusively on the 5 GHz band [11, 13] .

## Legacy Standards

These older standards are still supported by modern routers for "backward compatibility" but are rarely the primary
connection method for new devices:

| Generation | Technical Name | Year | Max Speed | Frequency   |
| ---------- | -------------- | ---- | --------- | ----------- |
| Wi-Fi 4    | 802.11n        | 2009 | 600 Mbps  | 2.4 & 5 GHz |
| Wi-Fi 3    | 802.11g        | 2003 | 54 Mbps   | 2.4 GHz     |
| Wi-Fi 2    | 802.11a        | 1999 | 54 Mbps   | 5 GHz       |
| Wi-Fi 1    | 802.11b        | 1999 | 11 Mbps   | 2.4 GHz     |
| (Legacy)   | 802.11         | 1997 | 2 Mbps    | 2.4 GHz     |

[8, 11, 16, 17]

## Why These Standards Matter

1.  Interoperability: They allow a Samsung phone to talk to a Netgear router and an Apple laptop simultaneously [3] .
2.  Frequency Bands: Older standards used the crowded 2.4 GHz band (shared with microwaves and baby monitors). Newer ones use
    5 GHz and 6 GHz for more speed and less congestion [4, 15] .
3.  Efficiency: Newer standards like Wi-Fi 6 and 7 use technologies like OFDMA and MU-MIMO to handle dozens of smart devices
    in one home without slowing down [12, 18] .
