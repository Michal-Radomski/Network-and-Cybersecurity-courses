A Wireless Network is a way for devices—like your phone, laptop, or smart TV—to connect to the internet or to each other
without using physical cables (like Ethernet cords). Instead of wires, these networks use radio waves to send data through
the air, similar to how a car radio or a walkie-talkie works.

## How it works in your daily life:

1.  The Source: Your internet service comes into your home or office through a wire (fiber or cable) connected to a router.
2.  The Signal: The router converts that data into radio waves and broadcasts them.
3.  The Connection: Your device has a wireless adapter that "catches" those waves and turns them back into digital data you
    can use to browse the web.

## The most common types:

- Wi-Fi: Used for local connections (like your home or a coffee shop). It has a limited range, usually about 100–300 feet.
- Cellular Networks (4G/5G): Used for long-distance connections. Your phone talks to massive cell towers miles away.
- Bluetooth: Used for very short distances, like connecting your headphones to your phone.
