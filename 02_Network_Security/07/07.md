While Network Standards (like Wi-Fi 6 or 7) determine how fast your data travels, Security Standards determine how well that
data is encrypted and protected from hackers. These standards are developed by the Wi-Fi Alliance to ensure that your
"conversations" over the air stay private.

## 1. WPA3 (Wi-Fi Protected Access 3)

Released in 2018, this is the current gold standard. If you are buying a router today, it must support WPA3.

- SAE (Simultaneous Authentication of Equals): This is the "secret sauce" of WPA3. It prevents hackers from using "offline
  dictionary attacks" (guessing millions of passwords per second until they find yours).
- Individual Data Encryption: On a public WPA3 network, your data is encrypted separately from everyone else’s, even if you
  all use the same password.
- Forward Secrecy: If a hacker somehow discovers your network password tomorrow, they still cannot go back and decrypt the
  data they recorded from you today.

## 2. WPA2 (Wi-Fi Protected Access 2)

Released in 2004, this was the industry standard for nearly 20 years and is still the most common standard in use.

- AES Encryption: Uses the "Advanced Encryption Standard," which is the same level of security used by banks and governments.
- The Flaw (KRACK): In 2017, a vulnerability called "KRACK" was discovered, allowing hackers to potentially eavesdrop. Most
  modern devices have been patched for this, but it’s why WPA3 was created.

## 3. WPA (Wi-Fi Protected Access)

This was a "quick fix" released in 2003 to replace the broken WEP standard.

- TKIP Encryption: It used a temporary method to scramble data.
- Verdict: It is now considered weak and insecure. You should avoid using this setting on your router.

## 4. WEP (Wired Equivalent Privacy)

The original security standard from 1999.

- Status: Completely broken.
- Verdict: A teenager with a basic smartphone app can crack a WEP password in less than 60 seconds. Most modern routers will
  give you a warning if you try to enable this.

---

## Which one should you use?

When you log into your router settings, you will usually see a list of options. Here is the priority list:

1.  WPA3-SAE (AES): Best. Use this for all modern phones and laptops.
2.  WPA2/WPA3 Mixed: Best for homes with a mix of new and old devices (like an old printer or smart bulb).
3.  WPA2-PSK (AES): Good. Reliable and still very secure for most home users.
4.  Open: Dangerous. No encryption at all.
