To protect your network from tools like aircrack-ng, your password needs to be resistant to dictionary attacks (guessing
common words) and brute-force attacks (guessing every possible combination).

## The "Passphrase" Strategy

The most secure passwords are long strings of unrelated words. They are easy for humans to remember but nearly impossible for
computers to guess.

- Weak: P@ssword123! (Commonly used and easily guessed by AI tools).
- Strong: Purple-Elephant-Skiing-2026!
- Why? A computer trying to crack this would have to guess four separate words plus numbers and symbols, which could take
  centuries to compute.

## The Gold Standard Rules

1.  Length is King: Aim for at least 16 characters. Every character you add increases the difficulty for a hacker
    exponentially.
2.  Avoid Personal Info: Never use your name, birthday, pet’s name, or address. These are the first things hackers put into
    their custom "wordlists" for you.
3.  Mix Character Types: Use a combination of UPPERCASE, lowercase, numbers, and special characters (!, @, #, etc.).
4.  Unique per Network: Never reuse your Wi-Fi password for your email or bank account. If one is compromised, the others
    remain safe.

## Protecting Against Tools Like Aircrack-ng

Even a strong password can be vulnerable if your router settings are weak. To specifically block the "cracking" workflow I
described earlier:

- Disable WPS: This shuts down the easiest "backdoor" into your network.
- Switch to WPA3: As mentioned before, WPA3 uses "SAE" encryption, which makes the "offline cracking" method used by
  aircrack-ng virtually impossible.
- Use a Guest Network: Put "smart" devices (like lightbulbs or cheap cameras) on a separate guest network. These often have
  weak security and can be used as a jumping-off point to attack your main devices.
