IoT stands for the Internet of Things. It refers to the billions of physical devices around the world that are now connected
to the internet, all collecting and sharing data. In short: if it’s a device with an On/Off switch and an internet
connection, it’s likely an IoT device.

## What are "those things" exactly?

"Things" can be almost anything equipped with sensors and software:

- Consumer/Home: Smart thermostats (Nest), light bulbs (Philips Hue), smart fridges, voice assistants (Alexa/Google Home),
  and wearable fitness trackers (Fitbit).
- Industrial (IIoT): Sensors on factory machines that predict when a part will break, or smart "bins" that tell the city when
  they need emptying.
- Healthcare: Heart monitors, smart insulin pumps, or "smart beds" that track patient movement.
- Infrastructure: Smart streetlights that dim when no one is around or sensors in bridges that detect structural cracks.

## How do they work?

Most IoT devices follow a simple four-step process:

1.  Sensing: The device collects data from its surroundings (e.g., a thermostat sensing room temperature).
2.  Connecting: It sends that data to the Cloud via Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, or Cellular.
3.  Processing: A software program analyzes the data (e.g., "The temperature is too high").
4.  Acting: The device performs an action or sends an alert (e.g., "Turn on the AC").

## The Big Security Catch

Because many IoT devices are built to be cheap and small, they often lack the processing power for heavy security. This makes
them a major target for hackers.

- Default Passwords: Many come with "admin/admin" and are never changed.
- Lack of Updates: Some devices can't be patched, leaving them permanently vulnerable.
- Privacy: They are constantly collecting data about your habits, location, and even conversations.

This is why, as we discussed earlier, many people use VLANs to isolate their IoT devices from their private computers.
