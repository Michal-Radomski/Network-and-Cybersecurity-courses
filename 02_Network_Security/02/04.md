NAC (Network Access Control) is a security solution that "checks the ID" of any device trying to connect to a network before
granting it access. It ensures that only authorized users and healthy devices can get in. [1, 2, 3, 4] Think of NAC as a
bouncer at a club who not only checks your name on the guest list (Authentication) but also makes sure you aren't carrying
anything dangerous (Security Posture). [5, 6, 7]

## How NAC Works (The Three Phases)

NAC follows a specific workflow to secure the network edge: [8, 9, 10]

1.  Authentication: Who are you? (Username/Password, Digital Certificate, or MAC address).
2.  Posture Assessment (Health Check): Is your device safe? The NAC system checks:

- Is your Antivirus up to date?
  - Are you running the latest OS security patches?
  - Is the device encrypted?

3.  Authorization (The Result): Based on the first two steps, the NAC places you into a specific category:

- Accept: Full access to the internal network.
  - Quarantine: Limited access (only to update servers) until you fix your security issues.
  - Reject: No access at all. [11, 12, 13, 14, 15]

## Key Types of NAC

- Pre-admission: Happens before the connection is established. If you don't meet the requirements, you never get an IP
  address.
- Post-admission: Continuously monitors the device after it has connected. If the user starts behaving suspiciously or turns
  off their firewall, the NAC can kick them off.
- Agent-based vs. Agentless:
- Agent: Requires a small piece of software installed on the device to report health status.
  - Agentless: The NAC scans the device over the network to guess its status (often used for printers or IoT devices). [16,
    17, 18, 19, 20]

Implementing NAC on Ubuntu usually involves the 802.1X standard. On a desktop or server, Ubuntu acts as the Supplicant (the
device requesting access), proving its identity to a switch or wireless controller. Here is how NAC looks and is managed on
Ubuntu:

## 1. The Graphical Interface (Desktop)

On Ubuntu Desktop, NAC is handled by NetworkManager. When you connect to a network secured with 802.1X, you will see a popup
asking for "Authentication."

- Security Tab: Under Wired or Wi-Fi settings, you'll find a "Security" or "802.1x" tab.
- Authentication Methods: You typically choose PEAP, TLS (using certificates), or TTLS.
- The "Look": It looks like a login box requiring a username and password, or the selection of a .p12 or .crt certificate
  file provided by your IT department.

## 2. The Command Line (Server/Advanced)

On Ubuntu Server or for automated setups, NAC is managed via wpa_supplicant. This is the background service that handles the
"handshake" with the network. You configure it in a file (usually /etc/wpa_supplicant/wpa_supplicant.conf):

network={ key_mgmt=IEEE8021X eap=PEAP identity="your_username" password="your_password" phase2="autheap=MSCHAPV2" }

## 3. Posture Assessment (The "Health Check")

If your organization requires a Posture Assessment (checking if your Ubuntu is updated or has antivirus), they will often use
an "Agent."

- Cisco AnyConnect / Cisco Secure Client: Common on Linux. It runs as a background process (daemon) and reports your kernel
  version and firewall status to the NAC server.
- Open-source Alternatives: Tools like PacketFence can perform scan-based (agentless) NAC on Ubuntu by checking open ports or
  using SNMP to verify the device type.

## 4. NAC for Managed Networks (Netplan)

Modern Ubuntu uses Netplan to manage networking. You can define NAC requirements directly in your YAML configuration file:

network: version: 2 ethernets: enp3s0: auth: key-management: 8021x method: peap identity: "user@domain.com" password:
"password123"

## Summary of Ubuntu NAC Tools

- Supplicant: wpa_supplicant (The engine).
- Configuration: NetworkManager (GUI) or Netplan (CLI).
- Health Reporting: Cisco Secure Client or Forescout SecureConnector.
