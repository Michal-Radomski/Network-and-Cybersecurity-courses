Cloud computing is the on-demand delivery of computing services—like servers, storage, databases, and software—over the
internet. Instead of buying and maintaining physical data centers and servers, you "rent" these resources from providers like
Amazon (AWS), Microsoft (Azure), or Google (GCP). [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]

## Core Concepts

- Pay-as-you-go: You only pay for the resources you actually use (like a utility bill).
- Scalability: You can increase (scale up) or decrease (scale down) resources instantly based on demand.
- Accessibility: You can access your data and applications from anywhere in the world with an internet connection. [6, 7, 8,
  9, 10]

## The Three Service Models (The "Stack")

1.  IaaS (Infrastructure as a Service): You rent the "raw" hardware. You get virtual servers and storage but are responsible
    for the OS and software.

- Example: AWS EC2, Google Compute Engine.

2.  PaaS (Platform as a Service): Provides a framework for developers to build and deploy apps without worrying about the
    underlying hardware or OS.

- Example: Heroku, Google App Engine.

3.  SaaS (Software as a Service): Completed software delivered via the web. You just use the app; the provider handles
    everything else.

- Example: Gmail, Slack, Office 365. [11, 12, 13, 14, 15]

## Deployment Types

- Public Cloud: Resources are shared with other organizations (the "multi-tenant" model).
- Private Cloud: Resources are used exclusively by one organization, often for high security.
- Hybrid Cloud: A mix of both, allowing data to move between private and public environments. [16, 17, 18, 19, 20]
