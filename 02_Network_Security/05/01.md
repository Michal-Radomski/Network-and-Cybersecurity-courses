A network intrusion is any unauthorized activity or "penetration" of a digital network with the intent to compromise its
security. It is similar to a physical break-in, where an intruder bypasses defenses to steal or cause harm. [1, 2, 3, 4]

## Passive vs. Active Intrusions

Intrusions are broadly classified by how they interact with the network: [5]

- Passive Intrusion: The attacker monitors or eavesdrops on network traffic to gather information without altering any data.
  Examples include packet sniffing and traffic analysis.
- Active Intrusion: The attacker directly interacts with or modifies network resources. This includes injecting malicious
  code, stealing data, or disrupting services like a Denial of Service (DoS) attack. [1, 2, 6, 7, 8, 9]

## Common Techniques

- Malware Deployment: Using viruses, worms, or Trojans to open backdoors or steal data.
- Social Engineering: Manipulating users through phishing to trick them into revealing credentials.
- Exploiting Vulnerabilities: Targeting unpatched software or weak protocols to issue unauthorized commands.
- Spoofing: Faking IP addresses or headers to hide the attacker's identity and bypass firewalls.
- Brute Force: Systematically trying all possible password combinations to gain entry. [1, 6, 10, 11, 12, 13]

## Protection Systems

Organizations use specialized tools to handle these threats: [13]

- Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS): Acts as a security alarm by monitoring traffic and alerting administrators to suspicious
  activity.
- Intrusion Prevention Systems (IPS): Proactively blocks detected threats in real-time, such as by dropping malicious data
  packets.
- Unified Threat Management (UTM): Combines firewalls, antivirus, and intrusion protection into a single platform for ease of
  management. [1, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18]
