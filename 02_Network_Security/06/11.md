The difference comes down to perspective and control. A network firewall sees traffic moving between houses; an OS-level
firewall (often called a "host-based" firewall) sees what’s happening inside the house.

## 1. Network Firewall (The Perimeter)

This is usually a dedicated piece of hardware (like your router or a corporate Cisco/Fortinet box) that sits between your
entire local network and the internet.

- What it sees: It only sees packets moving in and out of your internet connection. It identifies traffic by IP addresses and
  Port numbers.
- What it doesn't see: It has no idea which app is sending the data. To a network firewall, a malicious script and a
  legitimate web browser both just look like "Traffic on Port 443."
- Best for: Blocking entire countries, stopping massive external "denial of service" attacks, and protecting "dumb" devices
  like smart fridges that don't have their own firewalls.

## 2. OS-Level Firewall / App Firewall (The Host)

This is software (like UFW on Linux, Windows Defender Firewall, or ESET) running directly on your computer.

- What it sees: It has "insider knowledge." It knows exactly which Process ID or Application is requesting a connection.
- What it does better: It can block "Spotify" or "Zoom" specifically, whereas a network firewall would have to try and block
  thousands of ever-changing IP addresses used by those companies.
- Best for: Mobile devices (laptops on public Wi-Fi), blocking specific software from "phoning home," and stopping "lateral
  movement" (if a hacker gets into one device on your Wi-Fi, they can't easily jump to your computer).

## Key Comparison

| Feature      | Network Firewall                     | OS-Level (App) Firewall            |
| ------------ | ------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------- |
| Location     | Between the Internet and your Router | On the Computer/Phone itself       |
| Visibility   | IP addresses & Ports only            | Apps, Users, and Processes         |
| Protection   | Protects every device on the Wi-Fi   | Protects only that specific device |
| Public Wi-Fi | Useless once you leave home          | Critical protection everywhere     |
| Performance  | Zero impact on computer speed        | Uses a small amount of CPU/RAM     |

## Which do you need?

You need both. This is known as "layered security."

- The Network Firewall stops the "bulk" of the garbage from the internet before it even touches your devices.
- The OS Firewall handles the fine-grained control and protects you if the network firewall is bypassed or if the threat
  comes from inside your house (like a compromised guest device).
