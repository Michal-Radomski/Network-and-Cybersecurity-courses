Kerberos is a network authentication protocol that allows users and services to verify their identities to each other over an
"insecure" network. It is named after the three-headed dog from Greek mythology because it involves three parties: the
Client, the Server, and a trusted third party called the Key Distribution Center (KDC). [1, 2, 3, 4] Unlike standard logins,
Kerberos uses tickets instead of sending passwords across the network, making it highly resistant to eavesdropping. [5, 6]

## How Kerberos Works (Simplified)

1.  Login: You enter your password once to prove who you are to the KDC.
2.  The Master Ticket: The KDC gives you a Ticket Granting Ticket (TGT). This "master key" is stored in your computer's
    memory for a limited time (usually 10–24 hours).
3.  Single Sign-On (SSO): When you want to access a specific service (like a file share or SSH), your computer uses the TGT
    to ask the KDC for a "Service Ticket." You are granted access automatically without typing your password again. [7, 8, 9,
    10, 11, 12]

---

## How to Use Kerberos on Ubuntu

To use Kerberos on Ubuntu, you typically act as a Client connecting to an existing Kerberos realm (like a corporate Active
Directory or an MIT Kerberos server). [2, 13, 14, 15, 16]

## 1. Install Client Tools

Install the standard Kerberos user packages: [17]

sudo apt update sudo apt install krb5-user libpam-krb5

During installation, you will be prompted for your Default Kerberos 5 realm (e.g., EXAMPLE.COM). Use ALL CAPS. [18, 19, 20]

## 2. Configure the Realm

If you need to manually point Ubuntu to your Kerberos servers, edit the configuration file: [21, 22]

- File: /etc/krb5.conf
- Action: Ensure the [realms] section has the correct IP address or hostname for your KDC. [7, 23, 24]

## 3. Basic Commands

- Get a Ticket (Login): kinit your_username This prompts for your password and generates your TGT.
- List Active Tickets: klist Shows your current tickets and when they expire.
- Destroy Tickets (Logout): kdestroy Clears your current credentials from memory. [7, 10, 19, 24]

## 4. Practical Application: Passwordless SSH [25]

Once you have an active ticket (klist shows a TGT), you can log into Kerberos-enabled servers without a password: [26, 27,
28]

1.  Edit /etc/ssh/ssh_config on your client machine.
2.  Set GSSAPIAuthentication yes and GSSAPIDelegateCredentials yes.
3.  Now, just run ssh user@server and you will be logged in instantly using your ticket. [24, 29, 30]
