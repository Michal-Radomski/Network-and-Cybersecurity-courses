pfSense and OPNsense are Network Firewalls. [1, 2, 3] Specifically, they are classified as perimeter firewalls or gateway
firewalls. They are designed to sit at the edge of your network (usually right after your modem) to act as a barrier between
your entire local network and the internet. [4, 5, 6, 7] Here is why they are considered network-based rather than
host-based:

- Dedicated Hardware: They are typically installed on a standalone machine with multiple network ports (NICs). One port
  connects to the internet (WAN) and the others to your internal network (LAN).
- Infrastructure Management: They don't just filter traffic; they manage the entire network’s infrastructure, handling things
  like DHCP (assigning IP addresses), DNS, and VPN hosting.
- Inter-VLAN Routing: They can control traffic moving between different "zones" in your house—for example, preventing your
  sketchy Smart TV network from talking to your secure Home Office network.
- No App Awareness: Unless you install advanced "Deep Packet Inspection" plugins, these firewalls don't naturally know which
  specific software (like Spotify or Chrome) is sending data; they only see that a device at a certain IP address is
  requesting a connection. [8, 9, 10, 11, 12]

## How they work with UFW

In a professional or "prosumer" setup, you use both:

1.  OPNsense acts as the "Border Guard" at your front door, filtering the massive amount of noise and attacks coming from the
    open internet.
2.  UFW (on Ubuntu) acts as the "Bedroom Door Lock," protecting your specific machine from anything that might have slipped
    past the border or originated from another device inside your home.
