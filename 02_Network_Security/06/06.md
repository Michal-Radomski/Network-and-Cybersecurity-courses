Firewall rules are sets of instructions that tell your security system how to handle data packets entering or leaving your
computer. Think of them as a security checklist used by a bouncer at a club to decide who gets in and who stays out. [1, 2,
3, 4]

## The 5 Components of a Rule

A standard firewall rule (often called a "tuple") is defined by these five core pieces of information: [5, 6]

- Source: The IP address of the device sending the data.
- Destination: The IP address of the device intended to receive it.
- Protocol: The "language" being used, such as TCP (web browsing), UDP (streaming/gaming), or ICMP (ping tests).
- Port: The specific "doorway" being targeted (e.g., Port 80/443 for web, Port 22 for SSH).
- Action: What to do when a match is found—usually Allow, Deny (drop silently), or Reject (drop and send an error back). [2,
  5, 7, 8]

## Directional Rules

- Inbound Rules: Control traffic coming into your device from the internet. By default, most firewalls block all unsolicited
  inbound traffic unless you host a service (like a game server) that needs an open port.
- Outbound Rules: Control traffic leaving your device. Standard home firewalls often allow all outbound traffic, but
  high-security setups restrict it to prevent malware from communicating with a hacker's server. [5, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]

## How Rules Are Evaluated (The "Top-Down" Order)

Firewalls process rules in a specific sequential order from top to bottom: [2, 5]

1.  First Match Wins: The firewall starts at Rule #1. If the data packet matches Rule #1, it takes that action (Allow or
    Deny) and ignores every other rule below it.
2.  Order Matters: If you have a rule at the top that says "Allow All" and a rule at the bottom that says "Block IP 1.2.3.4,"
    the block rule will never be reached, and that IP will still get through.
3.  The "Deny All" Default: Most professional firewalls have an invisible rule at the very bottom: Implicit Deny. If a packet
    doesn't match any of your specific "Allow" rules, it is automatically blocked. [5, 6, 12, 13, 14, 15]

## Common Best Practices

- Least Privilege: Only allow the absolute minimum traffic needed for your apps to work.
- Group by Type: Organize rules into sections (e.g., "Web Browsing," "Internal Network," "Remote Access") so they are easier
  to manage.
- Log Hits: Enable logging for "Deny" rules to see who is trying to scan your network. [6, 10, 16, 17, 18, 19]
