Cryptography is the practice and study of techniques for securing communication and data. At its simplest, it is the process
of converting readable information (plaintext) into an unreadable format (ciphertext) using a mathematical algorithm and a
secret key. [1, 2, 3, 4, 5] It is the engine that drives modern digital security, from the green padlock in your browser to
the privacy of your WhatsApp messages. [6, 7, 8]

## The Four Objectives of Cryptography

Cryptography is designed to satisfy the four pillars of the IAAA and CIA frameworks:

1.  Confidentiality: Ensuring only the intended recipient can read the message.
2.  Integrity: Ensuring the message hasn't been changed during transit.
3.  Authentication: Confirming the sender’s identity.
4.  Non-repudiation: Preventing the sender from denying they sent the message. [9, 10, 11, 12, 13]

---

## Three Main Types of Cryptography## 1. Symmetric-Key Cryptography (Secret Key) [14]

The sender and receiver use the same key for both encryption and decryption. It is extremely fast but has a major flaw: you
must find a secure way to share the key with the other person first. [15, 16, 17, 18, 19]

- Analogy: A physical safe where both people have a copy of the same key.
- Common Algorithms: AES (Advanced Encryption Standard), DES. [20, 21, 22, 23, 24]

## 2. Asymmetric-Key Cryptography (Public Key)

This uses a pair of keys: a Public Key (which anyone can see) and a Private Key (kept secret by the owner). [25, 26, 27, 28,
29]

- If you encrypt something with my Public Key, only my Private Key can decrypt it.
- Analogy: A mailbox. Anyone can put mail in (Public Key), but only the owner has the key to open it and read the mail
  (Private Key).
- Common Algorithms: RSA, ECC (Elliptic Curve Cryptography). [30, 31, 32, 33, 34]

## 3. Hash Functions (One-Way Encryption) [35]

Hashing is a mathematical process that turns any amount of data into a fixed-length string of characters. It is one-way; you
cannot "decrypt" a hash back into the original data. [36, 37, 38, 39, 40]

- Usage: Primarily for Integrity (checking if a file was tampered with) and Password Storage (so the server doesn't store
  your actual password).
- Common Algorithms: SHA-256, MD5 (now considered insecure). [41, 42, 43, 44, 45]

---

## Modern Applications

- SSL/TLS (HTTPS): Uses asymmetric encryption to exchange a symmetric key, then uses that symmetric key to encrypt your web
  session.
- Digital Signatures: Uses your private key to "sign" a document, proving it came from you and hasn't been altered.
- End-to-End Encryption (E2EE): Used in messaging apps to ensure data is encrypted on your device and only decrypted on the
  recipient's device. [46, 47, 48, 49, 50]
