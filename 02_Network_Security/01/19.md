Think of **SSL** and **TLS** like the same brand of car, just from different decades. SSL is the classic (but now unsafe)
original model, while TLS is the modern, high-performance successor.

### The Big Picture

Both **SSL (Secure Sockets Layer)** and **TLS (Transport Layer Security)** are cryptographic protocols that encrypt data sent
between a web server and a browser. When you see a padlock icon and `https://` in your address bar, one of these is working
behind the scenes.

---

### SSL (The Ancestor)

Developed by Netscape in the mid-90s, SSL was the first attempt to secure the web.

- **Versions:** 1.0 (never released), 2.0 (1995), and 3.0 (1996).
- **Current Status:** **Completely Deprecated.** You shouldn't use it anymore. It has major security holes (like the "POODLE"
  attack) that modern hackers can easily exploit.
- **The Legacy:** Even though we don't use the technology, the name stuck. This is why people still say "SSL Certificate"
  when they technically mean "TLS Certificate."

### TLS (The Modern Standard)

In 1999, the IETF (Internet Engineering Task Force) took over the protocol and renamed it TLS to show it was no longer tied
to a single company like Netscape.

- **Versions:** 1.0, 1.1, 1.2, and 1.3.
- **Current Status:** **TLS 1.2 and 1.3** are the current industry standards. TLS 1.0 and 1.1 were officially retired
  in 2021.
- **Why it's better:** It uses much stronger encryption algorithms, has a faster "handshake" (the initial connection
  process), and fixes the vulnerabilities found in the old SSL versions.

---

### Key Differences at a Glance

| Feature            | SSL (Older)                             | TLS (Modern)                                 |
| :----------------- | :-------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------- |
| **Security**       | Weak; vulnerable to modern attacks.     | Strong; designed for modern threats.         |
| **Speed**          | Slower handshake (more back-and-forth). | Faster (TLS 1.3 is "1-RTT" or even "0-RTT"). |
| **Authentication** | Basic message authentication.           | Uses HMAC (Hashed Message Auth Code).        |
| **Status**         | Deprecated / "End of Life."             | Active Industry Standard.                    |

---

### ⚠️ A Note for 2026

If you are managing a website or server today, there’s a major shift happening with certificates. As of **March 2026**, the
industry has begun shortening the lifespan of TLS certificates.

- **Old rule:** Certificates lasted 398 days (~13 months).
- **New 2026 rule:** New certificates now have a maximum lifespan of roughly **200 days**.
- **The Goal:** Shorter lifespans force organizations to automate their renewals and ensure they aren't using compromised or
  outdated encryption for long periods.

**Pro-tip:** If you're setting up a server, make sure to **disable** SSL 2.0, SSL 3.0, and TLS 1.0/1.1 entirely. Only support
TLS 1.2 and 1.3 to keep your users safe.
