MAC spoofing is a software technique used to change the Media Access Control (MAC) address that your device reports to a
network. [1, 2] While a MAC address is a unique identifier "burned in" to your hardware (NIC) by the manufacturer, spoofing
allows you to mask this identity with a different one without physically altering the hardware. [3, 4, 5, 6]

## How it Works

- Software Override: Your operating system or specialized tools can override the hardware address with a Locally Administered
  Address (LAA).
- The "Name Tag" Analogy: If a MAC address is like a permanent digital name tag, spoofing is like wearing a fake mustache or
  a temporary name tag over it.
- Local Scope: The spoofed address only exists on your local network (LAN); it is usually stripped away once your traffic
  passes through a router to the wider internet. [5, 7, 8, 9, 10]

## Common Reasons for Spoofing

- Privacy Protection: Many modern devices (like iPhones and Androids) automatically randomize their MAC addresses when
  scanning for Wi-Fi to prevent retailers or public hotspots from tracking your movements.
- Bypassing Restrictions:
- Time Limits: Resetting the timer on a "30 minutes free" public Wi-Fi hotspot.
  - MAC Filtering: Gaining access to a network that only allows specific "whitelisted" device IDs.
- Troubleshooting & Testing: IT professionals use it to simulate new hardware or test security configurations without
  physically swapping equipment.
- Software Licensing: Some legacy software licenses are tied to a specific MAC address; spoofing allows you to keep using the
  software if you replace your network card. [1, 5, 8, 11, 12, 13, 14]

## Is it Legal?

The act of spoofing a MAC address is generally legal for privacy or testing purposes. However, it becomes illegal if used to
commit a crime, such as: [5, 15, 16, 17]

- Impersonating another user to steal data (Man-in-the-Middle attacks).
- Accessing a network you are not authorized to use.
- Evading paid service fees (like hotel Wi-Fi). [5, 12, 16, 18, 19]
