To understand the **UDP (User Datagram Protocol)**, you first have to understand its "personality." Unlike the
detail-oriented and cautious TCP, UDP is built for speed and efficiency. It follows a "fire and forget" philosophy.

---

### The UDP Header

The UDP header is incredibly lightweight—only **8 bytes** in total. For comparison, a TCP header is at least 20 bytes. This
simplicity is why UDP is used for things like gaming and live streaming where every millisecond counts.

| Field                | Size    | Description                                                                |
| :------------------- | :------ | :------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Source Port**      | 16 bits | The port number on the sender's device.                                    |
| **Destination Port** | 16 bits | The port number on the receiver's device.                                  |
| **Length**           | 16 bits | The total length of the header plus the data.                              |
| **Checksum**         | 16 bits | Used to check if the data was corrupted during transit (optional in IPv4). |

After these 8 bytes, the actual **Data (Payload)** begins immediately.

---

### The UDP "Handshake"

Here is the twist: **There is no UDP handshake.**

UDP is a **connectionless** protocol. In TCP, there is a "Three-Way Handshake" ($SYN \rightarrow SYN-ACK \rightarrow ACK$) to
establish a connection. UDP skips all of that.

#### How it works instead:

1.  **The Sender:** Packages the data, attaches the 8-byte header, and sends it out into the internet.
2.  **The Receiver:** If it's listening on that port, it gets the data. If it’s not, it usually just ignores the packet (or
    the OS sends an ICMP error back).
3.  **No Confirmation:** The sender never asks "Are you ready?" and the receiver never says "I got it."

> **The Postal Analogy:** If **TCP** is like a **Registered Letter** (where you need a signature and a receipt), **UDP** is
> like a **Postcard**. You write the address, drop it in the box, and hope it gets there. If it gets lost in the rain, the
> post office doesn't call you to let you know.

---

### Why use UDP if it doesn't "handshake"?

Because handshakes take time (latency). By removing the handshake and the overhead of tracking packets, UDP achieves the
lowest possible delay. It is ideal for:

- **Online Gaming:** It's better to miss one frame of movement than to pause the whole game waiting for a "retransmission."
- **DNS:** Your computer just needs a quick answer for an IP address.
- **VoIP / Video Calls:** If a packet drops, you get a tiny bit of static, but the call stays in real-time.
