A spoofing attack is a situation where an attacker impersonates a trusted source—such as an email address, phone number,
website, or IP address—to deceive victims and gain an illegitimate advantage. It is essentially a digital "disguise" used to
build trust before an attacker carries out a more damaging act like stealing data, spreading malware, or committing financial
fraud. [1, 2, 3, 4]

## How Spoofing Works

Attackers manipulate technical identifiers to hide their true identity and mimic a legitimate entity. This process typically
involves: [5]

- Concealment: Cloaking their own identity using specialized tools or by altering data attributes.
- Mimicry: Imitating a trusted person or organization, such as a bank, a boss, or a government agency.
- Exploiting Trust: Leveraging the inherent trust that users or systems have for certain communication channels to bypass
  security defenses. [6, 7, 8, 9]

## Common Types of Spoofing (2026)

- Email Spoofing: Forging the "From" address or headers to make an email appear to be from a trusted contact. In 2026, AI is
  frequently used to mimic specific writing styles of executives.
- Caller ID & Voice Spoofing: Manipulating phone numbers to appear as a local or trusted contact. Advanced attacks now use AI
  deepfakes to clone real voices for highly convincing scams.
- Website Spoofing: Creating a near-identical copy of a legitimate site (e.g., a bank login) to harvest user credentials.
- IP & DNS Spoofing: Technical attacks where an attacker fakes an IP address to bypass network filters or corrupts DNS
  records to redirect users to malicious sites without their knowledge.
- GPS Spoofing: Broadcasting fake location signals to mislead navigation systems. By 2026, this has significantly impacted
  aviation and maritime operations. [3, 6, 8, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14]

## Spoofing vs. Phishing

While often used together, they are distinct concepts:

- Spoofing is the "Costume": It is the technical method of impersonation to appear legitimate.
- Phishing is the "Con": It is the actual social engineering attack that uses that fake identity to trick a victim into an
  action, like revealing a password or transferring money. [4, 15, 16]

## Key Warning Signs

- Urgency & Fear: Messages that demand immediate action or warn of dire consequences (e.g., "account suspended").
- Slight Inconsistencies: Subtle typos in domain names (e.g., paypa1.com instead of paypal.com) or sender addresses that
  don't match the supposed sender's name.
- Unusual Requests: Unexpected requests for sensitive information like passwords or PINs, which legitimate businesses will
  never ask for over email or phone. [17, 18, 19]
