Passive network attacks are "silent" intrusions where an attacker monitors or intercepts data as it travels across a network
without altering it or disrupting the system [1, 2]. Unlike active attacks (which change data or block access), passive
attacks are notoriously difficult to detect because they leave no footprint or system "hiccup" [2, 3].

## Common Types of Passive Attacks

- Packet Sniffing / Eavesdropping: The attacker uses software (like Wireshark) to capture and read data "packets" being sent
  over the network [2]. If the data isn't encrypted, they can see plain-text passwords, emails, and files [3, 4].
- Traffic Analysis: Even if the data is encrypted, the attacker observes the frequency, timing, and destination of messages
  [1]. This can reveal who you are talking to, how often, and even your physical location [1, 3].
- Footprinting (Reconnaissance): Collecting publicly available information about a network’s structure and IP addresses to
  plan a future active attack [4, 5].

## Key Characteristics

| Feature      | Passive Attack                         | Active Attack                          |
| ------------ | -------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| Detection    | Nearly impossible (no system changes)  | High (causes errors or downtime)       |
| Goal         | Confidentiality breach (theft of info) | Integrity/Availability breach (damage) |
| Tool Example | Wiretapping, Packet sniffers           | Ransomware, DDoS, Pharming             |

## How to Prevent Them

Since you cannot easily detect these attacks, you must focus on prevention:

- Strong Encryption: Always use protocols like HTTPS, TLS, and SSH. Even if the data is stolen, it will be unreadable [3, 5].
- VPNs: Encrypt all traffic leaving your device, making it impossible for a "sniffer" on a local network (like public Wi-Fi)
  to see your activity [5, 6].
- Avoid Public Wi-Fi: These networks are the most common environments for eavesdropping [5].
