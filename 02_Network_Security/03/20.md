Network security vulnerabilities are weaknesses or flaws in a network's design, implementation, or operation that can be
exploited by attackers to gain unauthorized access, disrupt services, or steal data. These "security gaps" generally fall
into three broad categories: hardware issues, software flaws, and human error. [1, 2, 3]

## Common Technical Vulnerabilities

- Outdated or Unpatched Software: Systems running old software versions often have known bugs with publicly available
  exploits. Simply installing updates can eliminate nearly half of these vulnerabilities.
- Firewall & System Misconfigurations: Incorrectly configured firewalls can leave network ports open or allow unauthorized IP
  addresses, rendering the primary barrier against intruders ineffective.
- Weak Authentication: Use of default credentials, single-factor authentication (missing MFA), and short or reused passwords
  provides an easy entry point for attackers.
- Insecure Protocols: Relying on legacy communication protocols (like mDNS, NBNS, or LLMNR) that lack encryption allows
  attackers to intercept data or spoof network traffic.
- IoT Device Exposure: Smart devices often operate with minimal security controls and default passwords, making them easy
  targets for building large-scale botnets. [1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9]

## Current Top Global Risks (2025)

According to the OWASP Top 10:2025 and industry reports, the most pressing vulnerabilities impacting modern networks include:

- Broken Access Control: The #1 risk, where failures to properly enforce user permissions allow attackers to access
  restricted data or administrative functions.
- Software Supply Chain Failures: A major surge in 2025, focusing on compromises in third-party dependencies, CI/CD
  pipelines, and automated update mechanisms.
- Cryptographic Failures: Use of weak hashing algorithms or failing to encrypt sensitive data in transit and at rest.
- Mishandling of Exceptional Conditions: A new category in 2025 where improper error handling reveals internal system info or
  allows attackers to trigger predictable crashes. [10, 11, 12, 13]

## The Human Element

Human-based vulnerabilities remain the most prevalent and difficult to secure: [1, 14]

- Social Engineering/Phishing: Deceptive emails or calls that trick employees into sharing credentials or clicking malicious
  links.
- Insider Threats: Risks posed by individuals with legitimate access—such as employees or contractors—whether their actions
  are malicious or purely negligent. [2, 4, 15]
