The **Diffie-Hellman Key Exchange** algorithm is a foundational method in cryptography that allows two parties to safely
establish a shared secret key over an insecure public channel (like the internet).

Once this shared secret is created, the two parties can use it to encrypt their subsequent communications using symmetric
cryptography.

---

## The Core Concept: An Analogy

Before diving into the math, it helps to understand the classic **paint analogy**:

1. **The Public Base:** Alice and Bob agree publicly on a starting paint color (e.g., **Yellow**). An eavesdropper (Eve) also
   knows this color.
2. **Secret Colors:** Alice and Bob each choose a secret color that only they know. Alice chooses **Blue**; Bob chooses
   **Red**.
3. **The Mix:** \* Alice mixes her secret Blue with the public Yellow to get **Light Blue**.

- Bob mixes his secret Red with the public Yellow to get **Orange**.

4. **The Exchange:** Alice sends her Light Blue mix to Bob. Bob sends his Orange mix to Alice. Eve sees these mixed colors
   but cannot easily separate them to find the original secret colors.
5. **The Final Shared Secret:** \* Alice adds her secret Blue to Bob's Orange mix.

- Bob adds his secret Red to Alice's Light Blue mix.
- Both end up with the exact same final color (**Muddy Brown**). Eve is left frustrated because she lacks the individual
  secret colors to recreate it.

## How the Mathematics Works

The actual algorithm relies on modular arithmetic and the difficulty of solving the **Discrete Logarithm Problem**.

### 1. Setup

Alice and Bob publicly agree on two large numbers:

- A prime number $p$
- A generator $g$ (a number that, when raised to powers, creates a wide distribution of results modulo $p$)

### 2. Private Keys

- Alice chooses a secret integer $a$.
- Bob chooses a secret integer $b$.

### 3. Public Keys

They calculate their public keys and send them to each other:

- Alice calculates $A = g^a \pmod p$ and sends $A$ to Bob.
- Bob calculates $B = g^b \pmod p$ and sends $B$ to Alice.

### 4. Computing the Shared Secret

Upon receiving the other person's public key, they perform a final calculation:

- Alice calculates the secret $s$ using Bob's public key:

$$s = B^a \pmod p$$

- Bob calculates the secret $s$ using Alice's public key:

$$s = A^b \pmod p$$

Because of the laws of exponents, both calculations yield the exact same result:

$$s = (g^b)^a \pmod p = (g^a)^b \pmod p = g^{ab} \pmod p$$

## A Quick Example with Small Numbers

> **Note:** In the real world, $p$ is hundreds of digits long, but let's use small numbers for simplicity.

1. **Public Agreement:** Let's say $p = 23$ and $g = 5$.
2. **Secret Choices:** Alice chooses $a = 4$. Bob chooses $b = 3$.
3. **Public Key Calculation:**

- Alice: $A = 5^4 \pmod{23} = 625 \pmod{23} = 4$
- Bob: $B = 5^3 \pmod{23} = 125 \pmod{23} = 10$

4. **The Exchange:** Alice sends **4** to Bob; Bob sends **10** to Alice.
5. **Shared Secret Calculation:**

- Alice calculates: $s = 10^4 \pmod{23} = 10000 \pmod{23} = 18$
- Bob calculates: $s = 4^3 \pmod{23} = 64 \pmod{23} = 18$

Both Alice and Bob successfully arrived at the shared secret key **18** without ever revealing their private keys ($4$ and
$3$).

---

## Why is it Secure?

The security relies on the **Discrete Logarithm Problem**. If an attacker knows $g$, $p$, and $g^a \pmod p$, it is
computationally incredibly difficult to figure out what $a$ is if $p$ is a massively large prime. It is a "one-way
function"—easy to calculate in one direction, but practically impossible to reverse within a reasonable timeframe using
modern computers.
