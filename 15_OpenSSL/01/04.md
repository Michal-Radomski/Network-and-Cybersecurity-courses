The **RSA algorithm** (named after its creators Rivest, Shamir, and Adleman) is one of the first and most widely used
asymmetric cryptographic systems in the world.

It solves a fundamental dilemma in security: **How can two people securely exchange a secret message without safely sharing a
secret password beforehand?**

RSA solves this by using a **KeyPair**:

1. **The Public Key:** Anyone can see this. It is used to **encrypt** (lock) the message.
2. **The Private Key:** Kept strictly secret by the owner. It is used to **decrypt** (unlock) the message.

---

## The Mailbox Analogy

Think of RSA like a public mailbox on the street:

- Anyone can walk up to the mailbox, drop a letter inside through the slot, and walk away. This represents the **Public
  Key**.
- However, once the letter is dropped inside, no one can reach back in to pull it out.
- Only the mail carrier holds the physical key required to open the back of the box and read the letters. This represents the
  **Private Key**.

---

## How RSA Works (The Simplified Math)

RSA relies on a branch of mathematics called **modular arithmetic** and a specific quirk of number theory: **it is incredibly
easy to multiply two giant prime numbers together, but exceptionally difficult to do the reverse (factoring) to find out what
those original numbers were.**

Here is the step-by-step breakdown of how keys are generated and used:

### 1. Generating the Keys

- **Step A:** Pick two very large, distinct prime numbers, $p$ and $q$. (In real life, these numbers are hundreds of digits
  long).
- **Step B:** Multiply them together to get $n$:

$$n = p \times q$$

The number $n$ is called the **modulus**. The length of $n$ (measured in bits) determines the key size.

- **Step C:** Pick a small number $e$ (often `65537`) that shares no common factors with $(p-1)(q-1)$.
- **The Public Key** is made up of the numbers **$(e, n)$**.
- **Step D:** Use a complex mathematical formula (the Extended Euclidean Algorithm) to calculate a secret number $d$, which
  acts as the mathematical inverse of $e$.
- **The Private Key** is made up of the numbers **$(d, n)$**.

Once $n$ and $d$ are calculated, the original prime numbers $p$ and $q$ are permanently destroyed.

### 2. Encryption (Locking)

If Alice wants to send a secret message $M$ to Bob, she converts her text into a number and uses Bob's public key $(e, n)$ to
calculate the scrambled ciphertext $C$:

$$C = M^e \pmod n$$

### 3. Decryption (Unlocking)

When Bob receives the scrambled message $C$, he uses his secret private key $d$ to recover the original message $M$:

$$M = C^d \pmod n$$

Because of the mathematical relationship between $e$ and $d$, raising the scrambled message to the power of $d$ cleanly
reverses the encryption, revealing Alice's original text.

---

## Why is RSA Secure?

The security of RSA rests entirely on the **Factoring Problem**.

An attacker trying to intercept Alice's message will know the Ciphertext ($C$) and Bob's Public Key ($e$ and $n$). To break
the encryption and find the secret private key ($d$), the attacker must figure out the original prime numbers $p$ and $q$
that were multiplied together to make $n$.

If $n$ is small (like 15), a computer instantly figures out that $3 \times 5 = 15$. But if $n$ is a modern **2048-bit or
4096-bit number** (a digit hundreds of numbers long), it would take a modern supercomputer hundreds of thousands of years of
continuous calculation to guess the correct prime factors.

---

## Limitations of RSA

While incredibly secure, RSA has two major drawbacks:

- **It is Slow:** Because it involves raising giant numbers to giant powers, it requires heavy processing power.
- **Large Key Sizes:** To stay ahead of increasingly fast computers, RSA keys have to get longer and longer. Today, an RSA
  key must be at least 2048 or 4096 bits to be safe, whereas newer algorithms like **ECC (Elliptic Curve Cryptography)** can
  provide the exact same level of safety with a key that is only 256 bits long.
