Imagine you are sending a highly confidential letter, and you want to make absolutely sure of two things:

1. Only the intended recipient can read it.
2. The recipient knows without a doubt that it actually came from you, not an impostor.

In the digital world, **Public Key Infrastructure (PKI)** is the framework that makes this security possible. It is a system
of roles, policies, hardware, software, and procedures needed to create, manage, distribute, use, store, and revoke digital
certificates and manage public-key encryption.

Here is a breakdown of how it works and why it keeps the internet secure.

---

## The Core Concept: Asymmetric Cryptography

At the heart of PKI is **asymmetric cryptography** (also known as public-key cryptography). Instead of using one password to
both lock and unlock a file, it uses a mathematically linked pair of keys:

- **The Public Key:** This key is shared with everyone. Anyone can use it to encrypt a message or verify a digital signature.
- **The Private Key:** This key is kept strictly secret by the owner. Only this key can decrypt messages encrypted by its
  corresponding public key, or create a digital signature.

> **The Mailbox Analogy:** Think of a public key like a mailbox slot. Anyone can walk up and drop a letter inside. However,
> once the letter is inside, only the owner with the private key (the mailbox key) can open it and read the contents.

---

## The Components of PKI

PKI isn't just the keys themselves; it's the entire ecosystem that proves those keys actually belong to who they say they do.
Without PKI, a hacker could easily pretend to be your bank and give you a fake public key.

PKI solves this using a few critical components:

### 1. Certificate Authority (CA)

The CA is the trusted third party—the "digital notary" of the internet (like DigiCert, Let's Encrypt, or GlobalSign). The
CA’s job is to verify the identity of individuals, websites, or companies. Once verified, the CA issues a **Digital
Certificate** binding that identity to their public key.

### 2. Registration Authority (RA)

The RA acts as the assistant to the CA. It handles the groundwork of verifying the identity of the entity requesting a
certificate. Once the RA is satisfied that you are who you claim to be, it tells the CA to issue the certificate.

### 3. Digital Certificates (X.509)

This is the digital passport. It contains the owner's public key, their identity details, expiration dates, and the digital
signature of the CA that issued it. When you visit a website with `https://` and see the padlock icon, your browser is
reading its digital certificate.

### 4. Certificate Revocation List (CRL) & OCSP

Sometimes certificates become compromised before they expire (e.g., a company loses control of its private key). PKI
maintains a "blacklist" called a **Certificate Revocation List (CRL)** or uses a real-time protocol called **OCSP (Online
Certificate Status Protocol)** so browsers know instantly if a certificate is no longer trustworthy.

## How PKI Works in Daily Life

Every time you log into a secure website, make an online purchase, or update your phone's software, PKI is working behind the
scenes.

Here is what happens when you visit a secure website (like `https://example.com`):

```
[Your Browser]                                       [Website Server]
      |                                                     |
      | ---- 1. "Hello! Let me see your certificate" -----> |
      |                                                     |
      | <--- 2. Sends Certificate (contains Public Key) --- |
      |                                                     |
      | -- 3. Browser verifies certificate with trusted CA  |
      |       (If valid, padlock icon appears)              |
      |                                                     |
      | -- 4. Encrypts a temporary session key with ------- |
      |       the website's Public Key and sends it         |
      |                                                     |
      | <--- 5. Server decrypts it using its Private Key -- |
      |                                                     |
[Secure encrypted connection established for your browsing session]

```

---

## Summary of Benefits

- **Confidentiality:** Ensures that data is encrypted and cannot be read by eavesdroppers.
- **Authentication:** Proves that the website or user you are communicating with is legitimate.
- **Integrity:** Guarantees that the data has not been altered or tampered with in transit.
- **Non-repudiation:** Ensures that a sender cannot deny sending a message, because only their unique private key could have
  created the digital signature.
