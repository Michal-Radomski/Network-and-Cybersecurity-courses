**SSL** (which stands for **Secure Sockets Layer**) is a deprecated cryptographic protocol designed to secure communications
over a computer network. It was most famously used to power the secure version of the web, turning `http://` into `https://`
and displaying a padlock icon in your browser.

While people still casually use the term "SSL" today, the internet actually migrated away from it years ago due to security
flaws. Its modern, highly secure successor is called **TLS** (Transport Layer Security).

---

## What Did SSL Actually Do?

Whenever you send data over an unencrypted network, it travels in "plaintext." This means anyone sitting between you and the
server (like a malicious actor on a public Wi-Fi network) can read your passwords, credit card numbers, or private messages.

SSL solved this by providing three core protections:

1. **Encryption:** It scrambles the data being sent so that only the intended recipient can unscramble and read it.
2. **Authentication:** It uses website certificates to prove that the server you are connecting to is actually who it claims
   to be (e.g., verifying that `bank.com` is your real bank, not a fake phishing site).
3. **Integrity:** It ensures that data cannot be secretly altered or tampered with while traveling across the internet.

---

## How the SSL/TLS Connection Works (The Handshake)

Before any secure data is sent, your browser and the website's server perform what is known as a **Handshake**. This happens
in the background in a fraction of a second:

- **Step 1: The Hello:** Your browser contacts the server, stating what versions of SSL/TLS and which **Cipher Suites** it
  supports.
- **Step 2: The Certificate:** The server responds with its **Website Certificate** (which contains its public key).
- **Step 3: Verification:** Your browser checks the certificate against trusted Certificate Authorities to ensure it is valid
  and authentic.
- **Step 4: Key Exchange:** Your browser generates a random session key (a symmetric password), encrypts it using the
  server's public key, and sends it over. The server decrypts it using its private key.
- **Step 5: Secure Session:** Now, both sides share a secret password that they use to encrypt all data moving forward.

---

## SSL vs. TLS: What’s the Difference?

You can think of SSL as the prototype and TLS as the polished, final product.

- **SSL 1.0, 2.0, and 3.0:** Developed by Netscape in the 1990s. Over time, cryptographers found severe vulnerabilities in
  these protocols (such as the POODLE attack). **SSL 3.0 was completely deprecated in 2015** and is no longer safe to use.
- **TLS 1.0 to 1.3:** The Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) took over the protocol and renamed it TLS to make it an open
  standard. The current standard used across the web today is **TLS 1.3**, which is faster and vastly more secure than
  original SSL.

> **Why do we still call it SSL?** > Branding. Because "SSL" became synonymous with internet security for over two decades,
> companies still sell "SSL Certificates" even though those certificates work perfectly fine with modern TLS protocols.
