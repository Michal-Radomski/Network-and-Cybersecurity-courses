In the context of the **OSI (Open Systems Interconnection) Model**, SSL and TLS do not fit perfectly into a single, clean
layer. This is because the OSI model was designed before modern internet protocols like TCP/IP became the standard.

However, when analyzing their functions, SSL and TLS operate **between Layer 4 (Transport) and Layer 7 (Application)**, most
commonly mapped to **Layer 5 (Session)** and **Layer 6 (Presentation)**.

---

## Detailed Breakdown by OSI Layer

Here is exactly how SSL/TLS interacts with the relevant layers of the OSI model:

### Layer 7: Application Layer (e.g., HTTP, FTP, SMTP)

- **The Relationship:** This is where your actual application data lives (like web traffic or emails). High-level protocols
  sit right on top of TLS. When HTTP runs over TLS, it becomes **HTTPS**.

### Layer 6: Presentation Layer

- **Why it fits here:** The Presentation layer is historically responsible for data formatting, translation, and—most
  importantly—**encryption and decryption**. Because TLS takes plaintext data from the Application layer and encrypts it into
  ciphertext (and vice versa), it heavily aligns with Layer 6.

### Layer 5: Session Layer

- **Why it fits here:** The Session layer manages the establishment, maintenance, and termination of connections between
  applications. The **TLS Handshake** is responsible for opening a secure session, authenticating the parties, negotiating
  cipher suites, and cleanly closing the connection.

### Layer 4: Transport Layer (TCP)

- **The Relationship:** TLS sits directly on top of the Transport Layer. It relies completely on **TCP** (Transmission
  Control Protocol) to reliably move packets back and forth. TLS cannot begin its handshake until a standard TCP connection
  (the TCP 3-way handshake) has already been established.

---

## The Concept of the "Glue" Layer

Because it spans multiple functions, security professionals often describe TLS as a **"Shim" or "Glue" layer** that sits
right between the Transport layer (Layer 4) and the Application layer (Layer 7).

It acts as a secure buffer: data drops down from the Application layer, gets managed and encrypted by TLS (Layers 5 and 6),
and is then handed off to TCP (Layer 4) to be sent across the network.
