Both website certificates and digital signatures are fundamental building blocks of internet security. They rely on the same
underlying math (asymmetric encryption, which we looked at earlier with your public/private key question), but they serve
different purposes.

---

## 1. Digital Signature: The "Wax Seal" of the Digital World

A **digital signature** is a mathematical technique used to validate the authenticity and integrity of a digital message,
document, or piece of software. It proves two things:

1. **Authenticity:** The message genuinely came from the claimed sender (signer).
2. **Integrity:** The message has not been altered or tampered with since it was signed.

### How it works:

- **Signing:** The sender takes their file, hashes it (turns it into a unique, fixed-length string of characters), and
  encrypts that hash using their **private key**. This encrypted hash is the digital signature.
- **Verifying:** The recipient receives the file and the signature. They decrypt the signature using the sender's **public
  key** to reveal the original hash. Then, they hash the file themselves. If the two hashes match, the signature is valid.

---

## 2. Website Certificate: The Digital Passport

A **website certificate** (commonly called an **SSL/TLS Certificate**) is a specific application of digital signatures. It is
a file installed on a website's server that proves the website's identity and enables encrypted communication (HTTPS) between
your browser and the site.

Think of it as a digital passport for a website. If you want to know if `google.com` is actually Google and not a hacker
spoofing the site, the certificate proves it.

### What is inside a Website Certificate?

- The domain name (e.g., `example.com`).
- The company/organization that owns the site.
- The website's **public key** (so your browser can encrypt data sent to it).
- An expiration date.
- **A Digital Signature** from a trusted third party called a **Certificate Authority (CA)** (like Let's Encrypt, DigiCert,
  or VeriSign).

---

## How They Work Together

When you visit a secure website (marked with a padlock icon `https://`):

1. **The Request:** Your browser connects to the website.
2. **The Passport Check:** The website hands your browser its **Website Certificate**.
3. **The Verification:** Your browser checks the **Digital Signature** on that certificate. Because your operating
   system/browser implicitly trusts the Certificate Authority that signed it, it verifies the signature using the CA's public
   key.
4. **The Secure Connection:** Once verified, your browser uses the public key inside that certificate to initiate a secure,
   encrypted session with the website.
