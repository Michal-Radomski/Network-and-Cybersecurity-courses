A **Certificate Signing Request (CSR)** is a structured block of encoded text that an applicant generates on their server and
sends to a Certificate Authority (CA) to apply for a digital certificate.

If a digital certificate is like a high-tech passport, the CSR is the official **passport application form**. It contains all
the information that will go onto your certificate, along with proof that you own the matching cryptographic keys.

---

## 1. What is Inside a CSR?

A CSR contains three main pieces of information:

### A. Your Organization's Identity Details

This is the plain-text information that identifies who owns the certificate. It uses the X.509 standard fields:

- **Common Name (CN):** The fully qualified domain name you want to secure (e.g., `www.example.com`).
- **Organization (O):** The legal name of your company or entity.
- **Organizational Unit (OU):** The department handling the certificate (e.g., "IT Security").
- **Locality/City (L):** The city where your organization is located.
- **State/Province (S):** The state or province.
- **Country (C):** The two-letter country code (e.g., `US`).

### B. Your Public Key

Before you generate a CSR, your server generates a **Key Pair** (a Private Key and a Public Key). The CSR bundles your newly
created **Public Key** inside it. This tells the CA, _"This is the public key I want you to vouch for."_ > ⚠️ **Crucial
Rule:** The **Private Key** is _never_ included in the CSR. It stays securely locked down on your server and is never sent to
the CA.

### C. A Digital Signature

To prevent anyone from tampering with the application while it travels over the internet, the server takes all the
information above and signs it using your server's **Private Key**. The CA uses this signature to verify that the CSR wasn't
altered in transit and that you actually possess the private key matching the public key inside the request.

To a human, a raw CSR file looks like a scrambled block of text encoded in Base64 (PEM format). It always begins and ends
with specific header tags:


## 3. How a CSR Fits into the PKI Workflow

1. **Generation:** You use a tool like OpenSSL on your web server to generate a Private Key and a CSR simultaneously.
2. **Submission:** You copy and paste the text block of the CSR into the application portal of a trusted CA (like DigiCert or
   Let's Encrypt).
3. **Validation:** The CA checks the identity details in the CSR to make sure you actually own the domain name.
4. **Signing & Issuance:** Once verified, the CA extracts your **Public Key** and **Identity Info** from the CSR, strips away
   your temporary digital signature, signs the data with the _CA's own_ private key, and sends back your completed **Digital
   Certificate**.
