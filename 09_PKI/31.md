**Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC)** is a highly efficient approach to public-key cryptography. Instead of relying on the
mathematical difficulty of factoring massive prime numbers (like older RSA algorithms do), ECC gets its security from the
complex mathematics of algebraic curves.

---

## 1. How Elliptic Curve Cryptography Works

ECC is based on a branch of mathematics called **The Elliptic Curve Discrete Logarithm Problem**.

Instead of multiplying huge numbers, ECC uses a mathematical formula to plot a specific curve on a graph. The simplified
equation for these curves looks like this:

$$y^2 = x^3 + ax + b$$

### The "Billiard Ball" Analogy

Imagine the curve is a custom-shaped pool table.

1. You take a ball at a starting location (Point $A$) and strike it across the table.
2. It bounces off the curved wall and hits another spot.
3. You reflect that point across the graph's horizontal axis to get a new point (Point $B$).
4. You repeat this skipping process a specific number of times (let's say 500 times).

Your **Private Key** is the secret number of times you skipped the ball (500). Your **Public Key** is the final coordinates
where the ball landed.

Because the math only moves forward easily, anyone who knows the final landing spot and the starting point has virtually zero
chance of calculating exactly how many times the ball bounced to get there.

---

## 2. Why ECC is Better Than RSA

The primary advantage of ECC is its efficiency. It provides the exact same level of cryptographic security as RSA, but with
drastically smaller key sizes.

- **Smaller Keys:** A 256-bit ECC key offers the same level of security as a massive 3072-bit RSA key.
- **Speed:** Smaller keys mean faster mathematical calculations. This translates to faster website loading times, quicker
  data transfers, and less CPU strain.
- **Low Power consumption:** Because the math requires less computing power, it is ideal for battery-dependent devices like
  smartphones, smartwatches, and Internet of Things (IoT) hardware.

---

## 3. What is P-384?

**P-384** (sometimes called secp384r1) is a specific, standardized elliptic curve defined by the National Institute of
Standards and Technology (NIST).

The "384" indicates the size of the key in bits.

### Why Use P-384?

While **P-256** (a 256-bit curve) is the most common standard for everyday web browsing and SSL/TLS certificates, P-384 is a
much heavier, enterprise-grade step up.

- **Top-Tier Security:** A 384-bit ECC key offers an incredible level of cryptographic strength (roughly equivalent to a
  massive **7680-bit RSA key**).
- **CNSA Compliance:** The Commercial National Security Algorithm (CNSA) suite requires P-384 for protecting Top Secret
  government and military data.
- **Future-Proofing:** While P-256 is perfectly safe for standard commercial applications today, P-384 is chosen by
  organizations that need maximum security longevity, ensuring data encrypted today remains unreadable to adversaries decades
  from now.
