Yes, **conceptually, every cryptographic key is just a big number.** Whether you are using a key for your Wi-Fi, an SSH
connection, a Bitcoin wallet, or a website's SSL certificate, computers process all of them as numbers.

However, how that number is used and how it looks to a human can vary. Here is how it breaks down:

---

## 1. To a Computer, Everything is a Number

Computers don't understand letters, symbols, or files—they only understand **binary (0s and 1s)**. When a computer looks at a
key, it sees a long string of binary digits. Any string of binary digits is mathematically just a base-2 number.

- A **128-bit key** is a number between $0$ and $2^{128}-1$.
- A **256-bit key** is a number between $0$ and $2^{256}-1$.

---

## 2. Why Don't Keys _Look_ Like Numbers?

If you open a key file on your computer, you usually won't see a standard base-10 number (like `1,492,384...`). That is
because raw numbers are highly inefficient for humans to copy, paste, or read. Instead, computers represent that big number
in different text formats:

### Hexadecimal (Base-16)

Often used for Wi-Fi keys or Bitcoin private keys. It uses numbers (0-9) and letters (A-F) to compress the binary number.

- _Example:_ `4A3F2B...` is just a human-readable shortcut for a massive binary number.

### Base64

Used for SSH keys and SSL certificates (like `.pem` files). It uses lowercase letters, uppercase letters, numbers, and
symbols to compress the big number even further.

- _Example:_ `tYg5+pQ2...`

---

## 3. The Only Real Exception: Passphrases

If you use a password or a passphrase (like _"CorrectHorseBatteryStaple"_) to encrypt a file, you might think, _"Aha! This
key is made of words, not numbers!"_ But even then, the computer doesn't use those words directly as the cryptographic key.
Instead, it runs your words through a **Key Derivation Function (KDF)** (like PBKDF2 or bcrypt). This math function takes
your password, scrambles it, and spits out—you guessed it—a **massive, fixed-length random number** to use as the actual
encryption key.

So, no matter how it looks on your screen, at the hardware layer, **every single cryptographic key is ultimately just a very
large number.**
