In a Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) system, **Certificate Authorities (CAs)** and **Registration Authorities (RAs)** are the
two core components responsible for managing digital certificates (like the SSL/TLS certificates that keep websites secure).

Think of them like a government passport office: one checks your paperwork, and the other actually prints and stamps your
passport.

---

## 1. Certificate Authority (CA)

The **Certificate Authority** is the ultimate trusted third party in PKI. It is the entity that actually issues, signs, and
revokes digital certificates.

### Key Responsibilities:

- **Issuing Certificates:** The CA digitally signs a certificate using its own private key, vouching that a specific public
  key belongs to a specific person or organization.
- **Maintaining the Certificate Revocation List (CRL):** If a certificate is compromised before its expiration date (e.g., a
  private key is stolen), the CA adds it to a public "blacklist" so browsers and systems know not to trust it.
- **Publishing Certificates:** The CA stores and publishes issued certificates in a public repository so others can access
  them.

> **In the passport analogy:** The CA is the main government printing office that manufactures the passport and stamps it
> with the official, counterfeit-proof state seal.

---

## 2. Registration Authority (RA)

The **Registration Authority** acts as the "front office" or the gatekeeper for the CA. The RA does not actually issue or
sign certificates itself; instead, it handles the administrative work of verifying who you are.

### Key Responsibilities:

- **Identity Verification:** The RA collects and checks the background information, identification documents, or company
  credentials of an applicant to ensure they are who they claim to be.
- **Validating Requests:** It reviews **Certificate Signing Requests (CSRs)** to make sure the technical details are correct.
- **Forwarding Approvals:** Once the RA is satisfied that the applicant's identity is legitimate, it sends an approved
  request to the CA, telling it, _"We checked them out, they are good to go. Please issue the certificate."_

> **In the passport analogy:** The RA is your local post office or clerk's office where you physically go to show your birth
> certificate and photo ID. They verify your identity, but they don't print the passport—they just forward your approved
> application to the main office.

---

## CA vs. RA: A Quick Comparison

| Feature                       | Certificate Authority (CA)                               | Registration Authority (RA)                                  |
| ----------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Primary Role**              | The "Issuer" and "Signer"                                | The "Verifier" and "Gatekeeper"                              |
| **Can it sign certificates?** | **Yes.** It uses its private key to create trust.        | **No.** It has no signing authority for public certificates. |
| **Interaction**               | Processes backend technical requests from the RA.        | Interacts directly with the user/applicant.                  |
| **Core focus**                | Security, cryptography, and maintaining the trust chain. | Identity verification and administrative workflows.          |
