When you visit a website, your web browser doesn't just blindly trust the digital certificate it receives. It performs a
rapid background verification process called checking the **Certificate Chain** to establish **Trust**.

Here is exactly how computers figure out if a certificate can be trusted.

---

## 1. The Concept of Trust in PKI

Trust in Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) is **hierarchical**. You don't have to trust every single website on the internet
individually; you only need to trust the **Certificate Authority (CA)** that vouched for them.

To make this work, every operating system (Windows, macOS, Linux) and web browser (Chrome, Safari, Firefox) comes
pre-installed with a **Root Store**. This is a built-in list of highly secure, heavily audited public keys and certificates
belonging to the world's major CAs (like DigiCert, IdenTrust, or Sectigo). These are called **Root Certificates**.

If a certificate can trace its lineage directly back to one of these pre-installed Root Certificates, your browser trusts it.

---

## 2. The Certificate Chain (Chain of Trust)

A Certificate Chain is an ordered list of certificates, containing an end-entity (website) certificate, one or more
intermediate certificates, and a root certificate.

Instead of a Root CA signing website certificates directly (which is too risky if a single private key is compromised), they
use a multi-tiered approach.

A typical chain looks like a three-tier pyramid:

### A. The Root Certificate (The Top)

- **Who it belongs to:** The Root CA.
- **Characteristics:** It is **self-signed**, meaning the certificate issuer and the subject are exactly the same. It uses a
  highly secure, offline master key.
- **Where it lives:** Pre-installed in your device's Root Store.

### B. The Intermediate Certificate (The Middle)

- **Who it belongs to:** A secondary department or specific branch of the CA.
- **Characteristics:** The Root CA signs the Intermediate CA's certificate. The Intermediate CA then uses _its_ key to sign
  the final website certificates. This acts as a buffer zone; if something goes wrong, the intermediate certificate can be
  revoked without destroying the Root CA's entire infrastructure.
- **Where it lives:** Sent by the web server along with the website's certificate.

### C. The Leaf / End-Entity Certificate (The Bottom)

- **Who it belongs to:** The specific website you are visiting (e.g., `google.com`).
- **Characteristics:** It cannot sign other certificates; its only job is to identify the server and encrypt data.

---

## 3. How the Browser Verifies the Chain (Walking the Chain)

When you connect to a secure website, the server hands your browser the **Leaf Certificate** and the **Intermediate
Certificate**.

Your browser then "walks up" the chain backward to verify it:

```text
[ Leaf Certificate ]
       | (Was signed by...)
       v
[ Intermediate Certificate ]
       | (Was signed by...)
       v
[ Root Certificate ] ---> Matches a trusted certificate inside your device's built-in Root Store!

```

1. The browser looks at the **Leaf Certificate** and sees it was signed by _Intermediate CA X_.
2. The browser examines the **Intermediate Certificate** to check its signature. It sees it was signed by _Root CA Y_.
3. The browser looks into its own internal **Root Store** for _Root CA Y_.
4. If _Root CA Y_ is found in the local database, the cryptographic signatures are verified all the way up. The chain is
   complete, a green lock icon (or secure connection status) is established, and the page loads.

If any link in this chain is broken, missing, or expired, the chain snaps, and your browser blocks the site with a
"Certificate Not Trusted" security warning.
