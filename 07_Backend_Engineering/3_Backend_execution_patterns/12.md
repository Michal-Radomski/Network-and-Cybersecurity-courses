Yes, absolutely. `LISTEN` is just one phase in the lifecycle of a network socket. TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) is a
"stateful" protocol, meaning it relies on a strict set of states to ensure data is sent and received reliably.

A socket transitions through various states depending on whether it is establishing a connection, actively transferring data,
or closing down.

---

## 1. Connection Establishment States

Before data can flow, the client and server must agree to connect via the three-way handshake.

- **`CLOSED`**: The default starting point. The socket exists but has no active connection and isn't listening.
- **`LISTEN`**: (Server only) The backend is waiting for an incoming connection request from a client.
- **`SYN-SENT`**: (Client only) The client has sent a `SYN` packet to the server and is waiting for a response.
- **`SYN-RECEIVED`**: (Server only) The server has received the client's `SYN`, replied with a `SYN-ACK`, and is waiting for
  the final `ACK` from the client.

---

## 2. The Active State

Once the handshake is complete, the socket enters the state where the actual work happens.

- **`ESTABLISHED`**: The connection is fully open. Both the client and the server can now read and write data (like sending
  HTTP requests and receiving JSON responses). This is where a socket spends most of its life.

---

## 3. Connection Termination States (The Four-Way Handshake)

Closing a connection is actually harder than opening one. Because TCP is "full-duplex" (both sides can talk at the same
time), each side must close its half of the connection independently. This results in a variety of closing states:

### If your backend initiates the shutdown (Active Close):

- **`FIN-WAIT-1`**: Your app sent a `FIN` (Finish) packet to the client, telling it, _"I am done sending data."_ Now it's
  waiting for the client to acknowledge it.
- **`FIN-WAIT-2`**: The client acknowledged your close request. Your backend is now waiting for the client to finish its own
  data transmission and send its own `FIN` packet.
- **`TIME-WAIT`**: The backend received the client's `FIN` and sent the final acknowledgment. The socket stays in `TIME-WAIT`
  for a brief period (typically 1 to 2 minutes, known as 2MSL) to ensure any stray network packets still bouncing around the
  internet don't accidentally get mixed up with a future connection on the same port.

### If the client initiates the shutdown (Passive Close):

- **`CLOSE-WAIT`**: The client sent a `FIN` packet. Your backend kernel acknowledged it, and now your application code is
  told, _"The client is done."_ The socket stays in `CLOSE-WAIT` until your application explicitly runs the `socket.close()`
  command.
- **`LAST-ACK`**: Your backend finally sends its own `FIN` packet to the client and is waiting for the absolute final
  acknowledgment before completely destroying the socket.

---

## Why these states matter to a Developer

You will usually see these states when debugging infrastructure issues using command-line tools like `netstat` or `ss`. Two
states in particular are notorious for causing backend issues:

- **Too many `TIME-WAIT` sockets:** If your backend connects to a database or external API thousands of times per second
  without reusing connections (no Keep-Alive), the OS will get flooded with sockets stuck in `TIME-WAIT`. Eventually, you
  will run out of available ephemeral ports, and your app will fail to make new connections.
- **Too many `CLOSE-WAIT` sockets:** This usually indicates a **bug in your code**. It means the client closed the
  connection, but your application failed to call `socket.close()` or clear the resource, causing a socket leak.
