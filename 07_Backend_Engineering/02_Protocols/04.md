Apps like **ESET Internet Security** perform very deep inspection of network traffic, going well beyond basic IP or port
checks. They utilize **Deep Packet Inspection (DPI)** and, yes, they **do decrypt data** to scan for threats hidden within
encrypted connections.

---

### **1. How Deeply Do They Inspect?**

ESET and similar security suites operate at the **Application Layer (Layer 7)** of the OSI model. Rather than just looking at
where a packet is going, they examine the **payload** (the actual content) of the packet.

- **Signature Analysis:** They compare the content against a database of known malware signatures.
- **Heuristic/Behavioral Scanning:** They look for suspicious patterns, such as a script trying to download an executable
  file from an unknown server (de Carné de Carnavalet & Mannan, 2016).
- **Protocol Filtering:** They ensure that traffic over a specific port actually matches the expected protocol (e.g.,
  ensuring HTTP traffic on port 80 isn't actually a hidden command-and-control signal for a botnet).

### **2. Do They Decrypt Data? (SSL/TLS Filtering)**

Yes. Since roughly **90% of web traffic** is now encrypted via HTTPS (SSL/TLS), antivirus software would be "blind" if it
didn't decrypt the data. ESET uses a process called **SSL/TLS Protocol Filtering** (or HTTPS Interception).

#### **How the Decryption Process Works:**

1. **The Proxy:** ESET acts as a "transparent proxy" sitting between your browser and the internet (Durumeric et al., 2017).
2. **Certificate Injection:** During installation, ESET adds its own **Root Certificate** to your computer's trusted store
   (de Carné de Carnavalet & Mannan, 2016).
3. **The Handshake:** When you visit an encrypted site, ESET intercepts the site's real certificate and generates a "copy"
   signed by ESET's own root certificate.
4. **Inspection:** Because your browser trusts ESET, it accepts the copy. This allows ESET to decrypt the traffic, scan the
   raw HTML/scripts for viruses, and then re-encrypt it before sending it to your browser.

---

### **3. Privacy and Security Trade-offs**

While this decryption is necessary for catching modern malware, it comes with specific trade-offs:

- **Privacy Concerns:** The software technically "sees" everything you do on a secure site, including passwords or bank
  details, during that millisecond of decryption. However, most suites have an **Exclusion List** for banking and financial
  sites to avoid inspecting highly sensitive data.
- **Security Risks:** If the antivirus software's decryption process is poorly coded, it could actually make your connection
  _less_ secure than the original browser connection (Durumeric et al., 2017).
- **Performance:** Decrypting and re-encrypting traffic requires significant CPU power, which is why some users notice a
  slight "lag" when SSL filtering is enabled.

> **Note:** You can usually find these settings in ESET under **Advanced Setup > Web and Email > SSL/TLS**. There, you can
> see the list of "Known Certificates" and choose which applications or sites should be excluded from inspection.
