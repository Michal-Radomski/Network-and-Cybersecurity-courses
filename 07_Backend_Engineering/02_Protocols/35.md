To understand how real-time communication (like WebRTC audio/video calls) works on the internet, you have to look at **ICE**
and **SDP**.

While **SDP** is the protocol used to negotiate _what_ kind of media two devices want to send to each other, **ICE** is the
framework used to figure out _how_ to physically route that data between them through the maze of routers and firewalls.

They work hand-in-hand to establish a connection. Here is how they break down.

---

## 1. SDP (Session Description Protocol)

SDP is not a transport protocol; it is a **text-based format** used by devices to describe their multimedia capabilities.
Think of it as a specialized resume or a "proposal" that a device sends to a peer.

When you start a video call, your browser generates an SDP object that details:

- What types of media it supports (audio, video, data).
- What codecs it can decode (e.g., VP8, H.264 for video; Opus for audio).
- The expected formats, bitrates, and timing information.
- Security keys for encrypting the media.

### The Offer/Answer Model

SDP operates on an **Offer/Answer** mechanism.

1. **The Offer:** Peer A creates an SDP "Offer" outlining its capabilities and sends it to Peer B.
2. **The Answer:** Peer B reviews the offer, compares it with its own capabilities, generates an SDP "Answer" (e.g., _"I also
   support VP8 and Opus, let's use those"_), and sends it back to Peer A.

> **Note:** SDP packets cannot just float across the internet on their own. They must be transported between peers using an
> external mechanism called a **Signaling Server** (usually a WebSocket or HTTP connection handled by the app developer).

---

## 2. ICE (Interactive Connectivity Establishment)

While SDP handles the _media parameters_, ICE handles the _network connectivity_. As established by protocols like STUN and
TURN, finding a direct path between two devices behind NATs and firewalls is incredibly difficult.

ICE is a framework that automates this discovery. It systematically finds all possible ways two devices can talk to each
other, tests them, and selects the most efficient path.

### ICE Candidates

ICE works by gathering **ICE Candidates**. A candidate is simply a combination of an IP address, a port, and a transport
protocol (usually UDP) that _might_ allow a peer to receive data. ICE gathers three types of candidates:

1. **Host Candidates:** The internal, private IP address and port assigned directly to the device's network card (e.g.,
   `192.168.1.15:50002`). This works if both peers are on the exact same local network.
2. **Server Reflexive Candidates (STUN):** The public IP and port assigned to the device's router by a STUN server. This is
   used for direct P2P connections across the internet.
3. **Relay Candidates (TURN):** The IP and port assigned by a TURN server. If all direct paths fail due to strict symmetric
   NATs, this relay path is used as a fallback.

### The ICE Process

- **Gathering:** Both peers talk to their network interfaces and STUN/TURN servers to gather as many candidates as possible.
- **Exchange:** The candidates are appended to the SDP or sent dynamically (via a technique called _Trickle ICE_) to the
  remote peer.
- **Connectivity Checks:** Once Peer A has Peer B's candidates, it starts pairing them up (Host-to-Host, Host-to-STUN,
  STUN-to-STUN) and sends rapid "STUN check" packets to see which pairs actually let traffic through.
- **Nomination:** ICE ranks the working pairs by efficiency (Direct P2P is ranked highest; TURN relays are ranked lowest).
  The best working path is selected, and the media starts flowing.

---

## How ICE and SDP Work Together

To tie it all together, think of setting up a business meeting between two executives (Peer A and Peer B) who speak different
languages and live in different secure buildings:

- **SDP is the Agenda:** Peer A sends a text document saying, _"I want to discuss project X, I speak English and Spanish, and
  I want to meet on Tuesday."_ Peer B responds, _"I also speak English, so let's use that. Tuesday works."_
- **ICE is the Logistics:** Simultaneously, their assistants look at map routes, security gate codes, and subway lines to
  find the physical path for them to meet. They exchange a list of options (_"We could meet in Lobby 1, or via the Public
  Square, or use a middleman courier"_). They test the doors, find the fastest open route, and send the executives through.

In software, the **SDP Offer/Answer** includes the **ICE Candidates**, ensuring that by the time both browsers agree on
_what_ they are streaming, they also know exactly _where_ to send the packets.
