To decrypt SSL/TLS traffic, a Next-Generation Firewall (NGFW) has to perform what is known as **SSL Inspection** (or "SSL
Break and Inspect"). It essentially acts as a "man-in-the-middle," but in a controlled, authorized way.

The firewall doesn't just "get" the original certificate from the website you are visiting. Instead, it uses a **CA
(Certificate Authority) Certificate** that you install on it.

Here is the step-by-step breakdown of how the certificate process works:

---

### 1. The Setup: The "Sub-CA" Certificate

For the firewall to decrypt traffic, the administrator must first generate or upload a **CA Certificate** onto the NGFW.

- This certificate must be **trusted** by every device on the network (laptops, phones, etc.).
- Usually, the IT department pushes this certificate out to all company computers via specialized software (like Active
  Directory/Group Policy).

### 2. The Request: Client to Website

When you try to visit a secure site (like `https://bank.com`), your browser sends a request. The NGFW intercepts this
request.

### 3. The Interception: The "Forged" Certificate

This is the clever part. The firewall connects to the real website on your behalf and looks at the site's real certificate.
Then:

1. The firewall **mimics** the website's certificate.
2. It creates a "copy" of the `bank.com` certificate but signs it with its **own private CA key**.
3. It sends this "forged" certificate back to your browser.

### 4. The Validation: Why doesn't your browser complain?

Normally, if a device sits in the middle and changes a certificate, your browser shows a huge red **"Your connection is not
private"** warning.

However, because your IT department already installed the firewall’s **Root CA** into your computer's "Trusted Root Store,"
your browser looks at the forged certificate, sees it was signed by the firewall, and says, "I trust the firewall, so I trust
this certificate."

### 5. The Decryption: Deep Packet Inspection

Now that the encrypted "tunnel" is established between your computer and the firewall:

- The firewall **decrypts** the data as it arrives.
- It scans the data for viruses, malware, or leaked company secrets (Layer 7 inspection).
- It then **re-encrypts** the data and sends it out to the actual website.

---

### Challenges with SSL Decryption

| Challenge               | Impact                                                                                                                                                 |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Privacy**             | Firewalls are usually configured to **bypass** decryption for sensitive categories like Finance or Healthcare to protect user privacy.                 |
| **Performance**         | Decrypting and re-encrypting traffic in real-time is extremely CPU-intensive and can slow down the network.                                            |
| **Certificate Pinning** | Some apps (like certain mobile apps) are hard-coded to only trust one specific certificate. These apps will break if a firewall tries to inspect them. |
| **Hardware**            | Many NGFWs use specialized "ASIC" chips specifically to handle the math required for high-speed SSL decryption.                                        |

### Summary of the "Certificate" types involved:

- **The Original Certificate:** Owned by the website (e.g., Google's cert).
- **The Firewall's CA Certificate:** The "Master Key" used by the firewall to sign new, temporary certificates.
- **The Mimicked Certificate:** The temporary certificate generated by the firewall for a specific session to trick the
  browser into an encrypted connection with the firewall instead of the website.
