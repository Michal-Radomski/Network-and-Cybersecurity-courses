In computer networking, a **handshake** is an automated process where two devices (like your computer and a server) establish
the rules of communication before they start exchanging actual data.

Just like a real-world handshake can signal agreement, trust, and a formal beginning to a meeting, a digital handshake allows
devices to introduce themselves, verify each other's identity, and agree on protocols, encryption levels, and transfer
speeds.

There are different types of handshakes depending on the layer of networking, but the two most famous are the **TCP
Handshake** (for connection) and the **TLS Handshake** (for security).

---

## 1. The TCP Three-Way Handshake (Connection)

The Transmission Control Protocol (TCP) uses a **Three-Way Handshake** to establish a reliable connection over the internet.
It ensures that both the sender and receiver are online and ready to transmit data.

It follows three simple steps using specific flags inside the network packets:

1. **SYN (Synchronize):** The client sends a packet to the server with a "SYN" flag. Essentially, it says: _"Hi, I want to
   connect with you. Here is my starting sequence number for tracking data."_
2. **SYN-ACK (Synchronize-Acknowledgment):** The server receives the request and responds with both a "SYN" and an "ACK"
   flag. It says: _"I received your request (ACK), and I also agree to connect with you (SYN). Here is my starting sequence
   number."_
3. **ACK (Acknowledgment):** The client receives the server's response and sends one final "ACK" packet back. It says: _"Got
   it! We are now officially connected and ready to send data."_

---

## 2. The TLS Handshake (Security & Encryption)

Once a connection is established via TCP, if you are visiting a secure website (`https://`), a **TLS (Transport Layer
Security) Handshake** immediately takes place. This handshake does not just open a channel; it encrypts it.

During a modern TLS 1.3 handshake, the following happens in just one round-trip:

1. **Client Hello:** The browser sends a list of cryptographic algorithms (Cipher Suites) it supports and a random string of
   data.
2. **Server Hello & Certificate:** The server picks the best cryptographic algorithm, sends back its own random data, and
   shares its **Digital Certificate** to prove its identity (ensuring you aren't talking to a fake imposter website).
3. **Key Generation:** Using a mathematical trick called a Diffie-Hellman key exchange, both sides use the shared random data
   to independently calculate the exact same secret **Session Key**.

From this point on, all data sent back and forth is completely encrypted using that secret key.

---

## Why are Handshakes Necessary?

Without handshakes, orderly internet communication would collapse for a few major reasons:

- **Synchronization:** Computers need to agree on where a file starts and finishes. Handshakes allow them to synchronize
  packet sequence numbers so data doesn't arrive as a scrambled mess.
- **Capability Matching:** One computer might be a powerful server capable of processing gigabits of data, while the other
  might be an old smartphone. The handshake allows them to negotiate a speed and window size both can handle.
- **Security:** The handshake ensures that sensitive data (like passwords or credit cards) is never sent over the open web
  until a secure, encrypted tunnel is fully built and verified.
