```python
def to_bin(ip):
    return '.'.join([format(int(x), '08b') for x in ip.split('.')])

mask = "255.255.255.0"
ip = "192.168.1.3"

mask_bin = to_bin(mask)
ip_bin = to_bin(ip)

mask_parts = [int(x) for x in mask.split('.')]
ip_parts = [int(x) for x in ip.split('.')]
result_parts = [m & i for m, i in zip(mask_parts, ip_parts)]
result = '.'.join(map(str, result_parts))
result_bin = to_bin(result)

print(f"{mask}  -> {mask_bin}")
print(f"{ip}    -> {ip_bin}")
print(f"{result} -> {result_bin}")



```

```text
255.255.255.0  -> 11111111.11111111.11111111.00000000
192.168.1.3    -> 11000000.10101000.00000001.00000011
192.168.1.0 -> 11000000.10101000.00000001.00000000


```

This calculation is how a computer determines its **Network Address**. It uses a mathematical operation called a **Bitwise
AND**.

In networking, your computer needs to know if another IP address is on the same local network or out on the internet. To
figure this out, it takes the **Subnet Mask** ($255.255.255.0$) and your **IP Address** ($192.168.1.3$) and "ANDs" them
together.

### 1. Convert to Binary

Computers don't see decimals; they see bits ($1$s and $0$s). First, we convert the octets:

- **Subnet Mask ($255.255.255.0$):** $11111111 . 11111111 . 11111111 . 00000000$
- **IP Address ($192.168.1.3$):** $11000000 . 10101000 . 00000001 . 00000011$

### 2. Perform the Bitwise AND

The rule for an **AND** operation is simple: the result is $1$ **only** if both bits are $1$. Otherwise, the result is $0$.

Let's look at it bit-by-bit:

$$
\begin{array}{rcccc}
\text{Subnet Mask:} & 11111111 & 11111111 & 11111111 & 00000000 \\
\text{IP Address:} & 11000000 & 10101000 & 00000001 & 00000011 \\
\hline
\text{Result (AND):} & \mathbf{11000000} & \mathbf{10101000} & \mathbf{00000001} & \mathbf{00000000}
\end{array}
$$

### 3. Convert back to Decimal

Now we turn those binary chunks back into the numbers we recognize:

- $11000000 = \mathbf{192}$
- $10101000 = \mathbf{168}$
- $00000001 = \mathbf{1}$
- $00000000 = \mathbf{0}$

**Result:** $192.168.1.0$

---

### Why is this useful?

This result ($192.168.1.0$) is the **Network ID**.

- The **Subnet Mask** acts like a filter. The parts where the mask is all $1$s ($255$) tell the computer: _"This is the name
  of the neighborhood."_ _ The parts where the mask is $0$s tell the computer: _"This is the specific house number (the
  host)."\*

By performing this calculation, the computer strips away the "house number" ($3$) and is left with the "neighborhood name"
($192.168.1.0$). If it wants to talk to $192.168.1.50$, it performs the same math, sees the neighborhood matches, and knows
it can just shout across the local wire instead of sending the data to the router to go to the internet.
