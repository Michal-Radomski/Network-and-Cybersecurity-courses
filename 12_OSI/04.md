We have reached the foundation of the OSI model: **Layer 2 (Data Link)** and **Layer 1 (Physical)**. These are the hardware
layers. While the upper layers deal with abstract data and routing across the global internet, Layers 2 and 1 are responsible
for actually moving raw binary data across physical cables, switches, and radio waves.

---

## Layer 2: The Data Link Layer

The **Data Link Layer** establishes a reliable link between two _directly connected_ nodes. It takes the packets from Layer 3
and packages them into **frames**. While Layer 3 uses IP addresses to find networks, Layer 2 uses physical hardware
addresses—known as **MAC (Media Access Control) addresses**—to find specific devices on the local network.

- **Primary Function:** Transferring data between two devices on the same network.
- **What it does:**
- **Framing:** Encapsulating network packets into frames, adding a header (with MAC addresses) and a trailer (for error
  checking).
- **Physical Addressing:** It reads the MAC address embedded in your computer’s network card.
- **Error Detection:** It uses a trailer called a Frame Check Sequence (FCS) to detect if data was corrupted while traveling
  across the wire. If corrupted, the frame is dropped.

- **Common Technologies & Devices:** \* **Ethernet** and **Wi-Fi** (802.11)
- **Network Switches** and **Network Interface Cards (NICs)**

---

## Layer 1: The Physical Layer

The **Physical Layer** is the lowest layer of the OSI model. It is completely hands-on, dealing with the actual physical
hardware, electrical signals, and media that transmit raw data. It doesn't understand complex data structures, IP addresses,
or web pages—it only understands **bits** (1s and 0s).

- **Primary Function:** Transmitting raw unstructured bitstreams over a physical medium.
- **What it does:**
- **Signal Representation:** It defines how digital 1s and 0s are converted into electrical voltages (copper cables), light
  pulses (fiber-optic cables), or radio waves (Wi-Fi).
- **Data Rate:** It defines the transmission speed (e.g., 1 Gbps vs. 10 Gbps).
- **Physical Topologies:** It defines how cables and devices are physically laid out (e.g., star, bus, or ring topologies)
  and the shapes of mechanical plugs and connectors.

- **Common Hardware:** \* **Cables:** Cat6 Ethernet, Fiber-optic cords, Coaxial cables.
- **Connectors:** RJ-45 plugs.
- **Devices:** Hubs, repeaters, and modems.
