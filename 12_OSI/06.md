**PPPoE**, which stands for **Point-to-Point Protocol over Ethernet**, is a network protocol used to encapsulate **PPP**
frames inside **Ethernet** frames.

To understand why it exists, think of it as a hybrid. Pure PPP was designed for old dial-up connections where two devices
talked directly over a phone line. Ethernet, on the other hand, was designed for local networks where multiple devices share
the same physical wire. PPPoE bridges the gap: it allows ISPs to use the security, authentication, and management features of
PPP over a modern, multi-user Ethernet network.

It is most commonly used by Internet Service Providers (ISPs) to connect home routers to their broadband networks (especially
via DSL or certain Fiber setups).

---

## Why Do ISPs Use PPPoE?

If Ethernet is already fast and everywhere, why add PPPoE on top of it? Pure Ethernet lacks built-in features that ISPs need
to run a business. PPPoE adds those features back in:

- **Authentication:** ISPs can require a username and password before allowing a device onto the internet. If you haven't
  paid your bill, they can simply disable your login.
- **Access Control & Tracking:** It allows the ISP to track your session, see how long you’ve been connected, and monitor
  data usage.
- **IP Address Allocation:** It makes it easy for the ISP to dynamically assign a public IP address to your router when it
  logs in.

---

## How PPPoE Works (The Discovery and Session Stages)

A PPPoE connection goes through two distinct phases before you can start browsing the web:

### 1. The Discovery Stage

Because Ethernet is a broadcast medium (one device talking to many), your home router first needs to find the ISP's access
concentrator (the server handling connections). It does this through a four-step handshake:

- **PADI (PPPoE Active Discovery Initiation):** Your router broadcasts a message to the local network asking, _"Are there any
  ISP servers out there?"_
- **PADO (PPPoE Active Discovery Offer):** One or more ISP servers reply with their names and MAC addresses: _"I'm here, and
  I can take your connection."_
- **PADR (PPPoE Active Discovery Request):** Your router chooses the best server and sends a direct message: _"Great, I want
  to establish a connection with you."_
- **PADS (PPPoE Active Discovery Session-confirmation):** The ISP server confirms, generates a unique **Session ID**, and the
  tunnel is officially open.

### 2. The Session Stage

Once the Session ID is established, the discovery phase ends, and the actual PPP data transfer begins.

- The router sends its login credentials (username/password) using CHAP or PAP authentication.
- The ISP verifies the credentials and assigns an IP address.
- From this point forward, all your internet traffic is wrapped in a PPPoE frame and sent straight through the tunnel to the
  ISP.

---

## The PPPoE Frame Structure

Because a PPPoE header has to be stuffed inside a standard Ethernet frame, it takes up some extra space.

A standard Ethernet frame has a Maximum Transmission Unit (MTU) of **1500 bytes**. However, the PPPoE header takes up 6
bytes, and the PPP protocol ID takes up 2 bytes.

Because of this **8-byte overhead**, the maximum packet size inside a PPPoE network is reduced from 1500 bytes down to **1492
bytes**. This is why you will often see "MTU: 1492" in your home router's advanced settings if your ISP uses PPPoE.

### Summary Table

| Feature                   | Standard Ethernet          | PPPoE                                       |
| ------------------------- | -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| **Authentication**        | None built-in              | Supported (Username/Password via CHAP/PAP)  |
