While **Wi-Fi Protected Setup (WPS)** was created to make connecting devices to a router easy—usually by just pressing a
button or entering a short PIN—it introduces a massive security vulnerability into your network.

Here is exactly why WPS is dangerous and why it should almost always be disabled.

---

## 1. The Flaw in the 8-Digit PIN

The primary danger of WPS lies in its **PIN-based authentication** method.

A standard WPS PIN is an 8-digit number (e.g., `12345670`). Mathematically, you would think guessing an 8-digit number would
take up to 100 million attempts ($10^8$). However, the WPS protocol design makes a critical mistake in how it verifies this
PIN:

- **Split Verification:** When a device tries to connect via WPS, the router checks the first 4 digits **separately** from
  the last 4 digits.
- **The Math Drop:** This means an attacker only has to guess a 4-digit number (10,000 possibilities) and then a 3-digit
  number (the 8th digit is just a checksum, leaving 1,000 possibilities).
- **Total Attempts:** Instead of 100 million combinations, the problem is reduced to just **11,000 combinations**.

---

## 2. Vulnerability to Brute-Force Attacks

Because 11,000 combinations is an incredibly small number for a computer to guess, an attacker within range of your Wi-Fi can
use free, automated hacking tools (like _Reaver_ or _Bully_) to try every single combination.

- **The Timeline:** A standard computer or smartphone can cycle through these combinations in a matter of **a few hours**.
- **The Reward:** Once the tool guesses the WPS PIN, the router hands over the **actual Wi-Fi password** in plain text. Even
  if you have a massive, un-crackable, 30-character WPA2 password, WPS completely bypasses it.

---

## 3. "Pixie Dust" Attacks (Instant Cracking)

In 2014, security researchers discovered an even worse vulnerability affecting many routers, known as the **Pixie Dust
attack**.

Some router manufacturers used poor randomization (low entropy) when generating the cryptographic nonces used during the WPS
handshake. Because of this, an attacker doesn't even need to wait hours to brute-force the PIN. They can intercept a single
connection attempt, perform the math offline, and calculate your WPS PIN **in less than a second**.

---

## What Should You Do?

> **Disable WPS entirely.** Log into your router's admin settings page (usually by typing `191.168.0.1` or `192.168.1.1` into
> a web browser) and look for the WPS setting. Turn it off.

### What about the physical WPS button?

If your router has a physical button on the back, pressing it temporarily opens a 2-minute window for a device to connect
without a password. While this "Push Button Connect" (PBC) method is vastly more secure than the PIN method because it
requires physical access, **many routers do not allow you to disable the PIN method while keeping the button method active**.
Disabling WPS completely is the safest bet.
