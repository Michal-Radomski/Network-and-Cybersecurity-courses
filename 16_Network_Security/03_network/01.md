**Network isolation** is a cybersecurity strategy that divides a computer network into smaller, distinct, and disconnected
segments. The core idea is simple: if one device or section of your network gets compromised by malware or a hacker, the
isolation prevents the threat from moving "laterally" to the rest of the network.

Think of it like the bulkheads in a submarine. If a leak occurs in one compartment, that door slams shut to isolate the
water, preventing the entire submarine from sinking.

---

## Why is Network Isolation Used?

- **Containment:** If a laptop on a network gets infected with ransomware, network isolation ensures the ransomware cannot
  spread to the main database servers.
- **Security for Untrusted Devices:** Smart home gadgets (IoT devices like smart bulbs, security cameras, or TVs) are
  notoriously easy to hack. Isolating them on their own segment keeps your personal computers safe.
- **Compliance and Privacy:** Businesses use isolation to separate systems that handle sensitive data—like credit card
  processing or medical records—from the general corporate network.

---

## Common Methods of Network Isolation

There are different levels of isolation depending on how secure you need the network to be, ranging from software
configurations to physical separation.

### 1. VLANs (Virtual Local Area Networks)

This is the most common method used in both corporate networks and advanced home networks. Instead of buying separate routers
and switches for different departments, a single piece of hardware is split logically using software.

- _Example:_ A business might have VLAN 10 for the Finance department and VLAN 20 for Guest Wi-Fi. Devices on the Guest Wi-Fi
  cannot see or talk to devices in Finance, even though they plug into the same physical switch.

### 2. Subnetting and Firewalls

Network administrators divide an IP address range into smaller pieces called **subnets**. They then place a firewall between
those subnets. The firewall acts as a strict border guard, checking every packet of data trying to cross from one subnet to
another and blocking unauthorized traffic.

### 3. Air Gapping (Physical Isolation)

This is the ultimate, most secure form of network isolation. An **air-gapped** system is physically disconnected from all
other networks, including the internet. There are no cables, no Wi-Fi signals, and no cellular connections linking it to the
outside world.

- _Example:_ Nuclear power plant control systems, military command networks, and highly secure backup servers are often
  air-gapped. To transfer data to an air-gapped system, someone must physically walk over to it with an encrypted USB drive.

---

## Network Isolation at Home

You likely use network isolation every day without realizing it. Most modern home routers offer a **Guest Network** feature.

When you enable a Guest Network for friends visiting your house, the router creates an isolated segment. Your guests get
access to the internet, but they are completely blocked from seeing your home storage servers, your private computers, or
your smart home devices.
