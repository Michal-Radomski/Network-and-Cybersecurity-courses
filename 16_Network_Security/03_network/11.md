If you're asking about **email tracking and hacking**, these are two very different topics.

**Email tracking** refers to monitoring whether an email has been opened or a link has been clicked. It's commonly used in
marketing and sales. Typical methods include:

- Invisible tracking pixels that load when an email is opened.
- Unique links that record when they're clicked.
- Delivery and bounce notifications from email providers.

You can reduce email tracking by:

- Disabling automatic loading of remote images.
- Using email clients with privacy protections.
- Being cautious about clicking links in unexpected emails.

**Email hacking** generally means gaining unauthorized access to someone's email account, which is illegal and unethical. If
your interest is in protecting your own account, good security practices include:

- Using a strong, unique password.
- Enabling two-factor authentication (2FA).
- Watching for phishing emails that try to steal your credentials.
- Keeping your devices and software up to date.

Neither is inherently more secure—the answer depends on the provider, the client, and how you use them. Each has different
security trade-offs.

| Web email                                                                          | Email client                                                                           |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Runs in your browser, so you don't install extra software.                         | Stores and manages your email through a dedicated app.                                 |
| Security updates happen automatically on the provider's servers.                   | You need to keep the app updated yourself.                                             |
| Less likely to leave copies of email on shared computers if you sign out properly. | May store emails locally, which can be a risk if your device is compromised or stolen. |
| Relies on browser security and protection against phishing.                        | Can support encryption, offline access, and advanced security features.                |

In general:

- **Web email is often the safer default** for most people because providers automatically update the service, enforce
  security features, and don't require you to configure protocols like IMAP or POP.
- **A well-maintained email client can be equally secure** if:
  - it's kept up to date,
  - your operating system is secure,
  - your email provider uses encryption (TLS),
  - you enable two-factor authentication (2FA),
  - and your device is protected with disk encryption and a strong login.

The biggest security risks usually aren't whether you use webmail or a client. They're:

1. Phishing attacks.
2. Weak or reused passwords.
3. Malware on the device.
4. Lack of 2FA.
5. Unpatched software.

If your priority is **maximum security**, a good approach is:

- Use a reputable email provider.
- Enable 2FA (preferably with a security key or authenticator app).
- Keep your browser, operating system, and apps updated.
- Use a password manager with a unique password for your email.
- Be cautious with links and attachments.
