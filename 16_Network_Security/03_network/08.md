The short answer is **no, not significantly.** While MAC filtering sounds like a great security measure on paper, in reality,
it provides only a very thin layer of "security through obscurity." It might keep your tech-illiterate neighbors off your
Wi-Fi, but it will not stop a determined hacker for more than a few minutes.

Here is why MAC filtering is mostly an illusion of security:

---

## 1. MAC Addresses Are Broadcast in Plain Text

Every network card has a unique hardware identifier called a MAC (Media Access Control) address. When your phone or laptop
connects to your Wi-Fi router, it must send its MAC address through the air so the router knows where to send data.

Even if your Wi-Fi network is heavily encrypted, **MAC addresses are sent in plain text**. Anyone within physical range of
your Wi-Fi using a free network sniffing tool (like Wireshark) can see the MAC addresses of the devices currently connected
to your router.

---

## 2. MAC Spoofing is Incredibly Easy

Once an attacker intercepts a legitimate MAC address that your router allows, they can easily change their own device's MAC
address to match it. This process is called **MAC spoofing**.

- It requires no advanced coding skills.
- Most operating systems (Windows, macOS, Linux) allow users to change their MAC address natively in the settings.
- Once the attacker spoofs an allowed MAC address, the router welcomes them onto the network, completely bypassing the
  filter.

---

## 3. It Causes a Management Nightmare

If you enable MAC filtering, you have to manually log into your router and enter the unique MAC address of every new device
you buy, or any device a guest wants to connect.

Furthermore, modern smartphones (iOS and Android) now use a privacy feature called **MAC Randomization**. They automatically
change their MAC addresses periodically to prevent tracking. If this feature is active, your phone will randomly lock itself
out of your own home Wi-Fi until you update the router's settings again.

---

## What Should You Do Instead?

Instead of wasting time setting up a MAC filter, focus your energy on security measures that actually work:

- **Use WPA2 (AES) or WPA3:** Strong encryption is your primary defense.
- **Create a Strong Password:** A long, complex passphrase stops attackers from cracking your encryption.
- **Set Up a Guest Network:** Put smart home devices and visitors on a separate network so they can't access your main
  devices.
