Configuring your Wi-Fi securely is one of the best ways to protect your personal data and keep unauthorized users off your
network.

Here is a step-by-step checklist to lock down your home Wi-Fi router.

---

## 1. Change the Router’s Default Admin Password

Every router comes with a default username and password (like `admin` and `password`) printed on a sticker or easily found
online. Hackers know these.

- **What to do:** Log into your router’s settings page and change the **admin password** (this is different from your Wi-Fi
  password—it’s the password used to change router settings). Make it long and unique.

## 2. Upgrade to Strong Wi-Fi Encryption (WPA3 or WPA2)

The encryption protocol determines how well your wireless traffic is protected.

- **What to do:** Go to your wireless security settings and select **WPA3-Personal** if your router and devices support it.
  If not, choose **WPA2-Personal (AES)**.
- **What to avoid:** Never select WEP, WPA, or "WPA2/WPA Mixed Mode" unless you absolutely have to support ancient devices,
  as these introduce older vulnerabilities.

## 3. Create a Strong Wi-Fi Password (Pre-Shared Key)

Even with strong encryption, a weak password can be cracked using dictionary attacks.

- **What to do:** Create a Wi-Fi password that is at least 12–16 characters long. Use a mix of uppercase letters, lowercase
  letters, numbers, and symbols. Avoid using your name, address, or common words.

## 4. Disable WPS (Wi-Fi Protected Setup)

As mentioned previously, the PIN-based WPS feature is highly vulnerable to brute-force attacks.

- **What to do:** Find the WPS section in your router's menu and flip the switch to **Disabled** or **Off**.

## 5. Set Up a Guest Network

A guest network isolates visitors and smart devices from your main home network.

- **What to do:** Enable a Guest Network SSID. Give your guests and **all smart home (IoT) devices** (like smart TVs,
  security cameras, and smart plugs) access _only_ to this guest network.
- **Why:** If a smart bulb gets hacked, the attacker won't be able to pivot to your main computer or phone where your private
  data lives.

## 6. Disable Remote Management

Remote management allows you to log into your router's admin panel from outside your house (over the internet).

- **What to do:** Ensure **Remote Management** (sometimes called "WAN Management" or "Web Access from WAN") is turned
  **Off**. You should only be able to change router settings when you are physically connected to your home network.

## 7. Keep Router Firmware Updated

Router manufacturers constantly release updates to patch newly discovered security flaws.

- **What to do:** Check if your router has an "Auto-Update" feature and turn it on. If it doesn't, log into your router every
  few months to check for and install firmware updates manually.

---

## Quick Checklist Summary

- [ ] Changed default admin password?
- [ ] Using WPA2-AES or WPA3?
- [ ] Wi-Fi password is long and complex?
- [ ] WPS disabled?
- [ ] Guest network active for IoT/visitors?
- [ ] Remote management turned off?
- [ ] Firmware up to date?
