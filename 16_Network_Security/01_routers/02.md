**OPSEC**, short for **Operations Security**, is a process used to protect sensitive information from falling into the wrong
hands. It originated in the military but is widely used today in cybersecurity, corporate business, and everyday privacy.

The core goal of OPSEC is to look at your own actions from an outsider’s perspective to identify clues that could
accidentally reveal critical secrets.

---

### The 5 Steps of the OPSEC Process

OPSEC is typically practiced as a continuous five-step cycle:

1. **Identify Critical Information:** Determine exactly what needs protecting. This could be intellectual property, launch
   dates, travel itineraries, passwords, or personal locations.
2. **Analyze Threats:** Identify who might want that information (e.g., competitors, hackers, or stalkers) and what
   capabilities they have to get it.
3. **Analyze Vulnerabilities:** Look for weaknesses in how you handle that information. For example, talking about a secret
   project in a public coffee shop, or posting a photo on social media that accidentally shows a computer screen in the
   background.
4. **Assess Risks:** Evaluate how likely it is that someone will exploit those vulnerabilities, and how bad the consequences
   would be if they succeeded.
5. **Apply Countermeasures:** Implement specific actions to eliminate or reduce the risks. This could be using encrypted
   messaging apps, implementing strict "need-to-know" access rules, or wiping metadata from photos before posting them
   online.

---

### Common Examples of OPSEC Failures

OPSEC is rarely about a system breaking; it is almost always about a person accidentally giving away a piece of a puzzle.

- **The Fitness Tracker Leak:** In 2018, the fitness app Strava published an interactive heat map showing where users logged
  their runs. Because military personnel wore their fitness trackers while jogging around secret, unmapped military bases in
  remote areas, the map inadvertently revealed the exact layouts and locations of those bases.
- **Social Media Clues:** A company executive posting a selfie at an airport with the caption "Off to sealing the deal!" can
  accidentally tip off competitors about an unannounced merger or acquisition.
- **Background Details:** Sharing a picture of a new office pet, but accidentally catching a whiteboard in the background
  that contains API keys, source code, or a timeline of confidential product releases.

---

### Good OPSEC Habits

Practicing good OPSEC, especially in a digital environment, often involves minimizing your "digital footprint" and being
intentional about what you share:

- **Compartmentalization:** Keeping different parts of your life separate (e.g., using different emails for different types
  of accounts, or separate devices for work and personal use).
- **Mindful Sharing:** Before posting a photo or a status update, checking the background for sensitive items, location cues,
  or identifying information.
- **Disguising Patterns:** Avoiding rigid routines that make your movements or actions entirely predictable to an outside
  observer.
