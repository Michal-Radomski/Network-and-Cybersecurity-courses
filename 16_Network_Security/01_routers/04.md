**OpenWRT** and **DD-WRT** are custom, Linux-based open-source firmwares designed to replace the stock software built into
retail Wi-Fi routers.

Flashing a router with either system transforms a standard consumer device into a powerful networking tool by unlocking
advanced features (like built-in VPNs, granular firewall rules, traffic shaping, ad-blocking, and guest network management)
that manufacturers usually restrict or hide.

---

### OpenWRT vs. DD-WRT: The Core Differences

While they serve a similar purpose, they appeal to different types of users:

#### OpenWRT (The Modular Powerhouse)

- **What it is:** OpenWRT operates much like a minimalist Linux distribution. It utilizes a lightweight base system and
  features its own package manager (`opkg`).
- **Interface:** It uses a clean, web-based graphical interface called **LuCI**, but is highly focused on command-line
  configuration for advanced tweaks.
- **Best For:** Technical users, developers, and home-lab enthusiasts who want absolute control. You install only the
  features you need, keeping the router's memory lean and fast. It is also actively updated to support the newest Wi-Fi 6,
  6E, and Wi-Fi 7 chipsets.

#### DD-WRT (The All-in-One Classic)

- **What it is:** DD-WRT is a mature, monolithic firmware. Instead of making you download add-ons, it comes pre-packaged with
  nearly every feature you could want right out of the box.
- **Interface:** It relies heavily on a comprehensive, albeit older-looking, web interface crammed with options and
  check-boxes.
- **Best For:** Users who want a plug-and-play experience to unlock advanced features (like overclocking or setting up a
  repeater bridge) entirely through a visual menu without touching a command line.

---

### What Routers Are Compatible?

Not every router can run custom firmware. Compatibility depends entirely on the internal hardware components, specifically
the **CPU/Chipset** (e.g., MediaTek, Qualcomm, Broadcom) and having enough **Flash Memory** and **RAM** (usually a minimum of
16MB Flash and 128MB RAM is required for modern versions).

Compatibility spans several distinct hardware categories:

#### 1. Native / Pre-Installed OpenWRT Routers

Several companies manufacture routers specifically designed to run OpenWRT out of the box, meaning you don't have to risk
"bricking" the device by flashing it yourself:

- **GL.iNet:** Famous for their travel and home routers (like the _Flint 2 / GL-MT6000_, _Beryl AX_, and _Slate 7_). They
  build their custom UI directly on top of OpenWRT.
- **OpenWrt One:** An official, hardware-certified router designed directly by the OpenWRT project team with robust recovery
  options.

#### 2. Popular Consumer Routers (Flashable)

Many mainstream consumer routers have excellent open-source community support. If you buy the right hardware revision, you
can manually install OpenWRT or DD-WRT onto them:

- **Linksys WRT Series:** Legendary in the custom firmware space. Models like the _Linksys WRT3200ACM_ or _MR7350_ were built
  with open-source compatibility in mind.
- **Netgear Nighthawk Series:** Older workhorses like the _Netgear R7000 (AC1900)_ and _R7800 (X4S)_ are widely supported,
  particularly by DD-WRT.
- **ASUS Routers:** Devices like the _ASUS TUF AX6000_ or _RT-AC68U_ have strong community support (though many ASUS users
  also opt for _Asuswrt-Merlin_, a different custom firmware tailored strictly to ASUS hardware).
- **TP-Link Archer Series:** Budget-friendly options like the _Archer C7_ have been staples of the custom firmware community
  for years.

> ⚠️ **Crucial Warning Before Buying:** Manufacturers frequently change the internal chipsets of a router without changing
> its marketing name (e.g., a "Version 1" router might work perfectly, but a "Version 2" might use a completely different
> chip that is incompatible). Always check the official **OpenWRT Table of Hardware** or the **DD-WRT Router Database** to
> verify the exact model number and hardware revision before attempting to install custom firmware.
