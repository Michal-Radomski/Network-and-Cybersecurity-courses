**I2P** (Invisible Internet Project) and **Tor** (The Onion Router) are both decentralized, open-source networks designed to
provide online anonymity and privacy. While they share similar goals, they were built with fundamentally different
architectures and primary use cases in mind.

Here is a breakdown of what I2P is and how it compares to Tor.

---

### What is I2P?

I2P is a self-contained, anonymous network layer. It acts as an "overlay network" (a network within the internet) that
encrypts all traffic and bounces it through a global peer-to-peer network of user-run computers.

Unlike the traditional web, I2P is designed from the ground up for **internal communication**. It is built specifically for
hosting and accessing hidden services (called "eepsites" which use `.i2p` domains), file sharing, peer-to-peer applications,
and internal email.

---

### Key Differences Between I2P and Tor

While both networks use encryption and multi-hop routing to hide your identity, they differ significantly in their design and
execution:

| Feature                 | Tor (The Onion Router)                                                                                                        | I2P (Invisible Internet Project)                                                                                                                                             |
| ----------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Primary Design Goal** | Designed to access the **regular internet** (Clearnet) anonymously, though it can host hidden services (`.onion`).            | Designed to be a self-contained **internal network** for hosting and accessing internal services (`.i2p`).                                                                   |
| **Routing Style**       | **Onion Routing:** Traffic goes down a single circuit (3 hops) to a destination, and responses come back along the same path. | **Garlic Routing:** Bundles multiple messages together. It uses "Inbound" and "Outbound" tunnels, meaning messages take entirely different paths going out than coming back. |
| **Network Structure**   | **Directory-based:** Relies on a set of trusted directory authorities to manage and distribute the list of active nodes.      | **Peer-to-Peer (P2P):** Completely decentralized. Every client node participating in the network also routes traffic for others.                                             |
| **Speed & Performance** | Generally faster; optimized for web browsing.                                                                                 | Can be slower for general browsing; highly optimized for high-bandwidth P2P sharing (like torrenting).                                                                       |
| **Outproxying**         | Excellent. Built to safely exit the network to view normal websites via "Exit Nodes."                                         | Poor. While possible, it requires specific, scarce "Outproxies" because it wasn't built for regular web browsing.                                                            |

---

### Summary: When is each used?

- **Use Tor if:** Your primary goal is to browse normal websites (like news sites, search engines, or social media) without
  your ISP or government tracking who you are or what you are looking at.
- **Use I2P if:** You are looking to host or interact with highly resilient internal applications, engage in secure
  peer-to-peer file sharing, or want a decentralized network architecture that doesn't rely on central directory servers.
