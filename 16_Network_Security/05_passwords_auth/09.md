In cybersecurity, **password entropy** is a mathematical measure of how unpredictable and difficult a password is to guess or
brute-force.

Think of entropy as a rating of a password's "randomness." It is measured in **bits**. The higher the entropy (the more
bits), the more guesses a computer must make to crack it, and the more secure your account is.

---

## How Password Entropy is Calculated

Password entropy relies on two main factors:

1. **Length ($L$):** The number of characters in the password.
2. **Pool Size ($R$):** The total number of possible characters the attacker _thinks_ you might have chosen from (e.g.,
   lowercase letters, uppercase letters, numbers, symbols).

The formula to calculate password entropy ($H$) in bits is:

$$H = L \log_2(R)$$

### Character Pool Sizes ($R$)

To calculate entropy, we assume standard pools of characters:

- **Numbers only (0-9):** $R = 10$
- **Lowercase letters (a-z):** $R = 26$
- **Lowercase + Uppercase (a-z, A-Z):** $R = 52$
- **Alphanumeric (letters + numbers):** $R = 62$
- **Alphanumeric + common symbols:** $R \approx 94$

---

## Why Length Beats Complexity (The Math)

Many websites force you to use complex characters, but mathematical entropy proves that **making a password longer is far
more effective than making it complex**.

Let's compare two passwords:

### Case A: Short and Complex

- **Password:** `P@s$w0rd` (8 characters)
- **Pool ($R$):** 94 (uses lowercase, uppercase, numbers, and symbols)
- **Calculation:** $$H = 8 \log_2(94) \approx 8 \times 6.55 = 52.4 \text{ bits}$$

### Case B: Long and Simple

- **Password:** `correcthorsebatterystaple` (25 characters)
- **Pool ($R$):** 26 (only lowercase letters)
- **Calculation:** $$H = 25 \log_2(26) \approx 25 \times 4.7 = 117.5 \text{ bits}$$

Despite using only simple lowercase letters, **Case B has more than double the entropy of Case A**. It would take a
supercomputer trillions of times longer to crack Case B than Case A.

---

## How Many Bits Do You Need?

Attackers use highly optimized hardware to guess passwords. Here is how different levels of entropy hold up against modern
brute-force attacks:

| Entropy Level      | Strength                | What it Means                                                                                      |
| ------------------ | ----------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Under 28 bits**  | Very Weak               | Can be cracked almost instantly (seconds to minutes).                                              |
| **28 to 59 bits**  | Weak / Moderate         | Vulnerable to offline cracking attacks on standard consumer GPUs (hours to days).                  |
| **60 to 79 bits**  | Strong                  | Highly secure against online attacks; would take years to crack offline without massive resources. |
| **80 to 127 bits** | Very Strong             | Virtually uncrackable with current technology (thousands to millions of years).                    |
| **128+ bits**      | Overkill / Future-Proof | Cryptographically secure. Safe even against theoretical future quantum computer attacks.           |

---

## The Catch: Human Predictability

The standard entropy formula assumes you chose your characters **completely at random**.

If you use a password like `Password123!`, the mathematical formula might calculate it as having around 52 bits of entropy
because of the character pool. However, because humans are highly predictable and hackers use "dictionaries" of common
patterns, the **actual, real-world entropy** of that password is close to zero.

This is why using a **password manager** to generate truly random strings, or using a **passphrase** of 4 or 5 completely
random words, is essential to achieving true cryptographic entropy.
