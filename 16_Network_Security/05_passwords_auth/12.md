When it comes to securing your online accounts, passwords just aren't enough anymore. That’s where Multi-Factor
Authentication (MFA) and these various acronyms come into play. They all represent different methods of proving you are who
you say you are.

Here is a breakdown of what they mean and how they work.

---

## 1. OTP (One-Time Password)

An **OTP** is the broad, umbrella term for any password that is valid for only one login session or transaction. Unlike your
regular password, which stays the same until you change it, an OTP changes every single time you need it.

- **How it works:** You try to log in, and the system generates a unique string of numbers or characters.
- **Common delivery:** Sent via SMS text message, email, or a phone call.
- **The downside:** If sent via SMS, it can be intercepted through techniques like "SIM swapping" (where a hacker tricks your
  carrier into moving your number to their phone).

---

## 2. TOTP (Time-Based One-Time Password)

**TOTP** is a specific, much more secure type of OTP. Instead of waiting for a text message, your device generates the code
locally based on the current time.

- **How it works:** When you set it up, the website and your authenticator app (like Google Authenticator or cryptographic
  keys) share a secret digital "key." Both the website and your app use a mathematical formula that combines this secret key
  with the **current time** (usually changing every 30 seconds) to generate a matching 6-digit code.
- **Why it’s great:** It doesn't require an internet connection or cellular signal to generate the code on your phone, making
  it immune to SIM-swapping hacks.

---

## 3. HOTP (HMAC-Based One-Time Password)

**HOTP** is the predecessor to TOTP. Instead of using time as the moving factor to change the code, it uses a **counter**
(the number of times a code has been requested).

- **How it works:** Every time you press the button on your authenticator device or app, the counter increases by 1, and a
  new code is generated based on that number. The server keeps track of what number the counter should be on.
- **The downside:** If you accidentally press the button on your hardware token a bunch of times while it's in your pocket,
  the counter on your device will get out of sync with the server, and you might get locked out until it resynchronizes.

---

## 4. U2F (Universal 2nd Factor)

**U2F** is an open standard initially developed by Google and Yubico (now managed by the FIDO Alliance). It moves away from
6-digit codes entirely and uses physical hardware keys (like a USB or NFC keychain fob).

- **How it works:** When logging in, you plug the physical key into your computer or tap it against your phone. The website
  sends a challenge to the key, and the key cryptographically signs it to prove it's physically present.
- **Why it’s the gold standard:** It is virtually un-phishable. Even if a hacker creates a perfect replica of a banking
  website and tricks you into typing your password, the physical U2F key will recognize that the website domain is wrong and
  refuse to sign the login attempt.

---

### Quick Comparison Table

| Method   | What triggers the change?        | Delivery Channel       | Security Level                        |
| -------- | -------------------------------- | ---------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| **OTP**  | A new login attempt              | SMS, Email, Voice      | Moderate (Vulnerable to interception) |
| **TOTP** | Time passing (e.g., 30s)         | Authenticator App      | High                                  |
| **HOTP** | Clicking a button (Counter)      | Hardware token or App  | High                                  |
| **U2F**  | Physical cryptographic handshake | USB / NFC Security Key | Highest (Phishing-proof)              |
