Creating a secure password is one of the easiest ways to protect your accounts from being hacked. The goal is to make your
password easy for you to remember, but incredibly difficult for a computer to guess.

Here is a guide on what a strong password looks like, what you should do, and what you must avoid.

---

## 1. What a Good Password Looks Like (The "Passphrase" Method)

Forget trying to remember complex, ugly passwords like `P@$$w0rd123!`. Hackers have rule-based attacks that guess these
easily.

Instead, the modern standard is to use a **passphrase**—a string of random, unrelated words joined together.

> **Example of a strong password:** `correct-horse-battery-staple` **Why it works:** It is incredibly long (making it
> virtually impossible for computers to brute-force) but paints a picture in your mind that is easy to remember.

---

## 2. What to Do (Best Practices)

- **Prioritize Length over Complexity:** A password that is 16 characters long made of simple words is mathematically much
  harder to crack than an 8-character password with symbols. Aim for at least **12 to 16 characters**.
- **Use a Password Manager:** It is impossible to remember dozens of strong, unique passwords. Use an open-source tool like
  **KeePassXC** or a cloud manager like **Bitwarden** to generate and store them for you.
- **Make Every Password Unique:** If a hacker breaches a random website you signed up for and gets your password, the first
  thing they will do is try that same password on your email, bank, and social media accounts.
- **Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA):** Always turn on 2FA/MFA (like authenticator apps or security keys) on your
  important accounts. This acts as a second lock even if someone guesses your password.

---

## 3. What to Avoid (The Weak Spots)

- **Avoid Personal Details:** Never use your name, your pet's name, your birthday, your school, or your favorite sports team.
  This information is easily found on social media.
- **Avoid Dictionary Words on Their Own:** Single words like `password`, `shadow`, or `football` are cracked instantly by
  dictionary attacks.
- **Avoid Obvious Substitutions:** Hackers know all the tricks. Replacing `a` with `@`, `o` with `0`, or `s` with `$` (like
  `P@$$w0rd`) does not stop cracking software.
- **Avoid Sequential Patterns:** Avoid keyboard paths (like `qwerty` or `asdfgh`) and consecutive numbers (like `123456` or
  `2026`).
- **Never Write Them Down on Sticky Notes:** Do not leave your passwords written down on paper near your computer or stored
  in an unencrypted plain text file on your desktop.

---

## The Ultimate Rule of Thumb

> **If you can easily remember all of your passwords, they are probably not secure enough.** Let a password manager do the
> heavy lifting, and focus on remembering just one incredibly strong master password to unlock it.
