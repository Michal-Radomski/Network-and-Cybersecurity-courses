**Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)** is a security measure that requires you to provide two or more verification factors to
gain access to an account. Instead of just asking for a password (something you _know_), it also requires something you
_have_ (like a phone or security key) or something you _are_ (like a fingerprint).

When it comes to the "something you have" category, security systems generally use two types of tokens to verify your
identity: **Soft Tokens** and **Hard Tokens**.

---

## 1. Soft Tokens (Software-Based)

Soft tokens are digital security codes generated by software, usually installed on a device you already own, like a
smartphone, tablet, or computer.

- **How they work:** You open an authenticator app (like Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator, or Duo). The app
  constantly generates a unique, time-sensitive **6-digit code** (called a TOTP, or Time-based One-Time Password) that
  changes every 30 seconds. Alternatively, it might send a **push notification** to your phone asking you to tap "Approve."
- **Pros:** \* Convenient because most people always have their smartphones with them.
- Free or very cheap to implement since there is no physical hardware to buy.
- Easy to instantly issue or revoke by an IT department.

- **Cons:** \* If your phone battery dies, or if you lose your phone, you can get locked out.
- Vulnerable to sophisticated "phishing" attacks (where a hacker tricks you into typing the 6-digit code into a fake website)
  or "MFA fatigue" (where a hacker bombards your phone with push notifications until you accidentally tap "Approve").

---

## 2. Hard Tokens (Hardware-Based)

Hard tokens are physical, tangible devices dedicated solely to security. They are built specifically to grant access to
accounts and networks.

- **How they work:** They come in a few forms. Older hard tokens look like small key fobs with a tiny LCD screen that
  displays a changing number sequence. Modern hard tokens (like a **YubiKey**) are USB or NFC keys. Instead of typing a code,
  you plug the key into your device or tap it against your phone, then physically touch a gold contact point on the key to
  verify your presence.
- **Pros:** \* **Incredibly Secure:** Modern hard tokens (using the FIDO2/WebAuthn standard) are virtually immune to
  phishing. Even if you are tricked by a fake website, the physical key knows it's the wrong site and refuses to log you in.
- They don't rely on cell service, internet connections, or phone batteries.
- Isolated from your computer's operating system, meaning malware on your computer cannot steal the secret key inside the
  hardware.

- **Cons:** \* They cost money to buy and replace.
- If you lose the physical device or leave it at home, you cannot log into your accounts until you use a backup method.
- They can be physically damaged or stolen.

---

## Comparison Summary

| Feature                 | Soft Tokens (Apps/Push)                 | Hard Tokens (YubiKeys/Fobs)               |
| ----------------------- | --------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| **Physical Form**       | Digital (App on your phone)             | Physical (USB key or keychain fob)        |
| **Cost**                | Usually free                            | $20 to $70+ per device                    |
| **Phishing Resistance** | Moderate (Can be tricked by fake sites) | **Highest** (Immune to standard phishing) |
| **Convenience**         | High (You always have your phone)       | Medium (Must carry an extra device)       |
| **Battery Dependent**   | Yes (Requires a charged phone)          | No (Draws power from the USB port/NFC)    |

Both methods are vastly superior to using just a password, but organizations handling highly sensitive data usually prefer
**hard tokens** for their absolute resistance to remote hacking attempts.
