Mobile phone companies (carriers/ISPs) and OS providers have a few different ways they can track your device. Because they
provide the physical network you connect to and the software your phone runs on, this tracking can happen at both the network
level and the device level.

Here is how it breaks down on an Android device:

### 1. Cell Tower Triangulation (Location Tracking)

To route calls and text messages to your phone, your carrier constantly needs to know which cell tower you are closest to.

- As you move, your phone automatically connects to the nearest towers (a process called "pinging").
- By calculating the distance and signal strength between your phone and three or more nearby towers, mobile companies can
  pinpoint your location within a few hundred meters—even if you have turned off GPS and location services in your Android
  settings.

### 2. The IMEI and IMSI Numbers (Device & SIM Identification)

Your phone transmits two permanent, unique identification numbers to the mobile network:

- **IMEI (International Mobile Equipment Identity):** A unique serial number built into your physical phone.
- **IMSI (International Mobile Subscriber Identity):** A unique number tied directly to your SIM card and cellular account.

Whenever your phone connects to a cellular network, it broadcasts these numbers. This allows the carrier to track exactly
which physical device is using which phone number and account at any given moment.

### 3. DNS Requests and Browsing Activity

When you use mobile data (instead of Wi-Fi), your web traffic routes through your mobile carrier's servers. By default, your
phone uses the carrier’s **DNS (Domain Name System)** servers to look up website addresses. This means your mobile company
can see a log of the domain names of every website you look up or application you connect to, though modern encryption
(HTTPS) generally stops them from seeing the specific pages you view or things you type.

### 4. Network-Level Headers (Supercookies)

As mentioned previously, some cellular providers inject a **Unique Identifier Header (UIDH)** into unencrypted web traffic
passing through their mobile data networks. This acts as a network-level supercookie, letting the carrier and third-party
advertisers track your browsing habits across different apps and websites on your phone.

---

### How Android (Google) Differently Tracks You

While your _mobile carrier_ tracks you via network infrastructure, _Google_ (the maker of Android) tracks you primarily via
software:

- **Google Location Services:** If enabled, this tracks your precise GPS coordinates, along with nearby Wi-Fi networks and
  Bluetooth beacons, to map your movements.
- **Advertising ID:** Android creates a unique software string for your device used by apps to track your behavior and serve
  personalized ads.

---

### How to Limit This Tracking

While you cannot completely stop a mobile company from tracking your cell tower location (as long as your phone is on and
connected to a cellular network, it _must_ communicate with towers to function), you can block other forms of tracking:

- **Use a VPN:** This encrypts all internet data leaving your phone. Your mobile company will see that you are connected to a
  VPN, but they won't be able to see the websites you visit or inject tracking headers.
- **Change Your Private DNS:** Go to your Android settings (**Settings > Network & internet > Private DNS**) and set it to a
  private provider like Cloudflare (`1.1.1.1`) or Google. This stops your carrier from logging your DNS requests.
- **Reset Your Advertising ID:** Go to **Settings > Privacy > Ads** on your Android device to delete or reset your
  advertising ID, limiting how apps track your behavior.
- **Turn on Airplane Mode:** If you absolutely do not want cell towers tracking your location for a period of time, turning
  on Airplane Mode completely shuts off your phone’s cellular cellular radio.
