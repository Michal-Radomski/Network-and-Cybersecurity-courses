The **Do Not Track (DNT)** feature was a privacy preference technology introduced in the early 2010s. It was designed to let
users express their desire _not_ to be tracked by websites, analytics providers, advertising networks, and other web
services.

While it was a great idea in theory, its practical execution relied entirely on an "honor system," which ultimately led to
its obsolescence.

---

## How Do Not Track Works (Technically)

When you enabled DNT in your browser settings, the browser added a specific **HTTP header** to every single web request you
made.

An HTTP header is simply a piece of background information sent to a web server whenever you click a link or visit a page.
The DNT header looked like this:

$$\text{DNT: 1}$$

- **`DNT: 1`** meant: "I do not want to be tracked."
- **`DNT: 0`** meant: "I consent to tracking" (or the setting was explicitly turned off).
- **No header** meant the user had no stated preference.

### The Lifecycle of a DNT Request

1. **User Action:** You click on a website (e.g., `example.com`).
2. **The Request:** Your browser packages the request and slaps the `DNT: 1` label on it.
3. **The Server's Choice:** The website's server receives the request, sees the `DNT: 1` header, and... **decides whether or
   not to ignore it.**

---

## The Big Flaw: Why DNT Failed

The fatal flaw of Do Not Track was that **it was entirely voluntary**. There was never a legal or technical mechanism to
_force_ websites to respect the header.

- **Lack of Consensus:** Tech companies, advertisers, and privacy groups spent years debating what "tracking" actually meant.
  Advertisers argued that they should still be allowed to collect data for analytics, even if a user said no to targeted ads.
- **Widespread Ignoring:** Major platforms (including Google and Facebook) largely ignored the DNT signal. Because there were
  no penalties for ignoring it, most ad-driven sites simply acted as if the header wasn't there.
- **The Final Blow:** In 2019, Apple completely removed DNT from Safari. They realized that because so few sites respected
  the signal, the header was actually being used by data brokers as a "fingerprinting" tool—ironically making it _easier_ to
  track unique users based on their specific browser settings. Most other major browsers followed suit and deprecated the
  feature.

---

## What Replaced Do Not Track?

Because DNT failed, the privacy community and regulatory bodies moved toward two much stronger approaches:

### 1. Global Privacy Control (GPC)

This is the modern spiritual successor to DNT. GPC sends a signal (via both HTTP headers and JavaScript) stating that the
user wants to opt out of the sale or sharing of their personal data.

- **The Big Difference:** Unlike DNT, GPC is backed by **legal frameworks** like the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA)
  and Europe's GDPR. In regions with these laws, companies are legally required to respect the GPC signal or face massive
  fines.

### 2. Built-in Technical Blocking

Modern browsers no longer ask trackers for permission; they simply block them.

- **Tracking Protection:** Browsers like Brave, Firefox, and Safari now actively block tracking scripts, third-party cookies,
  and fingerprinting scripts by default.
- **Privacy Extensions:** Users frequently use extensions like uBlock Origin or Privacy Badger to forcefully strip out
  trackers before they can even load.
