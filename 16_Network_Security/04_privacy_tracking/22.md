## 1. What are "Bits of Identifying Information"?

In computer science and privacy testing, **bits of information** are used to measure how much a specific trait narrows down a
search.

Think of it like a game of **"Guess Who?"**. Every "bit" of information you share cuts the remaining pool of possibilities in
half:

- **1 bit** of information cuts the group in half (e.g., "Is the person male or female?").
- **2 bits** of information cuts the group in half twice (leaving $1/4$ of the original group).
- **3 bits** cuts it in half three times (leaving $1/8$ of the group).

Mathematically, if your browser fingerprint has $x$ bits of identifying information, you share that exact fingerprint with
only 1 out of every $2^x$ browsers.

---

## 2. Calculating the Math (17.32 Bits)

If we apply this formula to your score of **17.32 bits**:

$$2^{17.32} \approx 163,640$$

This means that your specific combination of browser settings, screen resolution, operating system, and installed fonts is so
unique that **only about 1 in every 163,640 browsers** will have the exact same footprint.

If a website has a database of half a million visitors, a fingerprint with 17.32 bits of information allows them to easily
narrow you down to just a tiny handful of people—or even pinpoint you uniquely.

---

## Why Is Your Score This High?

Your browser fingerprint gets more unique (and the "bits" number goes up) when you have a setup that differs from the average
internet user. Things that increase this number include:

- Using a rare or highly customized operating system/browser version.
- Having a large number of unique system fonts installed.
- Having a highly specific screen resolution or window size.
- Using a unique combination of browser extensions.

In the world of online privacy, **a lower number of bits is actually better**. It means your browser looks like everyone
else's, making it much harder for trackers to single you out in a crowd.
