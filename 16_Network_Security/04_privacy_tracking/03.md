A **supercookie** is a type of tracking technology used to identify and track web browsers across the internet.

While they share a similar goal with traditional browser cookies—remembering who you are and tracking your browsing
habits—they are vastly different in how they operate, where they are stored, and how difficult they are to get rid of.

Here is a breakdown of what makes them different and how they work.

---

### Supercookie vs. Regular Cookie

| Feature                    | Regular Cookie                                                          | Supercookie                                                                                         |
| -------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Where it's stored**      | In your browser's designated storage folder.                            | Outside the browser (e.g., in Flash storage, Silverlight, or injected at the network level).        |
| **Ease of Deletion**       | Very easy. You can clear them in your browser settings anytime.         | Very difficult. Clearing your browser history/cookies does not affect them.                         |
| **Cross-Browser Tracking** | No. A cookie placed on Chrome cannot track you if you switch to Safari. | Yes. Because they live outside the browser, they can track you across Chrome, Safari, Firefox, etc. |

---

### How Supercookies Work

Supercookies generally fall into two categories depending on how they are implemented:

#### 1. Device-Based (Flash/Zombie Cookies)

Historically, these were files stored in plugin folders outside of the browser's control, such as Adobe Flash or Microsoft
Silverlight. If a website noticed its regular cookie was deleted, it would reach into the Flash folder, grab the backup ID,
and "re-create" (or resurrect) the deleted browser cookie. This is why they are sometimes called **Zombie Cookies**.

> _Note: Since Adobe Flash was discontinued, this specific type of supercookie is rarely seen today._

#### 2. Network-Based (UIDH)

This is the most common modern form of a supercookie, and it doesn't actually store anything on your device at all. Instead,
it is injected by your **Internet Service Provider (ISP)**.

When you visit an unencrypted website, your ISP injects a unique string of characters called a **Unique Identifier Header
(UIDH)** into your internet traffic.

- Every website you request can see this ID.
- Because the ID is tied to your data plan/account, advertisers can buy this data from ISPs to build a profile of your
  browsing habits across multiple devices and browsers.

---

### Why Are They Controversial?

Supercookies are highly criticized by privacy advocates for a few major reasons:

- **No Consent:** Users usually don't know they are being tracked by a supercookie, and they don't opt-in to it.
- **Hard to Bypass:** Standard privacy tools like "Incognito Mode" or "Private Browsing" do not protect against network-level
  supercookies because your ISP can still see your data traffic.
- **Resilient:** Standard browser cleaning habits do nothing to stop them.

---

### How to Protect Yourself

Because supercookies operate outside standard browser boundaries, defending against them requires a few specific strategies:

- **Use HTTPS:** Network-level supercookies rely on reading your web traffic. If a website uses HTTPS (which encrypts your
  connection), your ISP cannot inject headers into the data packet. Today, the vast majority of the web uses HTTPS by
  default.
- **Use a VPN (Virtual Private Network):** A VPN encrypts all of the traffic leaving your device. Because your ISP cannot see
  what websites you are visiting, they cannot inject a UIDH supercookie.
- **Keep Browsers Updated:** Modern browsers have heavily clamped down on local storage exploits, making it incredibly
  difficult for websites to hide "zombie" files on your computer.
