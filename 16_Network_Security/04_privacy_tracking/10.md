The main difference between incognito browsing and normal browsing is **what gets saved on your local device** (your computer
or phone) after you close the window.

Here is a breakdown of how they compare:

---

## Normal Browsing

When you browse normally, your browser actively keeps a record of your session to make your internet experience smoother.

- **History:** It saves a list of every website you visit.
- **Cookies:** It keeps you logged into your accounts (like Google, social media, or shopping sites) and remembers items in
  your shopping carts.
- **Form Data & Cache:** It remembers things you type into search bars (like your address or username) and caches images to
  help pages load faster the next time you visit.

---

## Incognito Browsing (Private Mode)

When you open an incognito window, your browser creates a temporary session that acts like a blank slate. **Once you close
the incognito window, all data from that session is permanently deleted from your device.**

- **No Saved History:** Anyone else who uses your computer or phone won't be able to see the websites you just visited.
- **Temporary Cookies:** You will be logged out of all your accounts. If you log into a site while in incognito, you will be
  instantly logged out the moment you close the window.
- **No Search History:** Your searches won't pop up as suggestions the next time someone uses the search bar.

---

## The Big Misconception: What Incognito Does _Not_ Do

Many people believe incognito mode makes them completely invisible online. **It does not.** Incognito only hides your
activity from _other people who use your physical device_.

Even when you are using incognito mode, your browsing activity is still visible to:

- **Your Internet Service Provider (ISP):** The company you pay for internet can still see every website domain you visit.
- **Your School or Employer:** If you are on a school or work Wi-Fi network, or using a managed device, the network
  administrators can see your traffic.
- **The Websites You Visit:** The websites themselves can still see your IP address and track your activity while you are on
  their page. If you log into your Google or Amazon account while in incognito, that company instantly knows exactly who you
  are and connects your activity to your profile.

> **Summary:** Use **normal browsing** for your everyday use when you want convenience, password saving, and quick logins.
> Use **incognito** when you are sharing a device and want to hide your history from family or friends, or when you want to
> look up a flight or gift without targeted ads chasing you around later.

---

---

Because incognito mode only protects your privacy from other people who physically use your device, you might be wondering
how to get _actual_ privacy from the rest of the internet.

If you want to keep your browsing private from your Internet Service Provider (ISP), network administrators, and online
trackers, you have to look beyond your browser settings. Here are the most effective tools for real privacy:

---

## 1. Use a Virtual Private Network (VPN)

A VPN creates an encrypted tunnel between your device and the internet.

- **What it hides:** It hides your true IP address and location. It also encrypts your traffic so your ISP, school, or a
  hacker on public Wi-Fi cannot see which websites you are visiting.
- **What it doesn't do:** It doesn't stop websites from tracking you if you log into your personal accounts (like Google or
  Instagram) while the VPN is turned on.

## 2. Switch to a Privacy Browser

Mainstream browsers like Chrome and Edge are built by advertising companies. To prevent tracking:

- **Brave or Firefox:** These browsers actively block third-party trackers, fingerprinters, and intrusive ads by default.
- **Tor Browser:** If you want maximum anonymity, Tor bounces your traffic through three different encrypted servers around
  the world, making it incredibly difficult for anyone to trace your online activity back to you.

## 3. Swap Your Search Engine

As mentioned earlier, switching from Google or Bing to an alternative like **DuckDuckGo**, **Brave Search**, or **Startpage**
ensures that your search history isn't being saved, cataloged, and tied to your real identity.

## 4. Tweak Your Device Settings

Your phone and computer have built-in privacy settings that are usually turned off by default:

- Go to your phone's privacy settings and turn off **Location Services** for apps that don't need it.
- Turn on **"Limit Ad Tracking"** or **"Delete Advertising ID"** in your phone's Google or Apple account settings to stop
  apps from sharing your behavior with data brokers.
