When you cruise around the internet, you aren't exactly traveling invisible. **Internet tracking** is the practice of
websites, advertisers, and tech companies collecting data about your online activities.

Think of it like walking through a mall where every store secretly takes notes on what you look at, how long you stand in
front of a display, and what you buy—except online, it happens instantly and at a massive scale.

---

## How Are You Being Tracked?

Companies use a mix of clever technologies to build a profile of who you are and what you like. Here are the main culprits:

### 1. Cookies (The Classic Way)

Cookies are tiny text files that websites drop into your browser when you visit them.

- **First-party cookies:** These are helpful. They remember your login info or what you left in your shopping cart so you
  don't have to re-enter it.
- **Third-party cookies:** These are placed by advertisers and trackers embedded in the site. They follow you from website to
  website, which is why that pair of shoes you looked at once suddenly haunts your social media feed for two weeks.

### 2. Browser Fingerprinting (The Sneaky Way)

Even if you clear your cookies, companies can still identify you. Websites can read specific details about your device to
create a unique "fingerprint." This includes:

- Your screen resolution
- Your operating system (e.g., Windows, iOS, Android)
- The exact version of your browser
- Installed fonts and your time zone

Because the combination of these traits is incredibly unique, trackers can recognize your device with up to 99% accuracy.

### 3. Tracking Pixels and Web Beacons

These are tiny, invisible 1x1-pixel images hidden in websites and emails. When you open a webpage or an email, that tiny
image loads from a server, alerting the sender that you opened it, what time you did it, and what device you used.

### 4. IP Addresses and Geolocation

Your **IP (Internet Protocol) address** is like your digital home address. Every time you connect to a website, it sees your
IP, which reveals your general physical location (like your city or neighborhood) and your internet service provider.

### 5. Account Logins

The easiest way to track you is when you willingly log in. If you are logged into a Google, Apple, Meta, or TikTok account,
those companies can track your activity across millions of other apps and websites that use their login buttons, "like"
plugins, or ad networks.

---

## Why Do They Track You?

- **Targeted Advertising:** The biggest driver of internet tracking is money. Detailed profiles mean advertisers can serve
  you ads you are highly likely to click on.
- **Analytics and Improvement:** Website owners want to know which pages are popular and where users get confused or leave.
- **Personalization:** Ensuring your weather app shows your local forecast and your streaming app suggests shows you actually
  want to watch.

---

## How to Limit Tracking

While it is almost impossible to be 100% invisible online, you can significantly claw back your privacy with a few habits:

- **Use a Privacy-Focused Browser:** Browsers like Brave, Firefox, or Safari have built-in protections against third-party
  cookies and fingerprinting.
- **Install an Ad Blocker:** Extensions like _uBlock Origin_ block tracking scripts before they can even load.
- **Use a VPN (Virtual Private Network):** A VPN hides your real IP address and encrypts your traffic, making it look like
  you are browsing from a different location.
- **Opt-Out:** When websites show you those annoying "Cookie Consent" banners, take five seconds to click "Reject All" or
  "Manage Preferences" instead of "Accept All."
