**Browser fingerprinting** is a highly accurate method that websites use to identify and track individual visitors. Unlike
traditional tracking methods (like cookies), which rely on storing a file on your device, browser fingerprinting gathers
information about your browser’s configuration and hardware to create a unique profile—a **"fingerprint"**—that is incredibly
difficult to delete or block.

Even if you clear your browsing history, delete your cookies, or use "Incognito" mode, a site using fingerprinting can often
still recognize you when you return.

---

## How It Works

When you visit a website, your browser has to share certain technical specifications with the host server so the website
loads and displays correctly.

Websites run background scripts (usually written in JavaScript) that request dozens of these small, seemingly mundane
details. Individually, these details are common. But when they are combined, they create a highly specific data set that is
statistically unique to you.

Commonly collected data points include:

- **User-Agent String:** The name and version of your browser and operating system.
- **Screen Resolution:** Your screen size, color depth, and display settings.
- **Installed Fonts:** The exact list of fonts available on your operating system.
- **Time Zone and Language:** Your local time zone and preferred language settings.
- **Active Extensions:** The browser extensions or plugins you have enabled.
- **Hardware Specifications:** Your CPU class, device memory, and graphics card details.

These parameters are processed through a mathematical function to generate a short, unique string of characters called a
**hash** (e.g., `4a8f9c0e...`). This hash serves as your digital fingerprint.

---

## Advanced Fingerprinting Techniques

Trackers do not just look at your settings; they also test how your computer _performs tasks_ to highlight subtle hardware
differences:

- **Canvas Fingerprinting:** The website instructs your browser to draw a hidden 2D image or piece of text in the background.
  Because of microscopic differences in your graphics card, drivers, and operating system, every computer renders this image
  slightly differently at the pixel level. The resulting image is hashed into a unique ID.
- **WebGL Fingerprinting:** Similar to canvas fingerprinting, this forces your browser to render 3D images, exposing specific
  details about your physical graphics hardware (GPU).
- **Audio Fingerprinting:** The site plays a silent audio signal through your browser's audio API. The way your device's
  audio stack processes and compresses that sound wave creates a unique mathematical signature.

## Why Is It Used?

While browser fingerprinting is controversial due to privacy concerns, it has both tracking and security applications:

| Use Case                | How It Works                                                                                                                                                                                                                          |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Cross-Site Tracking** | Advertisers track your fingerprint across different websites to build a profile of your interests and serve targeted ads without needing cookies.                                                                                     |
| **Fraud Prevention**    | Banks and e-commerce sites use fingerprinting to detect suspicious activity. If someone tries to log into your account using your credentials, but their browser fingerprint differs wildly from your usual one, the system flags it. |
| **Bot Detection**       | Automated bots often use standard browser profiles. Fingerprinting can distinguish a real human user using a unique device from a automated script.                                                                                   |

---

## How to Protect Yourself

Because fingerprinting relies on standard web technologies required to run modern websites, completely blocking it can often
"break" pages. However, you can significantly reduce your visibility:

- **Use Privacy-Focused Browsers:** Browsers like **Brave**, **Firefox** (with enhanced tracking protection enabled), and
  **Safari** have built-in protections that randomize or spoof fingerprinting data.
- **Use the Tor Browser:** The Tor Browser takes the ultimate anti-fingerprinting approach: it makes everyone's browser setup
  look _exactly the same_. Since everyone's fingerprint is identical, trackers cannot isolate individuals.
- **Avoid Over-Customizing:** Ironically, installing too many privacy extensions, rare fonts, or custom configurations can
  make your browser look more unique to fingerprinting algorithms. Keeping a standard, common setup is often safer.
