Performing a vulnerability scan on your home router is a great way to secure your network against unauthorized access. You
can use OpenVAS (or Greenbone, which you just set up) to do this.

Before you start, make sure you are scanning **only your own equipment**. Scanning networks or devices you do not own or have
explicit permission to test can violate terms of service or local laws.

Here is a step-by-step guide to scanning your home router using the Greenbone Security Assistant (GSA) web interface:

---

## Step 1: Find Your Router's IP Address

Most home routers act as the "Default Gateway" for your local network. You need this specific IP address to target it.

- **On Windows:** Open Command Prompt and type `ipconfig`. Look for the **Default Gateway** address (usually something like
  `192.168.1.1` or `192.168.0.1`).
- **On Linux/Mac:** Open the terminal and type `ip route show` or `route -n`. The IP address next to `default` is your
  router.

---

## Step 2: Create a Target in OpenVAS

1. Log into your OpenVAS web UI (`https://localhost:9392`).
2. In the top menu, navigate to **Configuration** > **Targets**.
3. Click the **New Target** icon (a star icon in the top left corner of the page).
4. Fill out the target details:

- **Name:** "Home Router"
- **Hosts:** Enter your router's IP address (e.g., `192.168.1.1`).
- Leave the other settings as default for a standard scan.

5. Click **Save**.

---

## Step 3: Create and Run the Scan Task

1. In the top menu, navigate to **Scans** > **Tasks**.
2. Hover over the star icon in the top left and click **New Task**.
3. Configure the task:

- **Name:** "Router Vulnerability Scan"
- **Scan Targets:** Select the "Home Router" target you created in Step 2.
- **Scan Config:** Choose **Full and fast**. This is the standard recommended configuration; it balances speed with
  thoroughness without crashing older hardware.

4. Click **Save**.
5. You will be taken back to the Tasks page. Find your new task and click the green **Start** (play button) icon under the
   "Actions" column.

---

## Step 4: Analyze the Results

The scan will take anywhere from 5 to 20 minutes depending on how many ports your router has open.

1. Once the status shows **Done**, click on the task name to view the summary.
2. Click on the **Results** tab to view a detailed breakdown of what was found.
3. OpenVAS categorizes findings by severity:

- 🔴 **High:** Critical security flaws (e.g., outdated firmware with known exploits or default passwords).
- 🟡 **Medium:** Minor misconfigurations or potential entry points.
- 🔵 **Low / Log:** Information about open ports or software versions that don't pose an immediate threat.

---

## Common Router Vulnerabilities & How to Fix Them

If your scan flags any issues, they are usually related to these three common areas:

- **Open Management Ports:** If ports like `22` (SSH), `23` (Telnet), or `80/443` (HTTP/HTTPS) are accessible from the **WAN
  (Internet)** side, turn off "Remote Management" in your router settings immediately.
- **Default Credentials:** If OpenVAS flags a default password, log into your router's admin panel and change the password to
  a strong, unique one.
- **Outdated Firmware:** If the scanner finds vulnerabilities in the router's operating system, check your router
  manufacturer's website (e.g., Netgear, ASUS, TP-Link) to download and apply the latest firmware update.

Performing a vulnerability scan on your home router is a great way to secure your network and protect your personal devices.
Think of it as checking the locks on your digital front door.

Here is a step-by-step guide to doing it safely and effectively.

---

## Step 1: Use Built-In Router Tools

Many modern routers come with basic, built-in security scanners right in their administration panels.

1. **Log in to your router:** Open a web browser and type your router's IP address (usually `192.168.1.1` or `192.168.0.1`)
   into the address bar.
2. **Enter your credentials:** Log in using your admin username and password.
3. **Look for Security/Maintenance:** Check the menus for terms like "Security Scan," "Device Health," or "Access Control."
4. **Run the check:** If available, run the built-in analyzer to see if any obvious risks or outdated firmware are detected.

---

## Step 2: Use a Trusted Network Scanner

To get a deeper look at what ports are open and what devices might be vulnerable, you can use a reputable network scanning
tool from your computer or smartphone.

### Option A: Smartphone Apps (Easiest)

Apps like **Fing** (available on iOS and Android) are great for beginners.

- Connect your phone to your home Wi-Fi.
- Run a network scan.
- The app will list every device connected to your network and flag potential security vulnerabilities, like open ports or
  weak passwords.

### Option B: Desktop Software (Advanced)

If you want a more detailed technical breakdown, you can use **Angry IP Scanner** or **Nmap** (via Zenmap, its graphical
interface) on a computer.

- These tools scan your router's IP address to see which "ports" (digital communication channels) are open.
- _Note: Only scan your own home network. Scanning networks you don't own is unethical and often illegal._

---

## Step 3: Use an External "Port Scanner"

A vulnerability scan from _inside_ your network shows what a hacker could do if they already got past your Wi-Fi password. An
_external_ scan shows what a hacker sees from the outside internet.

1. Go to a trusted, free testing site like **ShieldsUP!** (by Gibson Research Corporation).
2. Run their **Common Ports** or **All Service Ports** test.
3. The tool will check if your router is successfully hiding your computer from the public internet (a state called
   "Stealth").

---

## Step 4: The Manual Checklist (What to actually look for)

Automated scanners are great, but a manual check is often the most effective vulnerability scan. Make sure your router passes
these three critical tests:

| Security Feature                   | What to Check                                                                                                                                                                  |
| ---------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Admin Password**                 | Ensure you have changed the default factory password (like "admin" or "password") to a strong, unique one.                                                                     |
| **Wi-Fi Encryption**               | Your Wi-Fi security protocol should be set to **WPA2** or **WPA3**. Avoid WEP or WPA, as they are easily cracked.                                                              |
| **Firmware Updates**               | Check the router's settings page to see if a firmware update is available. Outdated software is the #1 vulnerability for routers.                                              |
| **UPnP (Universal Plug and Play)** | Consider disabling UPnP in your router settings. While convenient for gaming, it allows devices to open ports automatically without your permission, creating a security risk. |
