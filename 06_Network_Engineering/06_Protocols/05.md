To understand the Diffie-Hellman (DH) exchange, it helps to imagine two people trying to agree on a secret color without an
eavesdropper being able to figure it out, even though the eavesdropper sees every message sent.

In TLS, this is the "magic" that allows a client and server to create a shared encryption key over an unsecure connection.

### 1. The "Color" Analogy

Imagine Alice and Bob want a secret color.

1. **Public Agreement:** They openly agree on a "common paint" (e.g., Yellow). An eavesdropper knows this.
2. **Private Selection:** Alice and Bob each choose a **secret** color (Alice chooses Red, Bob chooses Blue). They never tell
   anyone these colors.
3. **The Mix:** Alice mixes her Red with the Yellow to get Orange. Bob mixes his Blue with the Yellow to get Green.
4. **The Exchange:** Alice sends Bob her Orange paint; Bob sends Alice his Green paint. The eavesdropper sees Orange and
   Green but doesn't know the original secret colors used to make them.
5. **The Final Secret:** Alice adds her secret Red to Bob's Green. Bob adds his secret Blue to Alice's Orange.

**Both now have the exact same "Secret Brown" mixture.** The eavesdropper is stuck because they can't "un-mix" the public
colors to find the private ones.

### 2. The Mathematics (Discrete Logarithms)

In the digital world, we don't use paint; we use large prime numbers and modular arithmetic. The security relies on the fact
that while it's easy to calculate a power, it's computationally "hard" to do the reverse (finding the exponent).

The process follows these steps:

1. **Public Parameters:** Both parties agree on a large prime number $p$ and a base $g$ (called the generator).
2. **Private Keys:** \* Alice picks a secret integer **$a$**.

- Bob picks a secret integer **$b$**.

3. **Public Keys:** \* Alice calculates $A = g^a \mod p$ and sends $A$ to Bob.

- Bob calculates $B = g^b \mod p$ and sends $B$ to Alice.

4. **The Shared Secret ($s$):**

- Alice calculates $s = B^a \mod p$
- Bob calculates $s = A^b \mod p$

Mathematically, both calculations result in the same value because:

$$(g^b)^a \mod p = (g^a)^b \mod p = g^{ab} \mod p$$

---

### 3. Why this matters for TLS

This specific method provides **Perfect Forward Secrecy (PFS)**.

In older versions of SSL, the "master key" was often encrypted with the server's static private key. If an attacker stole
that private key a year later, they could decrypt _every_ past conversation they had recorded.

With Diffie-Hellman (specifically **ECDHE**, the Elliptic Curve version), the keys $a$ and $b$ are **ephemeral**—they are
generated fresh for every single session and thrown away immediately after. Even if the server is compromised later, there is
no "master key" sitting on a hard drive to be stolen, because the keys only existed in RAM for a fraction of a second.
