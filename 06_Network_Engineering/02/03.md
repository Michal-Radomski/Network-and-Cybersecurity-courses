An **IP Packet** is the basic unit of data that travels across a network. If the internet is a global highway system, the IP
packet is the standardized shipping crate used to move goods from one city to another.

Every packet consists of two main parts: the **Header** (the shipping label) and the **Payload** (the actual contents).

---

## 1. The Header (Control Information)

The header contains all the metadata a router needs to deliver the packet. It usually takes up about 20 to 60 bytes of the
total packet size.

- **Source IP Address:** Where the packet is coming from.
- **Destination IP Address:** Where the packet is going.
- **Protocol:** Tells the receiver which protocol is inside the payload (e.g., TCP or UDP).
- **TTL (Time to Live):** A "hop count" that prevents packets from circling the network forever. Each time a router handles
  the packet, the TTL drops by 1. If it hits 0, the packet is discarded.
- **Header Checksum:** A small math calculation used to verify that the header hasn't been corrupted during transit.

---

## 2. The Payload (The Data)

The payload is the "cargo" inside the crate. This is the actual information being transmitted, such as a piece of a
high-resolution image, a snippet of a text message, or a chunk of a website's HTML code.

The size of the payload can vary, but it is limited by the **MTU (Maximum Transmission Unit)** of the network, which is
typically **1,500 bytes** for standard Ethernet.

---

## 3. How Packets Travel

Because most files (like a 5MB photo) are much larger than 1,500 bytes, they are broken down into thousands of individual
packets.

1. **Fragmentation:** The source device chops the data into packets and numbers them.
2. **Routing:** Each packet might take a different path through the internet to reach the destination, depending on which
   route is fastest at that exact millisecond.
3. **Reassembly:** Once all packets arrive at the destination, the receiving device uses the sequence numbers in the headers
   to put them back together in the correct order.

---

## 4. IPv4 vs. IPv6 Packets

The structure of a packet depends on which version of the Internet Protocol is being used:

- **IPv4 Packets:** Use 32-bit addresses (e.g., `192.168.1.1`). The headers are variable in size and include a checksum.
- **IPv6 Packets:** Use 128-bit addresses (e.g., `2001:0db8:85a3...`). These headers are much larger but have a **fixed
  length**, which makes them faster for modern routers to process.
