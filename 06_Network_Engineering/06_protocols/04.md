The **Transport Layer Security (TLS)** protocol is the industry-standard cryptographic protocol designed to provide privacy
and data integrity between two communicating applications. It is the successor to the now-deprecated **SSL (Secure Sockets
Layer)**. When you see the padlock icon in your browser and "HTTPS" in the URL, TLS is the engine under the hood.

---

### 1. The Three Pillars of TLS

TLS ensures three things during a digital conversation:

1. **Encryption:** Hides the data being transferred from third parties.
2. **Authentication:** Ensures that the parties exchanging information are who they claim to be (usually via digital
   certificates).
3. **Integrity:** Verifies that the data has not been forged or tampered with during transit.

---

### 2. How it Works: The Handshake

Before any data is sent, the client (browser) and server perform a "handshake" to establish the rules of the conversation.

#### The TLS 1.3 Handshake (Modern Standard):

- **Client Hello:** The client sends a list of supported cipher suites (algorithms) and a random "nonce."
- **Server Hello & Certificate:** The server chooses the encryption method, sends its digital certificate (signed by a CA),
  and provides its own random nonce.
- **Key Generation:** Using a process like **Diffie-Hellman**, both sides generate a "session key" without ever sending the
  key itself over the wire.
- **Encrypted Data:** Once the keys match, all further communication is encrypted using **Symmetric Encryption** (which is
  much faster than the initial asymmetric handshake).

---

### 3. TLS vs. SSL

While people often use the terms interchangeably, SSL is technically dead.

- **SSL (1.0, 2.0, 3.0):** Developed by Netscape in the 90s. It has significant security flaws (like POODLE and BEAST
  attacks) and is no longer supported by modern browsers.
- **TLS (1.0, 1.1, 1.2, 1.3):** TLS 1.0 was released in 1999 as an upgrade to SSL 3.0. Today, **TLS 1.2** and **TLS 1.3** are
  the only versions considered secure.

---

### 4. Key Components

- **Handshake Protocol:** Negotiates security parameters.
- **Record Protocol:** Handles the actual data encapsulation and encryption.
- **Cipher Suites:** A set of algorithms used to secure the connection. A typical suite includes:
- A **Key Exchange** algorithm (e.g., ECDHE)
- An **Authentication** algorithm (e.g., RSA or ECDSA)
- A **Bulk Encryption** algorithm (e.g., AES)
- A **Message Authentication** algorithm (e.g., SHA-256)

### 5. Why TLS 1.3 is the Winner

Released in 2018, TLS 1.3 significantly improved upon TLS 1.2 by:

- **Reducing Latency:** It removed one entire "round trip" from the handshake, making connections faster.
- **Removing Weak Ciphers:** It cut out older, vulnerable encryption methods, leaving only the strongest options available.
- **Perfect Forward Secrecy:** It mandates that even if a server's private key is stolen later, past conversations cannot be
  decrypted.
