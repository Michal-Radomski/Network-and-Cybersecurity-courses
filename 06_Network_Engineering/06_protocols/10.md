## A root certificate is a special digital certificate that forms the absolute foundation of trust in the SSL/TLS security ecosystem. It is at the absolute top of what is known as the "Chain of Trust". [1, 2, 3, 4]

## What is a Root Certificate for SSL?

A root certificate is a self-signed digital certificate. This means the entity that generated the certificate used its own
private key to validate it. [1, 2, 5] In a standard SSL system, your browser needs to know it can trust a website (like
Google or your bank).

1.  The website presents an SSL Certificate.
2.  That certificate was signed and issued by an Intermediate Certificate.
3.  That intermediate certificate was signed directly by the Root Certificate. [1, 3, 5]

## If your operating system or browser trusts the top-level Root Certificate, it automatically trusts every single certificate underneath it. [3, 6]

## Who Owns the Root Certificates?

Root certificates are owned and guarded by highly audited organizations called Root Certificate Authorities (CAs). [2, 6, 7]
The industry is dominated by two main categories of owners:

- Commercial CAs: Large tech and cybersecurity companies like
  [DigiCert](https://knowledge.digicert.com/general-information/digicert-trusted-root-authority-certificates),
  [Sectigo](https://www.sectigo.com/blog/intermediate-vs-root-certificates), [GlobalSign](https://www.globalsign.com/), and
  Entrust. They sell SSL certificates to businesses.
- Open & Tech Entities: Organizations like the Internet Security Research Group (which runs the free
  [Let's Encrypt](https://letsencrypt.org/certificates/) service), and major tech infrastructure providers like
  [Amazon](https://support.apple.com/en-us/126047) or Google. [6, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12]
-

## To be useful, these owners must have their root certificates embedded into Root Trust Stores managed by Apple, [Microsoft](https://www.microsoft.com/), Google (Chrome), and Mozilla (Firefox). If Apple or Microsoft doesn't include a CA's root certificate in their operating system software updates, that root certificate is completely useless to the general public. [3, 5, 13, 14, 15]

## What to Do to Have a Root Certificate?

How you "get" a root certificate depends entirely on whether you need it for your own private network or for the public
internet.

## Scenario A: You want a Private Root Certificate (For Free / Internal Testing)

If you only need a root certificate for yourself, your company, or a private development lab, you can generate one yourself
instantly.

1.  Generate the Keys: Use a free command-line tool like OpenSSL.
2.  Issue the Certificate: Run a command to create a self-signed root certificate valid for 10–20 years.
3.  Manually Trust It: Because browsers don't know you, your website will show a "Not Secure" warning. You must manually
    install and import your root certificate into the local machine's
    [Trusted Root Certification Authorities Store](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/windows-hardware/drivers/install/trusted-root-certification-authorities-certificate-store)
    on every employee's computer or test device. [5, 8, 15, 16, 17, 18]

## Scenario B: You want a Public Root Certificate (To become a Public CA)

If you want to become an official, globally recognized Root Certificate Authority that billions of devices inherently trust
out-of-the-box, the barrier to entry is massive.

1.  Build a Data Center: You must establish a physically impregnable cryptographic facility. The private keys must live on
    disconnected, militarized [Hardware Security Modules (HSMs)](https://www.chillssl.com/ssl-glossary/root-certificate).
2.  Pass WebTrust Audits: You must spend hundreds of thousands of dollars annually to have external cybersecurity firms audit
    your business operations and identity verification processes.
3.  Apply to Root Programs: You must apply directly to the
    [Microsoft Root Certificate Program](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/security/trusted-root/participants-list), Apple,
    Mozilla, and Google. It typically takes years of intense vetting before they agree to add your root certificate into
    their operating systems. [3, 5, 13, 14]
