**ARP (Address Resolution Protocol)** is used whenever a device on a local network knows the **IP address** of another local
device but does not know its **MAC address** (physical hardware address). Because local network switches send data based on
Layer 2 MAC addresses, the source device cannot wrap its data into an Ethernet frame without discovering that destination MAC
address first.

Here is the exact step-by-step sequence of how ARP operates on a local network.

---

## The Scenario

Let's look at a concrete example:

- **Device A** (IP: `192.168.1.10`, MAC: `AA:AA:AA:AA:AA:AA`) wants to send an IP packet to **Device B** (IP: `192.168.1.20`,
  MAC: `BB:BB:BB:BB:BB:BB`).
- Both devices are connected to the same local network switch.
- Device A has never talked to Device B before.

---

## The Complete ARP Sequence

### Step 1: Checking the Local ARP Cache

Before sending anything over the wire, Device A checks its own internal memory, called the **ARP Cache** (or ARP Table). This
is a temporary list of IP-to-MAC address mappings it has already learned.

- **Result:** Device A looks for `192.168.1.20`. It finds nothing. Because it doesn't know the MAC address, the communication
  pauses, and Device A initiates an **ARP Request**.

---

### Step 2: Creating the ARP Request Frame

Device A constructs a specialized Layer 2 broadcast frame.

Because Device A doesn't know who owns the target IP, it addresses the packet to **everyone** on the local network. The frame
contains the following details:

- **Source IP:** `192.168.1.10`
- **Source MAC:** `AA:AA:AA:AA:AA:AA`
- **Target IP:** `192.168.1.20`
- **Target MAC:** `00:00:00:00:00:00` (Unknown/Blank)
- **Destination MAC (Ethernet Header):** `FF:FF:FF:FF:FF:FF` (The universal **Broadcast** address)

---

### Step 3: The Switch Broadcasts the Request

Device A sends this frame out its network cable to the local network switch.

- The switch looks at the destination MAC address (`FF:FF:FF:FF:FF:FF`).
- Recognizing it as a broadcast, the switch **floods** the frame out of every single port, except the port it arrived on.
  Every device connected to that switch receives a copy of this frame.

---

### Step 4: Devices Evaluate the Request

Every network interface card (NIC) on the network receives the broadcast frame, strips away the Ethernet header, and looks at
the ARP payload asking: _"Who has IP 192.168.1.20?"_

- **Device C (IP 192.168.1.30):** Sees the request, realizes `"192.168.1.20 is not me,"` and silently drops/discards the
  packet.
- **Device B (IP 192.168.1.20):** Sees the request and realizes, `"Hey, that’s my IP address!"`

---

### Step 5: Device B Updates its Own Cache

Before replying, Device B is smart. It looks at the incoming ARP request and says, _"Since Device A is talking to me, I will
probably need to talk back to it soon."_ \* Device B extracts Device A's mapping (`192.168.1.10` is at `AA:AA:AA:AA:AA:AA`)
and saves it into its own local **ARP Cache** to save time later.

---

### Step 6: Device B Sends the ARP Reply

Device B prepares an **ARP Reply**. Crucially, this reply is **not a broadcast**; it is a **Unicast** frame sent directly
back to Device A, because Device B already knows Device A's exact MAC address from the request.

The frame contains:

- **Source IP:** `192.168.1.20`
- **Source MAC:** `BB:BB:BB:BB:BB:BB`
- **Target IP:** `192.168.1.10`
- **Target MAC:** `AA:AA:AA:AA:AA:AA`

---

### Step 7: The Switch Delivers the Reply

Device B sends the reply frame to the switch.

- The switch inspects the destination MAC (`AA:AA:AA:AA:AA:AA`).
- Because switches maintain a MAC address table, it knows exactly which physical port Device A is plugged into. It forwards
  the frame **only** to Device A.

---

### Step 8: Device A Updates its Cache and Sends Data

Device A receives the ARP reply frame.

1. It opens the frame and reads: `192.168.1.20` is at `BB:BB:BB:BB:BB:BB`.
2. It writes this mapping into its local **ARP Cache**.
3. The original application data packet that was paused in Step 1 is unpaused. Device A wraps that data in an Ethernet frame
   using `BB:BB:BB:BB:BB:BB` as the destination MAC, and normal data transmission begins.

---

## Summary of the Conversation

To visualize it simply, the network conversation looks like this:

> **Device A (to Everyone):** _"Hey everyone! Who has the IP address 192.168.1.20? Tell me at 192.168.1.10!"_ **Device B (to
> Device A):** _"I have 192.168.1.20! My physical MAC address is BB:BB:BB:BB:BB:BB."_

Future packets sent from Device A to Device B will skip this entire sequence and go straight to Step 8 until the temporary
ARP cache entry expires (usually after a few minutes of inactivity).
