**NAT**, which stands for **Network Address Translation**, is a clever networking technique used to conserve IP addresses and
add a layer of security to private networks.

Think of it like the mailroom of a large corporate office building. The building has one main, public street address. Inside,
there are hundreds of employees with their own private desk numbers. When an employee sends a letter outside, the mailroom
stamps the building’s official public address on the envelope. When mail comes back, the mailroom figures out which desk it
belongs to and delivers it.

NAT does exactly that, but for internet data.

---

## Why Do We Need NAT?

To understand NAT, you have to understand the **IPv4 shortage**.

Every device connected to the internet needs a unique IP address. However, the traditional IPv4 system only allows for about
4.3 billion unique addresses. With billions of smartphones, laptops, smart TVs, and IoT devices online, we ran out of
addresses years ago.

NAT solved this crisis by dividing IP addresses into two types:

- **Private IP Addresses:** Used inside your home or office network (e.g., `192.168.1.X`). These addresses are free to use,
  but they cannot route data on the public internet. Millions of households use the exact same private IP addresses
  internally.
- **Public IP Addresses:** Unique addresses assigned to your router by your Internet Service Provider (ISP). These are
  visible to the entire world.

NAT acts as the bridge between these two worlds.

---

## How NAT Works (Step-by-Step)

When you sit on your couch and request a website on your laptop, a quick translation process happens under the hood:

1. **The Request:** Your laptop (Private IP: `192.168.1.50`) sends a request to open `google.com`.
2. **The Interception:** The request hits your home router. The router realizes this traffic needs to go to the public
   internet, but it can't use your private IP.
3. **The Translation:** The router strips away your laptop's private IP and replaces it with your router's single **Public
   IP** (e.g., `203.0.113.1`).
4. **The NAT Table:** The router makes a note in its internal "NAT Translation Table," remembering that it was _your_ laptop
   that asked for Google, usually tracking this by assigning a specific port number.
5. **The Return:** Google sends the website data back to your router's Public IP.
6. **The Delivery:** The router looks at its NAT table, matches the incoming data to your laptop's request, switches the
   public destination IP back to your private IP (`192.168.1.50`), and passes the data to your screen.

## The Main Types of NAT

Depending on how a network is set up, NAT can behave in a few different ways:

| NAT Type | How It Works | Common Use Case |
| -------- | ------------ | --------------- |

| **PAT (Port Address Translation)** <br>

<br>_(or NAT Overload)_ | Maps **many** private IP addresses to a **single** public IP by using different port numbers. |
Home routers and standard office networks. (This is what most people mean when they just say "NAT"). | | **Static NAT** |
Maps a single private IP to a single public IP in a permanent, **1-to-1 relationship**. | Hosting a web server inside a
private network that needs to be accessible from the outside. | | **Dynamic NAT** | Maps private IPs to a **pool** of public
IPs on a first-come, first-served basis. | Large enterprise networks that own a block of public IP addresses. |

## The Pros and Cons of NAT

Like most engineering workarounds, NAT comes with trade-offs.

### The Benefits

- **IP Conservation:** Allows thousands of devices to share a single public IP address, massively slowing down the exhaustion
  of IPv4.
- **Security:** Because your internal devices are hidden behind a single public IP, hackers on the internet cannot directly
  initiate a connection to your computer. Your router drops unsolicited incoming traffic.
- **Flexibility:** You can change your internal network setup (add devices, change internal IPs) without needing to notify
  your ISP or change your public presence.

### The Drawbacks

- **Performance Overhead:** The router has to modify the header of every single data packet passing through it, which
  requires processing power and can introduce a tiny amount of latency.
- **End-to-End Traceability:** It breaks the original blueprint of the internet where every device talks directly to every
  other device. This makes troubleshooting complex network paths harder.
- **Hosting Difficulties:** Because incoming traffic is blocked by default, hosting a gaming server or a webcam from home
  requires extra configuration like **Port Forwarding** so the router knows where to send unexpected incoming requests.

> **Fun Fact:** While the newer **IPv6** protocol offers trillions of trillions of unique addresses (theoretically
> eliminating the need for NAT altogether), NAT is so deeply ingrained in our network security habits that we will likely
> keep using variants of it for a very long time.
