TCP Meltdown (also known as the TCP-over-TCP problem) is a severe networking performance degradation that occurs when an
application's TCP traffic is encapsulated and transmitted inside another TCP connection. This architectural anti-pattern
usually happens when using a Virtual Private Network (VPN) configured to run over TCP mode (like
[OpenVPN](https://openvpn.net/as-docs/faq-tcp-meltdown.html) over TCP) or when routing data through SSH tunnels. [1, 2] Under
completely stable, zero-loss network conditions, nesting TCP over TCP works fine. However, the moment even minor packet loss
or wireless interference occurs on the physical wire, the independent timers and congestion control mechanisms of the two TCP
layers begin to fight destructively. [2, 3, 4]

## The Mechanics of the "Doom Loop"

To understand how a meltdown unfolds, look at the sequence of events triggered by a single lost packet on the physical
internet: [2]

1.  Outer TCP Responds: The tunnel protocol detects the missing packet on the wire. It halts transmission to retransmit that
    specific packet and slashes its congestion window size in half.
2.  Inner TCP Suffers: While the outer layer is occupied fixing the packet loss, your application's actual data packets are
    trapped inside the tunnel's buffers. The inner application notices a massive spike in Round-Trip Time (RTT).
3.  The Meltdown Trigger: If the outer TCP takes longer to repair the connection than the inner TCP's Retransmission Timeout
    (RTO), the inner application assumes its packet was dropped by the network. It triggers its own retransmission.
4.  Buffer Bloat and Collapse: The application layer now begins flooding the tunnel's buffer with redundant copies of data it
    has already sent. The inner layer queues these packets faster than the choked outer layer can ever forward them. [2, 5]

Consequently, the outer connection completely exhausts its buffer space, the inner connection stops receiving acknowledgments
entirely, and overall internet throughput collapses to zero. [5, 6]

## Why the Layers Conflict

TCP algorithms are explicitly built on the assumption that they are interacting directly with the underlying physical
network. Stacking them breaks this design logic in two core ways: [4]

- Noisy RTT Metrics: The outer connection hides the true state of the network from the inner application. This creates "ACK
  Compression," making it impossible for the inner app to accurately scale its bandwidth.
- Fighting Control Loops: Standard TCP implementations interpret any packet drop as a sign of network congestion and slow
  down. When both inner and outer protocols back off simultaneously, they push the effective connection latency from
  milliseconds to several seconds. [2, 4, 7]

## How to Prevent TCP Meltdown

| Strategy [1, 2, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12] | Actionable Solution                                            | Why it Works                                                                                                                            |
| ------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Primary Fix                          | Switch your VPN/Tunnel protocol to run over UDP.               | UDP is completely stateless and handles no retransmissions. It allows the inner application's TCP layer to manage reliability normally. |
| Modern Protocols                     | Deploy WireGuard or QUIC/HTTP3 architectures.                  | Modern VPNs like WireGuard natively use UDP. QUIC builds multi-stream reliability over UDP, bypassing head-of-the-line blocking.        |
| Legacy Tweak                         | Lower the Maximum Transmission Unit (MTU) size on your tunnel. | Reduces the likelihood of fragmentation over problematic connections.                                                                   |
