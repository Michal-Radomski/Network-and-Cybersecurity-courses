Imagine you are walking into a fancy restaurant. Instead of walking straight into the kitchen to tell the chef what you want,
you sit at a table and give your order to a waiter. The waiter takes your request to the kitchen, gets the food, and brings
it back to you. You never actually see or talk to the chef.

In the digital world, a **reverse proxy** is that waiter.

While a traditional (forward) proxy acts on behalf of the _client_ (to hide your identity or bypass restrictions), a
**reverse proxy acts on behalf of the server**. It sits in front of one or more web servers, intercepting requests from
clients (like web browsers) and directing them to the appropriate server.

---

## Why Use a Reverse Proxy?

Websites don’t just use reverse proxies to be fancy; they do it for several critical structural, security, and performance
reasons:

- **Load Balancing:** If a website gets millions of visitors a day, a single server will crash. A reverse proxy can
  distribute the incoming traffic evenly across a cluster of multiple servers so no single server gets overwhelmed.
- **Enhanced Security:** By placing a reverse proxy in front of your backend servers, those servers never have to expose
  their actual IP addresses to the public internet. This makes it much harder for hackers to target them directly with DDoS
  attacks or malware.
- **SSL/TLS Offloading:** Encrypting and decrypting data sent via HTTPS takes a lot of computing power. A reverse proxy can
  handle all the SSL decryption for the client, freeing up the backend servers to focus exclusively on serving content.
- **Caching:** If thousands of people are asking for the exact same homepage image, the reverse proxy can save a copy of that
  image and serve it directly to users without bothering the backend servers at all. This speeds up load times drastically.
- **Global Traffic Management (CDN):** Content Delivery Networks (CDNs) like Cloudflare use a massive network of reverse
  proxies scattered around the globe to serve cached content from the server physically closest to the user.

---

## Forward Proxy vs. Reverse Proxy

It is easy to get these two confused. The simplest way to remember the difference is to look at **who they are protecting**:

| Feature               | Forward Proxy                                      | Reverse Proxy                                   |
| --------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| **Who it represents** | The **Client** (User)                              | The **Server** (Website)                        |
| **Location**          | Sits in front of the user's private network        | Sits in front of the website’s internal servers |
| **Primary Goal**      | Anonymity, bypassing censorship, content filtering | Security, load balancing, speed, reliability    |
| **Example**           | A VPN or a school web filter                       | Cloudflare, NGINX, HAProxy                      |
