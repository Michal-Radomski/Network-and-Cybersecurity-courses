**OSINT** stands for **Open Source Intelligence**.

In plain English, it’s the practice of collecting and analyzing information from **publicly available sources** to answer a
specific question, solve a problem, or conduct an investigation.

If you’ve ever dug through someone’s public social media profile to see if they were telling the truth, or used satellite
maps to figure out where a viral photo was taken, you’ve actually done a basic form of OSINT!

---

## Where Does OSINT Information Come From?

OSINT relies strictly on information that anyone can legally access. Key sources include:

- **The Public Web:** Search engines, news articles, blogs, forums (like Reddit), and public records.
- **Social Media:** Profiles, posts, photos, check-ins, and public groups on platforms like X, Instagram, TikTok, and
  LinkedIn.
- **Government & Public Data:** Census data, business registration databases, property records, and court documents.
- **Geospatial Data:** Maps, satellite imagery (like Google Earth or Sentinel), and street-view footage.
- **Technical Data:** Domain name ownership records (WHOIS), IP address details, and website metadata.

---

## Who Uses OSINT?

OSINT is used across a huge variety of fields:

1. **Cybersecurity Professionals:** To find exposed data, vulnerable network ports, or leaked passwords before hackers do.
2. **Journalists & Fact-Checkers:** To verify news stories, debunk fake videos, and track political developments.
3. **Law Enforcement & Investigators:** To gather evidence for criminal investigations, locate missing persons, or combat
   human trafficking.
4. **Businesses:** To research competitors, analyze market trends, or conduct background checks on potential partners.

---

## Common OSINT Tools & Techniques

> **Important Note:** OSINT is entirely about **public** data. It does _not_ involve hacking into private accounts, stealing
> passwords, or using illegal wiretaps.

- **Advanced Google Search (Google Dorking):** Using special search operators (like `filetype:pdf` or `site:gov`) to filter
  out noise and find hidden public documents.
- **Reverse Image Searching:** Uploading an image to Google Images, TinEye, or Yandex to trace where else it appears online
  or find its original source.
- **Geolocation:** Analyzing background details in photos—like street signs, mountain ranges, architecture, or sun angles—to
  pinpoint exact coordinates.
- **Wayback Machine:** Checking archived versions of websites to see what used to be published before it was deleted or
  changed.
