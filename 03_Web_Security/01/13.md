A tracking cookie is a specific type of cookie designed to monitor and record your online behavior, such as the websites you
visit, the links you click, and the products you view. While regular cookies help a site remember your login or shopping
cart, tracking cookies are primarily used to build a detailed profile of your interests for marketing and analytics. [1, 2,
3, 4, 5, 6]

## How They Work

Unlike essential cookies that stay on one website, tracking cookies are often persistent, meaning they stay on your device
even after you close your browser. [7, 8]

- Unique ID: When you first visit a site, the cookie assigns you a unique identifier.
- Data Collection: As you navigate, the cookie logs your actions—like pages viewed, time spent, and search history—and ties
  them to that ID.
- Sharing: On future visits to the same site or partner sites, the browser sends this ID back to the server, allowing the
  company to "recognize" you and update your profile. [4, 5, 9, 10, 11]

## First-Party vs. Third-Party Tracking

- First-Party: Set by the website you are currently visiting. They track your behavior only on that specific site to improve
  your experience, such as showing you "recently viewed" items.
- Third-Party: Set by an external domain (like an ad network or social media platform) through embedded code or "pixels" on
  the site you are visiting. These are the most controversial because they can track you across multiple different websites,
  allowing advertisers to follow you around the web with the same ad. [1, 4, 5, 9, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16]

## Privacy and Regulation

Tracking cookies are not inherently malicious (like viruses or spyware), but they raise significant privacy concerns because
they can collect large amounts of personal data without a user's knowledge. [2, 16, 17]

- GDPR (Europe): Requires websites to get your explicit consent before setting any non-essential tracking cookies.
- CCPA (California): Focuses on your right to opt out of having your data sold or shared.
- Browser Changes: Many modern browsers like
  [Firefox](https://support.mozilla.org/en-US/kb/enhanced-tracking-protection-firefox-desktop) and
  [Safari](https://support.apple.com/en-us/102467) now block third-party tracking cookies by default to protect user privacy.
  [1, 14, 16, 18, 19, 20]
