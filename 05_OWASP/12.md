While the **OWASP Top 10** is the industry standard for high-level categories, it is often too broad for deep technical work.
If you are looking for a more granular and extensive list, you should look at the **CWE Top 25 Most Dangerous Software
Weaknesses**.

The **CWE (Common Weakness Enumeration)** list, maintained by MITRE and supported by CISA, focuses on specific technical
flaws rather than broad risk categories.

### Comparison: OWASP vs. CWE

| Feature          | OWASP Top 10                    | CWE Top 25                                          |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| **Focus**        | Management & Risk Categories    | Technical Weaknesses (Root Causes)                  |
| **Granularity**  | Broad (e.g., "Injection")       | Specific (e.g., "OS Command Injection")             |
| **Update Cycle** | Every 3–4 years                 | Annually                                            |
| **Use Case**     | Compliance, Training, Awareness | Coding Standards, Static Analysis, Vuln. Management |

### Even Longer Lists

If 25 isn't enough, there are two even more comprehensive resources:

- **The Full CWE Dictionary:** This contains over **900** distinct weaknesses. It is a hierarchical tree where "Injection" is
  a parent and things like "IMAP Injection" are specific leaves.
- **CISA Known Exploited Vulnerabilities (KEV) Catalog:** This is a live list of thousands of specific vulnerabilities that
  are _actively_ being exploited in the wild. It’s less about general weaknesses and more about specific software versions
  (e.g., a specific bug in a Cisco router).

### Pro-Tip: Mapping

Most security professionals use a **Mapping**. For example, if you find a **CWE-89** (SQL Injection), you would report it as
falling under **OWASP A03:2021 – Injection**. This allows you to give developers technical specifics while giving management
high-level risk metrics.
